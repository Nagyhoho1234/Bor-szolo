# Variety-level climate-change suitability and adaptation pathways for the 22 Hungarian wine districts (1971--2100): extending temperature indices to grape-specific projections

**Fehér Zsolt Zoltán**
University of Debrecen

---

## Abstract

Hungarian viticulture spans 22 protected wine districts (borvidék) covering 25,254 km² and 618 settlements, from the sub-Alpine fringe of Sopron to the sandy Great Plain of Csongrád. Recent work by Lakatos and Nagy (2025) quantified shifts in growing-season temperature, Winkler, Huglin, and biologically effective degree-day indices for all 22 districts under RCP4.5 and RCP8.5, confirming that Hungary is warming at roughly twice the global mean rate. However, that study did not project what these thermal shifts imply for individual grape varieties. We address this gap by coupling the FORESEE-HUN gridded climate dataset (v1.1 observed, 1971--2022; v1.0 projected, CNRM-ALADIN53, 2022--2100) to cultivar-specific suitability envelopes for 58 grape varieties --- 38 Hungarian and Central European, 14 Mediterranean, and 6 disease-resistant PIWI crosses. Each district--variety--period combination is scored through a trapezoidal Huglin gate, a soft-Winkler penalty function, and auxiliary frost and heat-day layers, with an independent validation layer drawn from the World Bank Climate Change Knowledge Portal (CCKP) CMIP6 ensemble. Under RCP8.5, mean national suitability follows an inverted-U trajectory: 0.75 (1991--2020) rising to 0.83 (2021--2040 and 2041--2060), then declining sharply to 0.59 (2061--2080) and 0.33 (2081--2100). We derive 696 variety-replacement recommendations across eight horizon combinations. Validation against real-world district plantings yields 5% strict and 20% permissive overlap, an honest reflection of the simplified envelope approach. We conclude that moderate warming benefits Hungarian viticulture through approximately 2050, but that post-2060 adaptation will require systematic introduction of Mediterranean and PIWI varieties alongside regulatory reform of the origin-protection system. The full dataset and interactive atlas are freely available (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19466301).

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Hungarian wine districts and the climate signal

Hungary's 22 wine districts (borvidék) are distributed across a pronounced continental climate gradient that runs from the cool, Atlantic-influenced western fringe --- Sopron on the Austrian border, sheltered by the Fertő (Neusiedler See) lake mesoclimate --- to the hot, semi-arid interior of the Duna--Tisza interfluve, where the Csongrád and Kunság districts sit on sandy Homokhátság soils with summer maxima regularly exceeding 35 °C. Between these extremes lie the six Balaton districts buffered by the largest Central European lake, the historically warm Pannonian red-wine belt of Villány, Szekszárd, and Tolna, and the northern Upper Hungary districts of Eger, Bükk, and Mátra, whose elevational relief creates pronounced mesoclimatic mosaics. Tokaj, the world's first legally demarcated wine region (1737) and a UNESCO World Heritage cultural landscape, occupies a distinctive position at the junction of the Bodrog and Tisza rivers, where autumn humidity has for centuries sustained the noble-rot production of aszú wines. Collectively, these 22 districts encompass roughly 61,000 hectares of vineyard, produce approximately 1--2% of global wine, and harbour an exceptional concentration of indigenous cultivars --- Furmint, Hárslevelű, Juhfark, Kéknyelű, Ezerjó, Cirfandli, and Kadarka --- that have no significant commercial home elsewhere.

Hungarian national mean temperature has risen by approximately 1.15 °C between 1907 and 2017, with an acceleration to roughly 0.057 °C per year since 2000 (OMSZ). The Carpathian Basin is a recognised European warming hotspot (Spinoni et al., 2015), and the consequences for viticulture are no longer hypothetical. The 2024 national grape harvest was the weakest in a decade, driven in part by an April frost event that exposed the frost--phenology decoupling problem: budburst now advances faster than the retreat of the last spring frost date, leaving early-budding varieties increasingly vulnerable (Kovács et al., 2016). At the other thermal extreme, the summer of 2024 delivered prolonged heat above 35 °C across the southern plain, triggering anthocyanin collapse in thin-skinned red varieties (Mori et al., 2007) and pushing must sugar levels well beyond the range that yields balanced wines.

Compounding these climatic pressures, the 2025 Flavescence dorée crisis has emerged as an immediate catalyst for sectoral re-evaluation. The insect vector *Scaphoideus titanus* was first established in Hungary in 2006; the phytoplasma itself was detected in 2013; and by 2025 the National Food Chain Safety Office (NÉBIH) had confirmed outbreaks in 21 of the 22 wine districts. The Hungarian government allocated 3.8 billion HUF in emergency aid as growers were compelled to uproot infected vines. This convergence of long-term thermal stress and acute phytosanitary emergency has created an unusually receptive policy environment for evidence-based adaptation planning.

The most comprehensive climate analysis to date for the Hungarian wine sector is that of Lakatos and Nagy (2025), published in *Frontiers in Plant Science*. Using the FORESEE bias-corrected ensemble of 14 EURO-CORDEX regional climate models under RCP4.5 and RCP8.5, they computed four bioclimatic indices --- average growing-season temperature (AGST), growing degree days (GDD/Winkler), the Huglin Index, and biologically effective degree days (BEDD) --- for all 22 districts across the period 1971--2100. Their results confirmed a counterintuitive warming gradient in which northern and eastern districts (Tokaj, Eger, Bükk, Mátra) warm faster than the traditionally warmer southern districts (Villány, Szekszárd), inverting the historical viticultural ordering of the country. They also presented a "dynamic function" of variety suitability for 21 grape varieties, mapping index thresholds to broad suitability categories. However, this suitability assessment was tied to pre-defined index-class boundaries rather than to per-variety multi-factor envelopes, and it did not generate explicit adaptation recommendations or validate its outputs against real-world planting decisions.

This leaves a critical gap in the literature. No published study projects variety-specific suitability across all 22 Hungarian wine districts with future-bin temporal resolution, incorporates multiple scoring dimensions beyond a single index threshold, extends the variety pool to include Mediterranean and PIWI candidates, or validates model outputs against what growers are actually planting.

### 1.2 From temperature indices to variety suitability

The bioclimatic indexing of viticultural regions has a well-established methodological lineage. Tonietto and Carbonneau (2004) proposed the Multicriteria Climatic Classification (MCC) system, combining the Huglin Index, a cool-night index, and a dryness index to characterise wine-growing regions worldwide. This framework has since been applied to project climate-change impacts on viticulture in Bordeaux (van Leeuwen and Darriet, 2016), the Barossa Valley (Webb et al., 2007), the western United States (Jones et al., 2005), the Douro Valley (Santos et al., 2012), and the broader Mediterranean basin (Fraga et al., 2013, 2016). A common finding across these studies is that temperature indices alone, while powerful for regional classification, are insufficient for variety-level decision-making. The same Winkler class III designation, for instance, encompasses fundamentally different suitability implications for Furmint (a cool-climate, high-acid variety adapted to the continental conditions of Tokaj) and Cabernet Sauvignon (a warm-climate variety that has historically required the thermal budget of Villány or Szekszárd to achieve phenolic maturity in Hungary).

The recognition that variety-specific physiological envelopes must complement regional indices has driven a line of research in which climatic suitability is scored against cultivar-level thresholds for heat accumulation, frost risk, and growing-season length. Jones (2006) proposed AGST-based maturity groupings for major international varieties. Van Leeuwen et al. (2013) refined this by integrating phenological observations with thermal requirements for individual cultivars. Fraga et al. (2016) applied a multi-index envelope approach to project variety suitability shifts across Europe under multiple Representative Concentration Pathways, finding that traditional cool-climate varieties would progressively lose suitable area in southern Europe while gaining it in northern regions. Hannah et al. (2013) extended this logic to a global scale, projecting that 25--73% of current wine-growing area could become climatically unsuitable by 2050, with the range depending on whether growers adopt water-management and variety-switching strategies.

A dimension that remains under-explored, particularly in the Hungarian literature, is the role of disease-resistant grape crosses --- collectively termed PIWI varieties, from the German *pilzwiderstandsfähig* --- as a climate-adaptation tool. Varieties such as Solaris, Bronner, Johanniter, Muscaris, Souvignier Gris, and Cabernet Cortis were bred primarily for fungal resistance, reducing spray-round demand by 60--80% (Töpfer et al., 2011). However, their thermal envelopes are also distinct from those of the traditional Central European varieties they are intended to complement or replace. In a warming climate where both heat stress and the northward expansion of pests such as *Scaphoideus titanus* favour reduced chemical intervention, PIWI varieties occupy a doubly advantageous niche. Yet their suitability under projected Hungarian conditions has not been systematically evaluated at district resolution.

### 1.3 Objectives

This study aims to bridge the gap between regional temperature-index projections and actionable, variety-level adaptation guidance for the Hungarian wine sector. We pursue four specific objectives:

(i) **Compute variety-specific suitability scores** for 58 grape varieties across 22 wine districts, four 20-year future bins (2021--2040, 2041--2060, 2061--2080, 2081--2100), and two emission scenarios (RCP4.5 and RCP8.5), using the FORESEE-HUN gridded climate dataset and a multi-factor scoring system that combines trapezoidal Huglin envelopes, soft-Winkler penalty functions, and frost and heat-day layers.

(ii) **Characterise the inverted-U suitability trajectory** --- the pattern in which moderate warming initially improves aggregate suitability before exceeding cultivar-specific thermal ceilings --- and identify the crossover period at which net suitability begins to decline for each district.

(iii) **Generate per-district adaptation recommendations** by ranking candidate varieties for each future horizon, and **validate** these recommendations against real-world planting data and producer decisions to provide an honest assessment of model skill.

(iv) **Publish the complete dataset and interactive atlas as open data** under a permissive licence (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19466301), including all suitability scores, variety envelopes, replacement rankings, and the reproducible analysis pipeline, to enable independent verification and extension by the Hungarian and international research communities.

We emphasise from the outset what this study does and does not provide. The suitability scoring is a simplified envelope-matching approach, not a mechanistic crop model. It does not simulate phenological timing, berry chemistry, or yield. It does not resolve sub-district mesoclimatic variation, nor does it account for soil, irrigation, or management effects. It relies on a single regional climate model member (CNRM-ALADIN53) from the FORESEE-HUN v1.0 projected dataset (Kern et al., 2024), and therefore cannot capture the full range of structural model uncertainty that a multi-model ensemble would provide. We note that newer versions of the FORESEE-HUN dataset (v1.2, v1.3) extend the observed period to approximately 2024 but do not alter the projected fields, which are drawn from the same EURO-CORDEX RCM runs with the same bias-correction methodology; our projected results are therefore unaffected by the version update. Within these acknowledged limitations, the study offers the first variety-resolved, temporally explicit, district-level suitability projection for Hungarian viticulture, accompanied by a transparent validation exercise and a freely accessible data product.
