## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Comparison with Lakatos and Nagy (2025)

Lakatos and Nagy (2025) provide the most comprehensive temperature-index analysis to date for all 22 Hungarian wine districts, computing AGST, GDD-Winkler, Huglin Index, and BEDD under both RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 using the full 14-member EURO-CORDEX ensemble within the FORESEE bias-corrected framework. Their "dynamic suitability function" maps 21 grape varieties onto decadal index thresholds across the period 2031--2100, yielding a binary class-match assessment of whether a given variety remains within its historical Winkler or Huglin class for each district and decade. Our study extends their work in five principal ways.

First, we expand the variety pool from 21 to 58, adding 14 Mediterranean cultivars (Tempranillo, Touriga Nacional, Garnacha, Mourvèdre, Assyrtiko, and others) and 6 PIWI crosses (Solaris, Bronner, Souvignier Gris, Johanniter, Muscaris, Cabernet Cortis). This expansion is consequential because the varieties most likely to replace current Hungarian flagships under end-century warming --- the Mediterranean reds and disease-resistant whites --- were absent from the Lakatos and Nagy assessment entirely. Second, we employ area-weighted polygon means derived from a regionmask weight matrix applied to the 0.1° FORESEE-HUN grid, rather than centroid sampling. For irregularly shaped districts such as Kunság (Hungary's largest, spanning the full Duna--Tisza interfluve) or the elongated Balaton-felvidéki footprint, the difference is non-trivial: centroid sampling collapses the internal thermal gradient to a single pixel, whereas polygon weighting preserves it. Third, our soft-Winkler scoring assigns a continuously declining penalty as a variety moves away from its optimal Winkler class, reaching zero suitability only at four classes of separation, whereas a binary class-match approach assigns full suitability within a class and zero outside it. The soft scoring better reflects viticultural reality, where a variety at the warm edge of its class is less well suited than one at the optimum, even though both lie within the same nominal class. Fourth, we incorporate an independent multi-model cross-check using the World Bank Climate Change Knowledge Portal (CCKP) CMIP6 ensemble (SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5), which provides qualitative confirmation that the single-RCM trajectory captured by CNRM-ALADIN53 is not an outlier relative to the broader CMIP6 distribution. Fifth, we validate model outputs against real-world planting data drawn from 22 district research dossiers, an exercise that Lakatos and Nagy (2025) did not undertake.

Where our results can be compared, the two studies show qualitative agreement. The general direction of Winkler and Huglin class shifts is consistent: districts that Lakatos and Nagy (2025) project into higher index classes by mid-century are the same districts that our suitability scoring identifies as transitioning from cool-climate to warm-climate variety profiles. Our 1991--2020 baseline index values for the four spotlight districts (Tokaji, Villányi, Csongrádi, Soproni) fall within the range reported by Lakatos and Nagy (2025), although exact numerical comparison is hampered by the fact that their per-district results are presented exclusively in figure format with no supplementary data tables --- a limitation we verified through exhaustive search of the Frontiers platform, including the Nuxt server-side rendering payload, which confirmed that no supplementary materials were deposited with the article.

The critical addition of our study is the inverted-U suitability trajectory. Lakatos and Nagy (2025) demonstrated that the climate of Hungarian wine districts is changing; we show that this change has a tipping point when evaluated at the variety level. Their index-class framework registers a monotonic shift --- districts move from lower to higher Winkler and Huglin classes as the century progresses. Our variety-specific scoring reveals that this monotonic index shift maps to a non-monotonic suitability outcome: moderate warming lifts cool-climate districts into more favourable thermal ranges for their current varieties (the ascending limb of the inverted-U), but continued warming pushes them past the thermal ceiling of those same varieties (the descending limb). This distinction has direct implications for adaptation timing. An index-only analysis implies a gradual, continuous pressure; our variety-level analysis identifies a window of opportunity followed by a squeeze, with the crossover occurring around 2060 under RCP8.5.

We acknowledge one important asymmetry in methodological robustness. Lakatos and Nagy (2025) used all 14 available RCM members and could therefore report ensemble spread, capturing structural model uncertainty across different regional climate model formulations. We used only CNRM-ALADIN53 and therefore cannot quantify this dimension of uncertainty from our primary projection. Our CCKP CMIP6 cross-check partially compensates by confirming that CNRM-ALADIN53's temperature trajectory lies within the CMIP6 ensemble range, but it cannot substitute for a true within-FORESEE ensemble analysis. Future work should incorporate all 14 members to produce suitability envelopes with uncertainty bounds.

### 4.2 The validation gap: 5% strict, 20% permissive

The validation exercise reported in Section 3.4 is this paper's most important honest disclosure. At 5% strict overlap (4 of 80 district--variety pairs where a model top-4 pick is named verbatim in the district's qualitative adaptation record) and 20% permissive overlap (including cases where growers endorse the generic "Mediterranean varieties" category without naming the specific cultivar the model selects), the convergence between our model recommendations and actual grower behaviour is low by any conventional model-skill standard. We argue that this gap is expected, informative, and not a failure of the modelling approach.

Three structural factors explain the discrepancy. First, the model explores a broad possibility space --- 58 varieties including Mediterranean cultivars (Touriga Nacional, Garnacha, Mourvèdre, Nero d'Avola) and PIWI crosses that no Hungarian grower has yet planted commercially --- while real-world adaptation is constrained by existing rootstock investment with 15--25 year amortisation cycles, Protected Designation of Origin (PDO) regulations that define legally permitted variety lists for each district, market inertia driven by consumer demand for familiar varieties, and rational risk aversion by growers who have limited capacity to absorb failed experiments. Second, the inverted-U trajectory indicates that Hungarian viticulture is still on the ascending limb through approximately 2050. Mean national suitability rises from 0.75 to 0.83 over 2021--2060, meaning that for most districts the urgency signal for variety replacement has not yet arrived. Growers optimising for the next 10--20 years are correctly prioritising incremental adaptations --- rootstock selection, canopy management, harvest timing, altitude migration within the district --- over the wholesale varietal shifts that the model recommends for 2081--2100. Third, the model's top-4 truncation obscures deeper convergence. In Villány, for example, growers are trialling Syrah, Grenache, Tempranillo, Tannat, Touriga Nacional, Marselan, Petit Verdot, Aglianico, and Sangiovese; several of these appear in the model's broader ranking but not in the top 4.

Analogous validation gaps have been documented in other wine regions. Bordeaux authorised six new grape varieties in 2021 (Touriga Nacional, Marselan, Castets, Arinarnoa, Alvarinho, and Liliorila) explicitly as a climate-adaptation measure, but restricted their use to 5% of vineyard surface and 10% of blend volume, with a 10-year trial period (INAO, 2021). This institutional caution mirrors exactly the gap we observe in Hungary: the direction of adaptation is consistent with climate projections, but the pace is deliberately slow. Morales-Castilla et al. (2020) showed that switching to better-adapted varieties could reduce projected suitability losses from 56% to 24% under a 2 °C warming scenario globally, but a survey of California growers found that only 20% had actually planted more heat- or drought-tolerant varieties (PLOS Climate, 2024). The pattern is consistent: climate-suitability models project what is climatically optimal; growers adopt what is economically and institutionally feasible; convergence between the two unfolds over decades, not years.

We therefore propose that the appropriate interpretive frame for our 5%/20% result is not "the model is wrong" but rather "the model is early." Real convergence between model projections and grower decisions will play out over the 2030--2060 window as the inverted-U begins to flatten and districts encounter the first sustained suitability declines. The value of the model lies in its directional signal --- which varieties gain and which lose, and in which districts --- not in predicting specific plantings at specific dates.

### 4.3 Limitations and uncertainty

**Single regional climate model.** Our primary projections are derived from a single member of the FORESEE-HUN ensemble (CNRM-ALADIN53), one of 14 EURO-CORDEX RCMs available in the dataset. This choice was driven by data accessibility at the time of analysis. The CCKP CMIP6 cross-check (Section 2.2) confirms that the CNRM-ALADIN53 temperature trajectory falls within the CMIP6 multi-model range, but it cannot capture the inter-RCM structural uncertainty that a full ensemble analysis would quantify. Suitability scores under ensemble members with stronger or weaker warming signals could shift the crossover point of the inverted-U by 5--15 years (estimated from the spread between RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 under our single member). Incorporating all 14 FORESEE-HUN members is a priority for future work.

**Hargreaves PET approximation.** The FORESEE-HUN projected fields provide only daily maximum and minimum temperature and precipitation. We therefore computed potential evapotranspiration using the Hargreaves-Samani equation (Hargreaves and Samani, 1985), the temperature-only fallback recommended by FAO-56 (Allen et al., 1998) when radiation, humidity, and wind speed data are unavailable. The Penman-Monteith reference evapotranspiration would be more physically complete, but the requisite vapour pressure deficit and solar radiation fields are not available in the FORESEE-HUN projections. For the drought-balance component of our suitability scoring, this introduces a systematic uncertainty of approximately 10--15% in estimated PET (Droogers and Allen, 2002), which propagates into the water-balance scoring for drought-sensitive varieties.

**Variety envelopes.** The 58 cultivar envelopes were bootstrapped from published thermal-requirement ranges (Tonietto and Carbonneau, 2004; OIV descriptors; Jones, 2006) supplemented by expert extrapolation for Hungarian endemic varieties whose thermal requirements have not been formally characterised (Furmint, Hárslevelű, Juhfark, Kéknyelű, Ezerjó, Cirfandli, Kadarka). The envelopes are not field-trial calibrated. The PIWI variety envelopes are particularly uncertain: these cultivars were bred for disease resistance at German and Swiss research stations (Freiburg, Geisenheim, Changins), and their thermal performance under Hungarian continental conditions has not been systematically documented. We also note that approximately 24 varieties named in the district research dossiers as active or proposed adaptations --- mostly Hungarian endemics and additional PIWI crosses (Generosa, Bianca, Turán, Aletta) --- lack envelope entries entirely and are therefore absent from our model. This is a known limitation that biases the model toward international varieties at the expense of Hungarian-bred alternatives.

**No phenology, no soil, no microclimate.** Our 0.1° grid (approximately 10 km resolution) cannot resolve the lake mesoclimate of Balaton, which defines four wine districts (Badacsonyi, Balaton-felvidéki, Balatonfüred-Csopaki, Balatonboglári) through thermal buffering and humidity regulation that operates at sub-grid scales. It likewise cannot capture the Móri-szél ventilating wind that is central to the Mór district's resilience, the volcanic-tuff thermal mass of the Tokaj hillside, or the elevation-driven mosaics within districts like Eger (where Nagy-Eged at 450 m is climatically distinct from the valley floor). These microclimate effects can shift suitability by the equivalent of 1--2 Huglin classes (van Leeuwen and Seguin, 2006). Soil water-holding capacity, which varies from the sand of Csongrád to the volcanic clay of Badacsony to the loess of Villány, is not modelled.

**Simplified scoring.** The multiplicative envelope match (Huglin score × Winkler score × frost penalty × heat penalty) does not capture physiological interactions. A variety may tolerate heat stress if adequate soil moisture is available, or maintain acid balance under high temperatures if night-time cooling is sufficient --- interactions that our Cool Night Index captures only partially. Process-based vine models such as VineLOGIC (Holtze, 1999), STICS-Vine (Brisson et al., 2003), or the recently developed models coupling phenological and quality modules (Fraga et al., 2016) would address these limitations but require parameterisation data --- phenological series, cultivar-specific thermal-time coefficients, root-zone soil properties --- that do not exist for most Hungarian endemic varieties or for PIWI crosses under Pannonian conditions.

### 4.4 Practical implications

**For investors.** The inverted-U trajectory defines a 20-year opportunity window. Districts that are currently "slightly too cool" for premium red-wine production --- Sopron, Zala, Pannonhalma, and Mór --- are projected to gain suitability through mid-century as warming lifts them into thermal ranges favourable for Pinot Noir, Riesling, and Grüner Veltliner. These districts also warm more slowly than the national average (Lakatos and Nagy, 2025), meaning the favourable window persists longer. Vineyard investment in these districts during the 2025--2045 window positions assets ahead of the post-2060 squeeze that will affect the currently warm southern districts. Conversely, investment in the Alföld districts (Csongrád, Kunság, Hajós-Baja) carries increasing climate risk unless paired with explicit Mediterranean-variety and irrigation strategies.

**For growers.** PIWI varieties (Souvignier Gris, Solaris, Bronner, Johanniter) deserve serious consideration as a portfolio hedge. Their broad thermal envelopes score well across all future scenarios in our model, and their fungal resistance --- reducing spray rounds by 60--80% (Töpfer et al., 2011) --- is additionally valuable in a warming climate where extended growing seasons increase disease pressure. We note that Bianca, Hungary's most widely planted disease-resistant variety (~90% of national plantings concentrated in Kunság; decision support brief, Section 3), is not in our envelope dataset and may be an even more immediate practical option for Great Plain districts. Among the traditional varieties, Sopron and Zala emerge as the most plausible cool-climate refuges for Pinot Noir, Kékfrankos, and Riesling as these varieties are progressively squeezed out of their current strongholds in Eger, Szekszárd, and the Balaton south shore.

**For policymakers.** Two converging threats demand a coordinated response. The Flavescence dorée phytoplasma, already confirmed in 21 of 22 districts with 3.8 billion HUF in emergency aid allocated in 2025, is forcing vine uprooting on a timeline measured in years. The climate suitability squeeze arrives on a timeline measured in decades. The intersection of the two creates a unique policy lever: as growers replant after mandatory uprooting of Flavescence-infected vines, they could be incentivised --- through PDO amendments and planting-grant conditions --- to trial climate-adapted varieties rather than replanting the same cultivars into a warming climate. PDO regulations may need updating to permit Mediterranean variety trials in districts whose legal variety lists currently exclude them. The Bordeaux precedent is instructive: the INAO's 2021 authorisation of six new varieties demonstrated that the PDO framework can accommodate climate adaptation without abandoning origin-protection principles, provided the changes are bounded (5% of vineyard surface, 10% of blend volume) and time-limited (10-year trial period). A similar structured-trial framework for Hungarian borvidék could accelerate adaptation without triggering the identity conflicts that an open-ended variety liberalisation would provoke.

**The atlas as an operational tool.** The per-district analysis underlying this paper --- including per-variety suitability scores for all 58 cultivars across all periods and scenarios, replacement recommendations, threats data, and the 22 long-form research dossiers --- is freely available at the Zenodo archive (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19466301) and the interactive website (GitHub: https://github.com/Nagyhoho1234/Bor-szolo). Operational planning at the district or estate level should draw on these district-specific details, not on the aggregated national numbers presented in this paper. The atlas provides downloadable A4 PDFs for each district, side-by-side district comparisons, and bilingual (Hungarian and English) content designed for direct use by Hungarian growers and policymakers.

## 5. Conclusions

Three principal findings emerge from this study.

1. **The inverted-U trajectory defines a window of opportunity followed by a squeeze.** Under RCP8.5, mean national grape-variety suitability rises from 0.75 (1991--2020 baseline) to 0.83 (2021--2040 and 2041--2060 plateau) as moderate warming lifts cool-climate districts into more favourable thermal ranges for their current varieties. This gain reverses sharply after 2060 as the Mediterranean thermal ceiling is exceeded: suitability falls to 0.59 (2061--2080) and 0.33 (2081--2100), with the sharpest declines in the Alföld districts (Csongrád, Kunság, Hajós-Baja) and the most insulated trajectories in the western refuges (Sopron, Zala, Pannonhalma). Under RCP4.5, the ascending limb is similar but the post-2060 decline is substantially moderated, underscoring the sensitivity of late-century outcomes to the emissions pathway.

2. **Variety-specific adaptation pathways diverge by district and by time horizon.** Cool-climate flagship varieties --- Furmint, Kékfrankos, Riesling, Pinot Noir --- remain stable or gain suitability through mid-century but decline thereafter. Heat-adapted Mediterranean varieties (Cabernet Sauvignon, Tempranillo, Touriga Nacional, Garnacha) and PIWI crosses (Souvignier Gris, Solaris, Bronner) gain suitability, but only post-2060 and primarily in the already-warm southern and central districts. No single variety or variety group is a universal solution across all districts and all horizons. Effective adaptation therefore requires district-specific strategies that match the right varieties to the right thermal trajectories at the right time --- the kind of spatially and temporally resolved planning that our atlas is designed to support.

3. **The validation gap is an honest signal, not a dismissal.** At 5% strict and 20% permissive overlap between model recommendations and real-world grower decisions, the model's outputs should be interpreted as a horizon-scan of climate-compatible possibilities, not as a planting prescription. The gap reflects the structural constraints under which growers operate --- rootstock amortisation, PDO regulations, market inertia, and the fact that the urgency signal has not yet arrived for most districts. Real convergence between model projections and grower decisions will unfold over the coming decades as the inverted-U flattens and the first sustained suitability declines are experienced. Comparable gaps between projected and realised adaptation have been documented in Bordeaux, California, and globally (Morales-Castilla et al., 2020; INAO, 2021).

**Data availability.** The full curated dataset (22 district polygons, annual indices, 30-year normals, variety suitability scores for 58 cultivars, 696 replacement recommendations, CCKP CMIP6 validation extracts, HungaroMet station observations, threats data, and bilingual district descriptions), the reproducible analysis pipeline, and the interactive atlas are archived at Zenodo (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19466301) and available at GitHub (https://github.com/Nagyhoho1234/Bor-szolo). The atlas website provides per-district detail pages with downloadable A4 PDFs, side-by-side district comparisons, and bilingual Hungarian--English content.
