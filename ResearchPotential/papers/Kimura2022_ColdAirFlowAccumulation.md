# Fine-Scale Mapping of Daily Minimum Temperature in a Cropland with Complex Terrains Through the Combination of a Cold Flow Accumulation Model with Inversion Strength

- **Authors:** Kensuke Kimura, Atsushi Maruyama, Kaori Sasaki, Ken Kudo, Eri Tanaka, Erina Fushimi, Hiroshi Nakagawa
- **Year:** 2022
- **Journal:** Agricultural and Forest Meteorology, 329, 109260
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.agrformet.2022.109260
- **Access:** [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0168192322004348) (paywalled)
- **Status:** ABSTRACT AND KEY FINDINGS AVAILABLE

## Abstract

The research addresses spatial temperature variability in complex terrain caused by cold air drainage. A modified conventional flow accumulation approach was integrated with inversion strength measurements representing nighttime atmospheric stability. The approach yielded superior spatiotemporal minimum temperature estimates compared to spatial interpolation alone.

## Key Findings

- Across 15 observation points in tea fields with only 73 m elevation variation, minimum temperatures differed by as much as **6.7C**
- Proposed model improved average residuals in valley zones from 1.9C to 0.03C compared to interpolation-only methods
- Clarified ridge-valley boundaries better than conventional algorithms
- Novel flow accumulation algorithm reconsiders effective cold air drainage areas
- Combined with vertical potential temperature gradients reflecting atmospheric stability

## Methodology

- Modified cold air flow accumulation algorithm
- Integration with inversion strength (vertical gradient of potential temperatures)
- Temporal changes in temperature inversion captured
- Applied to Musashino Plateau, Japan (10 x 7 km study area)
- DEM-based topographic analysis

## Relevance to Tokaj Research

Directly applicable to Manuscript 3 (cold air pooling and spring frost risk mapping). Tokaj's volcanic hills create complex topography where cold air drainage during clear, calm spring nights produces frost pockets. With 50 stations spanning different elevations, slopes, and valley positions, this cold flow accumulation + inversion strength approach can be applied to quantify the thermal belt effect and map frost probability at high resolution.
