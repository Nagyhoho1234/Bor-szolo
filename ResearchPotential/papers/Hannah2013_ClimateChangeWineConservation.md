# Climate Change, Wine, and Conservation

- **Authors:** Lee Hannah, Patrick R. Roehrdanz, Makihiko Ikegami, et al.
- **Year:** 2013
- **Journal:** Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 110(17), 6907-6912
- **DOI:** 10.1073/pnas.1210127110
- **Access:** Open access at [PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1210127110); [PDF](https://www.pnas.org/doi/pdf/10.1073/pnas.1210127110)
- **Status:** FULL TEXT AVAILABLE (open access)

## Abstract

This study projected that area suitable for viticulture decreases 25% to 73% in major wine producing regions by 2050 under the higher RCP 8.5 concentration pathway, and 19% to 62% under the lower RCP 4.5 scenario.

## Key Findings

- Projected 25-73% loss of wine-growing area by 2050 (RCP 8.5)
- 19-62% loss under RCP 4.5
- Wine production shifts into higher elevations and latitudes, creating conflicts with conservation areas
- Mediterranean climate regions most severely affected
- New wine regions may emerge at higher latitudes but with ecological costs
- Water consumption for vineyard irrigation could increase substantially

## Relevance to Tokaj Research

Provides the global context for why regional climate adaptation studies are critical. Tokaj sits in a transitional zone where warming may initially benefit suitability before eventually threatening traditional varieties. The 50-station network can ground-truth these global projections at the mesoscale.
