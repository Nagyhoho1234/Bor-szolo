# Composition and Quality of Musts and Wines of California Grapes

- **Authors:** Maynard A. Amerine, Albert J. Winkler
- **Year:** 1944
- **Journal:** Hilgardia, 15, 493-675
- **DOI:** N/A (pre-DOI era)
- **Access:** Historical publication; referenced widely
- **Status:** HISTORIC PAPER - limited availability

## Summary

This foundational paper created the original growing degree day (GDD) classification system for grape-growing regions, dividing California into five climate regions based on heat summation above 50F (10C) during the growing season (April 1 through October 31).

## Winkler Index / GDD Classification

The system is based on the hypothesis that grapevines do not grow below 50F (10C). Growing degree days are calculated as: GDD = Sum of (Tmean - 10) for days where Tmean > 10C, accumulated April-October.

### Regional Classification (Winkler Regions)

- **Region I:** < 1390 C-days (coolest; Burgundy/Champagne analogs)
- **Region II:** 1390-1670 C-days (Bordeaux analogs)
- **Region III:** 1670-1950 C-days (Rhone analogs)
- **Region IV:** 1950-2220 C-days (warm Mediterranean)
- **Region V:** > 2220 C-days (hot; table grapes, raisins)

## Key Contributions

- First systematic climate-based classification of grape-growing regions
- Established the relationship between heat accumulation and grape/wine quality
- Foundational for all subsequent viticultural climate indices
- Still widely used 80 years later

## Relevance to Tokaj Research

Winkler GDD is one of the core indices to compute at all 50 stations. Understanding how Tokaj's stations distribute across Winkler regions provides immediate context for grape variety suitability and quality potential.
