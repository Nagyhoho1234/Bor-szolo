# Climate change and the twenty-two wine districts of Hungary: a synthesis of district-level evidence reviews (1971–2100)

**Synthesis date:** 2026-04-07
**Scope:** All 22 Hungarian PDO wine districts (borvidék)
**Evidence base:** 22 district-level evidence reviews (`research/districts/*.md`; ~123,000 words; ~1,200 sources)
**Reading convention:** Each factual claim is flagged **[OBSERVED]**, **[PROJECTED]** or **[HU-AGGREGATE]**; contested findings are explicitly flagged.

---

## Abstract

Hungary hosts 22 protected wine districts (borvidék) distributed across four macro-regions (Tokaj, Upper Hungary, Transdanubia and the Great Plain) spanning a continental climate gradient from the sub-Alpine fringe of Sopron to the sandy Alföld interior of Kunság. This synthesis consolidates 22 district-level evidence reviews to produce the first integrated, Hungary-wide audit of observed and projected climate-change impacts on viticulture at PDO resolution. The central quantitative anchor is Lakatos & Nagy (2025, *Frontiers in Plant Science* 16:1481431), which applies the FORESEE 4.2 bias-corrected ensemble of 14 EURO-CORDEX regional climate models under RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 to all 22 districts using four bioclimatic indices (AGST, GDD-Winkler, Huglin Index, BEDD). Three findings stand out. First, the Hungary-wide warming gradient **inverts the traditional viticultural ordering of the country**: Csongrád, Kunság, Tokaj, Eger, Bükk and Mátra (historically "cool" or "intermediate" northern and eastern districts) warm faster than the southern Pannonian districts of Villány, Pécs and Szekszárd. Kunság shows the largest projected GDD rise (**+581.65 °C·d**), Bükk the largest BEDD rise (**+258.49 °C·d**), Csongrád an AGST rise exceeding **+2.5 °C**, and Eger the largest Huglin rise (**+566.15 °C**); Sopron at the opposite extreme shows the smallest GDD rise (**+66.16 °C·d** historical; **+475.98 °C·d** projected). Second, the observed chemical signal — sugar surplus of 55–60 g/L in cool-district whites, total-acidity loss of 1.5–2.5 g/L in Chardonnay 1980–2020, alcohol creep from ~12 % to ≥14 % vol in flagship reds — is already re-writing regional wine styles. Third, **the impact is bifurcated by starting temperature**: cool-climate districts (Sopron, Etyek-Buda, Nagy-Somló, Mór, Balaton) face a "double-edged sword" in which warming improves ripeness but erodes identity, while hot districts (Csongrád, Kunság, Hajós-Baja) face existential viability questions driven by heat-induced phenolic decoupling, extreme heatwaves, and 2–5 m Homokhátság groundwater drawdown. We identify seven research priorities headlined by the urgent need for district-level extraction of the Lakatos & Nagy supplementary tables, systematic phenology series outside Tokaj, coupled lake–vineyard mesoclimate downscaling of Balaton, and a statistical climatology of the Móri-szél.

## 1. Introduction

Hungary cultivates approximately 61,000 ha of vineyards (2023, down from 69,000 ha in 2018), organised into **22 protected wine districts (borvidék)** that are themselves grouped into six macro-regions: Tokaj, Upper Hungary (Bükk, Eger, Mátra), Upper Pannon (Mór, Etyek-Buda, Neszmély, Pannonhalma, Sopron), Balaton (Badacsony, Balaton-felvidék, Balatonfüred-Csopak, Balatonboglár, Nagy-Somló, Zala), Pannon (Pécs, Szekszárd, Tolna, Villány) and the Duna/Great Plain (Csongrád-Csanád, Hajós-Baja, Kunság). Cumulatively they produce only ~1–2 % of global wine, but contain disproportionate cultural, scientific and genetic value: Tokaj is the world's oldest demarcated wine region (1737) and a UNESCO World Heritage cultural landscape; the Pécs University vine gene-bank contains ~1,500 accessions (the world's sixth largest); Somló's Juhfark effectively exists only on 500 ha of one eroded basalt butte; and the Hungarian endemic varieties Furmint, Hárslevelű, Kéknyelű, Juhfark, Cirfandli, Ezerjó, Királyleányka and Kadarka have no significant commercial home elsewhere.

Hungarian national mean annual temperature has risen by **+1.15 °C between 1907 and 2017** and is now warming at **+0.057 °C yr⁻¹ since 2000 — nearly double the global mean** [`etyek-budai.md`; `pannonhalmi.md`; OMSZ]. The Carpathian Basin is a recognised European warming hotspot (Spinoni et al. 2015). For viticulture — an intrinsically climate-dependent land use whose economic value is tightly coupled to narrow temperature, acidity, pH, sugar and phenolic windows — this creates both direct thermal stress and a second-order disruption of the PDO-based origin-control architecture that defines wine identity at the district level.

Until recently, Hungarian climate-impact viticulture research was fragmented: isolated phenology series (Sopron, Zala), estate-level producer testimony, and Hungary-wide or European aggregates that averaged over the country's real internal gradient. The publication of Lakatos L. & Nagy R. (2025), *Assessment of historical and future changes in temperature indices for winegrape suitability in Hungarian wine regions (1971–2100)*, *Frontiers in Plant Science* 16:1481431, provided the first integrated, physics-based, bias-corrected reanalysis at district resolution for all 22 borvidék. It is the central anchor of this synthesis; each district review extends it with local terroir, variety, phenology, chemistry, pest, drought, adaptation and economic evidence.

The purpose of this synthesis is to (i) consolidate the Hungary-wide signal in the Lakatos & Nagy framework, (ii) cross-reference it against observed phenology and chemistry records, (iii) build a **variety-by-variety vulnerability taxonomy** anchored in Hungarian endemic cultivars, (iv) assemble a comparative adaptation inventory, and (v) distil the ~250 individual knowledge-gap items flagged across the 22 district reviews into a Hungary-wide research agenda.

## 2. Methods

This is a **narrative meta-synthesis** of 22 district-level evidence reviews (`research/districts/*.md`, synthesis date 2026-04-07). The district reviews followed a common 15-section template (profile; terroir; baseline climate; observed trends; phenology; chemistry; sensory; biotic stressors; abiotic extremes; indirect effects; projections; adaptation; economics; knowledge gaps; sources) and collectively cite ~1,200 peer-reviewed, agency and producer sources. No new external literature is introduced here.

**Key data sources recurring across the district reviews:**

- **Lakatos L. & Nagy R. (2025)**, *Frontiers in Plant Science* 16:1481431 — the authoritative recent Hungary-wide index reanalysis, based on FORESEE 4.2 (28 bias-corrected climate models including 14 EURO-CORDEX runs), RCP4.5 and RCP8.5, time slices 1971–2000, 2021–2050 and 2071–2100, computing AGST, GDD-Winkler, Huglin Index and BEDD across all 22 borvidék. A handful of district files (notably Tokaj and the Balaton PDOs) cite the same paper as "Kovács et al. 2025"; the underlying DOI is identical and the discrepancy reflects bibliographic confusion in the district review stage. This synthesis treats the two citations as equivalent and standardises on **Lakatos & Nagy (2025)**.
- **OMSZ** (Hungarian Meteorological Service) station and gridded products.
- **FORESEE 4.0 / 4.2** bias-adjusted climate database (ELTE).
- **EURO-CORDEX** CMIP5 regional downscaling ensemble; bias-correction evaluation by Mihaljević & Bíró (2025, *Sci. Tot. Environ.*).
- **CARPATCLIM** Carpathian-region reanalysis (referenced for historical station infilling).
- **Klímapolitikai Intézet (2023, 2024)** Hungarian climate-policy institute technical reports — source of the widely cited "**+55 to +60 g/L must-sugar surplus**" number for Transdanubian cool districts (Sopron-Zala aggregate 1961–1990 vs recent).
- **Producer testimony** — NAIK-SZBKI Tarcal, University of Pécs Wine Institute, Csopak Kódex producers, Kreinbacher/Tornai (Somló), Misik et al. Eger late-pruning trials, Frittmann et al. (Kunság), Tűzkő/Antinori (Tolna).

Findings are tagged **[OBSERVED]** where they rest on instrumental, phenological or chemical records; **[PROJECTED]** where they derive from the EURO-CORDEX/FORESEE ensemble; **[HU-AGGREGATE]** where the only peer-reviewed number available is national and has been transferred to a district by analogy. Contested findings are flagged explicitly in §10.

### 3.2 The counterintuitive north-over-south finding

The single most strategically important projection is that **northern Hungarian districts (Tokaj, Eger, Bükk, Mátra) warm faster than southern ones (Villány, Pécs, Szekszárd)**, inverting the traditional viticultural ordering of the country. This is visible on all four indices: Tokaj has the largest historical GDD rise (**+173.96 °C·d**) and the largest projected AGST rise (**+2.96 °C**); Bükk has the largest projected BEDD rise (**+258.49 °C·d**) because its cooler baseline leaves more thermodynamic head-room; Eger has the largest projected Huglin rise (**+566.15 °C**); Kunság (Great Plain, already hot) has the largest projected absolute GDD rise (**+581.65 °C·d**), reflecting its sand-surface convective amplification and continental exposure [`bukki.md`; `tokaji.md`; `kunsagi.md`]. Conversely, Pannonhalma shows the smallest projected AGST rise (**+2.3 °C**) and Sopron the smallest absolute rise on every index [`pannonhalmi.md`; `soproni.md`].

**Mechanism.** Three factors are invoked across the district reviews. (i) *Continentality*: northern and eastern Hungarian districts are further from the Atlantic moderation experienced by Sopron and the Alpine fringe, and their summer heat accumulates faster under the same radiative forcing. (ii) *Thermal head-room*: cooler baselines leave more distance to the Jones AGST class thresholds; the BEDD index in particular is cumulative and under-saturated in Bükk, so a given temperature change produces a larger BEDD change. (iii) *Lake and Danube buffering*: Sopron, Pannonhalma, Neszmély and the Balaton districts receive significant thermal buffering from Fertő, the Danube or Lake Balaton itself, compressing the local response [`soproni.md`; `balaton-felvideki.md`; `balatonfured-csopaki.md`].

**Contestation.** Lakatos & Nagy publish only aggregated Hungary-wide visualisations; per-district numerical tables are not in the main text, and several district reviews explicitly flag this as an obstacle to verification (notably `csongradi.md` knowledge gap #3 on Csongrád AGST; `bukki.md` gap #3 on BEDD; `etyek-budai.md` gap #2; `balaton-felvideki.md` gap #12). The precise ranking therefore depends on supplementary tables that should be extracted and re-published as a standalone open dataset; this is knowledge-gap priority A in §11.

## 4. Results — Phenology, yield and chemistry

### 4.1 Phenology

**[OBSERVED]** The best-published Hungarian phenology series remains Kovács, Puskás & Pozsgai (2016, Springer) for **Sopron/Zala 1986–2015 vs 1956–1985**: budbreak ~**8 days earlier**, flowering ~**7 days earlier**, veraison ~**8 days earlier**, harvest ~**11 days earlier**, with growing-season warming of ~+1.2 °C [`bukki.md`; `soproni.md`]. For Hungarian **Chardonnay and Pinot Noir** the harvest-date advance over 1981–1990 → 2011–2020 is ~**10–20 days** (consistent with the ~25-day Franciacorta Chardonnay signal and the ~0.9 g L⁻¹ TA / +0.7 % vol Champagne signal) [`etyek-budai.md`]. In **Tokaj**, the 2024 hot-July vintage began harvest **in early August — roughly a month earlier than historical norms** [`tokaji.md`]. On the Balaton south shore, Chardonnay and Merlot now routinely break bud **in the last week of March**, an event historically locked to April [`balatonboglari.md`].

**Systematic phenology series outside the Sopron-Zala pair do not exist in the published literature surveyed here for any of the other 20 districts.** Knowledge gaps for Bükk, Mátra, Nagy-Somló, Mór, Balaton-felvidék, Pannonhalma, Villány, Pécs, Szekszárd, Tolna, Hajós-Baja, Kunság and Csongrád are all flagged in the corresponding files. This is the **single largest Hungary-wide data gap** identified in §11.

**Frost-phenology decoupling.** Because budburst advances faster than the last-spring-frost date, **net spring-frost risk has not declined in proportion to warming** [`csongradi.md`; `kunsagi.md`; `hajos-bajai.md`; `bukki.md`; `etyek-budai.md`]. The **April 2024 frost event** was a Hungary-wide natural experiment that confirmed this: cold air penetrated to −2 °C or below across northern counties; Kunság, Mátra and Tokaj suffered the largest declines nationally (up to one-third less fruit on Great Plain vines); Bükk lost lower-terrace vineyards severely; Eger lost significant Bikavér volumes; Upper Pannon lost significant area; Etyek-Buda recorded 20–60 % local crop losses in frost pockets; Móri and Tokaj itself escaped the worst. The 2024 national grape harvest was the weakest in a decade (~3.75 Mt) [`tokaji.md`; `kunsagi.md`; `bukki.md`; `etyek-budai.md`; `mori.md`; `csongradi.md`].

### 4.2 Sugar, acidity, pH, alcohol

**[OBSERVED]** A consistent chemical signal emerges across every district with producer or published data:

- **Sopron-Zala cool-district Olaszrizling:** **+55 to +60 g L⁻¹ must-sugar surplus** 1961–1990 baseline → recent decades (Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023), equivalent to **+3.2–3.5 % vol potential alcohol**. Historical identity was ~11.0–11.5 % ABV crisp Olaszrizling; recent wines routinely sit at 12.5–13.5 % ABV or higher [`zalai.md`].
- **Etyek-Buda Chardonnay 1980–2020:** total acidity at harvest fell by **1.5–2.5 g L⁻¹** (from ~9–10 g L⁻¹ toward 6.5–8 g L⁻¹); analogous to the Champagne signal of ~−0.9 g L⁻¹ TA and +0.7 % vol potential alcohol since 1980 [`etyek-budai.md`].
- **Balatonfüred-Csopak Olaszrizling:** TA down ~1.0–1.5 g L⁻¹ (as tartaric); pH up ~0.1–0.15 units. The Csopak Kódex minimum TA of 5.5 g L⁻¹ has become a de facto **climate-stress indicator** — an increasing share of producer lots are failing the Kódex TA threshold in hot vintages [`balatonfured-csopaki.md`].
- **Móri Ezerjó:** wines that were historically ~11–11.5 % vol now routinely reach **12.5–13.5 % vol** [`mori.md`].
- **Villányi Franc:** the 1990s–early-2000s "velvet, blue-fruit, herbal, 13 % vol, 10–15 years ageing" reference style has partially given way to a **dense, opulent, dark-fruit, 14.5 %+ "Villány Devil's Cauldron" style**. The 2014 PDO reform of Villányi Franc explicitly tightened ripeness, oak and extraction parameters to resist this drift — one of the earliest European PDO reforms explicitly triggered by climate-induced style change [`villanyi.md`].
- **Kunság whites (Cserszegi fűszeres, Olaszrizling, Bianca):** baseline ~19–21 °Bx / 6–7 g L⁻¹ TA in the 1980s; recent hot vintages push both axes in the **same** direction, driving a structural bulk-wine cost squeeze [`kunsagi.md`].
- **Tokaji aszú:** Magyar et al. (2025, *npj Science of Food*) show compositional profiling of 1999–2019 vintages tracks annual precipitation, sunshine hours and temperature — the first long-term *chemical* confirmation that climate variability imprints on Tokaj wine composition [`tokaji.md`].

### 4.3 Phenolic and anthocyanin thermal collapse in reds

**[OBSERVED + MECHANISM]** The Mori et al. (2007, *J. Exp. Bot.*) classic finding — that berries exposed to T<sub>max</sub> ≈ 35 °C accumulate **less than half the anthocyanin** of berries at T<sub>max</sub> ≈ 25 °C, through both reduced biosynthesis and increased degradation — is now a directly observable signal in Hungarian reds. It is invoked explicitly in `hajos-bajai.md`, `csongradi.md`, `kunsagi.md`, `szekszardi.md`, `tolnai.md` and `villanyi.md`:

- **Kadarka** (thin-skinned, naturally anthocyanin-poor) shows the most marked colour instability: pale siller-style reds are now the default in Hajós-Baja and Csongrád [`hajos-bajai.md`; `csongradi.md`].
- **Cabernet Sauvignon and Merlot** retain more colour but shift toward acylated, less-reactive anthocyanin forms — visually duller wines with stable but less vibrant colour [`hajos-bajai.md`].
- **Kékfrankos** colour correlates *positively* with elevation at Eger: Nagy-Eged high-altitude blocks consistently out-perform valley sites, consistent with the ~0.6–0.65 °C / 100 m lapse rate as a *de facto* adaptation [`egri.md`].
- **Sugar/phenolic decoupling** (sugar ripeness arriving before tannin/anthocyanin ripeness) is the central climate-quality problem in all Pannonian and Great-Plain red districts [`villanyi.md`; `szekszardi.md`; `csongradi.md`; `kunsagi.md`]. It drove the late-pruning / apical-defoliation trials of Misik et al. (2025, MDPI Horticulturae) on Eger Kékfrankos, which demonstrated that late-season management can delay budburst and flowering by ~2 weeks and shift the sugar-vs-anthocyanin curve toward lower-alcohol, better-coloured wines [`egri.md`].

## 5. Results — An identity-risk taxonomy of Hungarian indigenous varieties

Hungary's PDO system is tied, more tightly than any other European wine country's except Burgundy and the Mosel, to indigenous cultivars with no significant commercial home elsewhere. Climate change therefore presents a *variety-extinction risk* that in most of western Europe is only a *style-drift risk*.

### 5.1 Style-level identity consequences

- **Tokaj** — the single most exposed flagship style in Hungary is aszú. Warmer, drier autumns make noble-rot formation increasingly unreliable; the structural pivot toward **dry Furmint and Hárslevelű** now accounts for ~**1/3 of the Tokaj harvest**, a commercial category that barely existed 25 years ago [`tokaji.md`].
- **Pannon reds** — Villányi Franc alcohol creep from ~13 % vol to ≥14.5 % vol; 2014 PDO reform is an early European regulatory response [`villanyi.md`].
- **Etyek-Buda sparkling base** — acid loss (−1.5 to −2.5 g L⁻¹ TA in Chardonnay 1980–2020) directly threatens the district's *raison d'être* as Hungary's sparkling-base heartland. Lakatos & Nagy do not flag Etyek-Buda as thermally "improving" because warming is uniquely an *unambiguous degradation signal* for this style [`etyek-budai.md`].
- **Balaton Olaszrizling** — Csopak Kódex compliance as a climate-stress indicator; style drift toward fuller-body, lower-freshness [`balatonfured-csopaki.md`; `balaton-felvideki.md`].
- **Csongrád/Kunság bulk whites and sandy reds** — the structural cost squeeze when must dilution and acidification become unavoidable [`csongradi.md`; `kunsagi.md`].

## 6. Results — Biotic and abiotic stressors

### 6.1 Invasive pest range expansions

**[OBSERVED — Hungary-wide]**

- ***Drosophila suzukii*** (spotted-wing drosophila): **first Hungarian record 2012** (Somogy county; Kiss et al.). Attacks grapes among many soft fruits; critically, **facilitates sour-rot outbreaks** by creating berry wounds exploited by *D. melanogaster* and rot microbes (Ioriatti et al. 2018). Direct threat to late-harvest and aszú workflows where berries hang into autumn [`tokaji.md`; `balaton-felvideki.md`].
- ***Halyomorpha halys*** (brown marmorated stink bug): **first Hungarian record 2013** (Budapest; Vétek et al.); confirmed **widespread across Hungary by 2017**; feeds on *Vitis vinifera*; causes "stink-bug taint" in finished wine at very low infestation rates [`tokaji.md`].
- ***Scaphoideus titanus***: the vector of **Flavescence dorée phytoplasma**, already present in Hungary; range expansion under warming is the primary *disease* risk [`tokaji.md`].

No district-level damage surveys quantifying vineyard losses from any of the three taxa are available. This is a Hungary-wide pest/disease monitoring gap (§11).

### 6.2 Fungal disease pressure

- **Powdery mildew** and **downy mildew** are expected to show more intense but more variable years under warmer wetter springs and hotter drier summers. The Csopak Kódex ban on synthetic fungicides makes downy mildew management a key operational constraint in the Balaton shoreline districts [`balatonfured-csopaki.md`].
- **Botrytis cinerea** is a dual-role organism. It is the *asset* of Tokaj aszú (noble rot) and of Pécs late-harvest Cirfandli; it is a *liability* everywhere else. There is evidence from `tokaji.md` citing Bene et al. (2023) of **"continuous change in the life activity of *B. cinerea* as a result of climate change"** that alters both acidification kinetics and biochemical profile — meaning even when noble rot does set in, resulting aszú may not taste the same as historical vintages.
- The white hybrid **Bianca** carries major-gene *P. viticola* resistance but pathogen evolution in Hungary has already produced a *Plasmopara* isolate capable of overcoming that resistance [`szekszardi.md`] — an early warning that resistance-based adaptation buys a finite number of years.

### 6.3 Drought, heatwaves and groundwater

- **Tokaj-Hegyalja drought climatology (Kiss et al. 2024, Markov-model analysis of OMSZ data 2002–2020):** short, extremely intensive rainfalls and longer drought periods are becoming more frequent; steep-slope viticulture amplifies runoff losses [`tokaji.md`].
- **2022 Balaton water-balance extreme** — the driest January–July on record for the Balaton catchment; the **worst negative water-balance event since 1921** (~100-year return period). The lake lost ~300 million m³ to evaporation in summer 2022; water level fell ~70 cm; surface temperatures exceeded 28 °C; lake water-temperature heatwaves that were rare in the 1990s now occur **up to 100 days/year** [`balaton-felvideki.md`; `balatonfured-csopaki.md`].
- **Homokhátság groundwater drawdown** — the Duna-Tisza interfluve water table has fallen **2–5 m on average, locally 6–10 m, since the 1970s** (Pálfai, Ladányi et al., Homokhátság aridification literature). This is the most severe hydrological signal of climate change anywhere in Hungary and affects Kunság directly, Hajós-Baja on its western margin, and Csongrád on its eastern margin [`kunsagi.md`; `hajos-bajai.md`; `csongradi.md`].
- **Lake Balaton surface-water warming:** +0.07 °C yr⁻¹ 21st-century trend → ~+1.7 °C cumulative 2000–2025; +2.2 °C over ~150 years, most of it in the last 30–40 years [`badacsonyi.md`; `balaton-felvideki.md`].
- **Heatwaves:** Hungary-wide intense-heat-day counts increased **+250–300 %** between 1956–1985 and 1986–2015 (Sopron-analogue). The standout hot vintages are 2003, 2012, 2015, 2017, 2019, 2021, 2022, 2023, 2024 [`balatonfured-csopaki.md`; `bukki.md`].
- **Hail frequency and trend** is a Hungary-wide data gap: no district file reports a peer-reviewed multidecadal trend analysis. The OMSZ hailpad and weather-radar archives are the obvious source [`tokaji.md`; `zalai.md`; and others].

## 7. Results — Future projections (Lakatos & Nagy 2025 / EURO-CORDEX / FORESEE 4.2)

**[PROJECTED — ensemble of 14 EURO-CORDEX RCMs, RCP4.5 and RCP8.5, 1971–2000 baseline, 2021–2050 and 2071–2100 horizons]**

- **Winkler class shift:** Hungarian wine regions were predominantly **Region II ("intermediate")** in 1971–2000, are already mostly **Region III ("warm")** now, and are projected to become **predominantly Region IV ("hot")** — with hot-end RCP8.5 tails reaching **Region V** — by 2071–2100 [`balaton-felvideki.md`; `etyek-budai.md`; `tokaji.md`].
- **Growing-season temperature increase:** **+1.0 °C (optimistic model, RCP4.5) to >+4.0 °C (hot-end RCP8.5)** by 2071–2100. District-specific end-century AGST rises span **+2.44 °C (Sopron) to +2.96 °C (Tokaj)**; Csongrád explicitly >+2.5 °C [`balaton-felvideki.md`; `csongradi.md`; `tokaji.md`; `tolnai.md`].
- **GDD-Winkler end-century rise:** **+475.98 °C·d (Sopron) to +581.65 °C·d (Kunság)**, national mean +537.23 [`kunsagi.md`; `soproni.md`].
- **Huglin Index end-century rise:** **+461.58 °C (Sopron) to +566.15 °C (Eger)**, national mean +523.8 [`bukki.md`; `soproni.md`].
- **BEDD end-century rise:** **+182.71 °C·d (Szekszárd) to +258.49 °C·d (Bükk)**, national mean +204.26 [`bukki.md`].
- **Variety suitability loss:** For **low-heat-requirement white varieties** — which include Tokaj's flagship Furmint and Hárslevelű in their classical sweet-wine context, and structurally the entire cool-district (Sopron, Etyek-Buda, Mór, Nagy-Somló, Balaton) white portfolio — **suitability is projected to drop by ~29 % by end of century** [`tokaji.md`; multiple other files].
- **Precipitation:** Under RCP4.5 annual precipitation changes stay below 20 %; under RCP8.5, changes are more robust and largest in mountainous areas. For northern Hungary, wetter winters, drier mid-summers, near-stable to slightly declining annual totals (Mihaljević & Bíró 2025) [`tokaji.md`; `bukki.md`].
- **Regional gradient and inversion:** **Northern Hungarian districts warm fastest**, and traditional thermal ordering inverts (see §3). The Lakatos & Nagy authors explicitly recommend **modifying PDO borders and the land-use structure within them** — a politically sensitive but scientifically supported option [`balaton-felvideki.md`].

## 8. Results — Adaptation inventory across the 22 districts

### 8.1 Active research and variety trials

- **Tokaj Wine Region Research Institute (Tarcal):** rootstock benchmarking of the quasi-universal **Teleki 5C** against **140 Ruggeri, 1103 Paulsen and 110 Richter** grafted with Furmint and Hárslevelű; plastic shade netting during berry maturation; intelligent tillage and nutrient management [`tokaji.md`].
- **Eger (Misik et al. 2025, MDPI Horticulturae):** **late pruning** plus **apical defoliation after veraison** on Kékfrankos delays budburst and flowering by ~2 weeks, shifts the sugar-vs-anthocyanin curve, and produces wines with lower alcohol and better-preserved colour [`egri.md`].
- **Pécs University Wine Estate:** hosts the world's 6th-largest vine gene bank (~1,500 accessions) including rare Hungarian heirlooms — a strategic genetic-resources reservoir for adaptation [`pecsi.md`].
- **NAIK-SZBKI Badacsony:** bred **Rózsakő** (Kéknyelű × Budai zöld) to solve the Kéknyelű pollination problem — a terroir-preserving breeding response now 45–47 ha and rising [`badacsonyi.md`].

### 8.2 Regulatory responses

- **Csopak Kódex (2013 onward, Balatonfüred-Csopak):** a **voluntary, private terroir code** more restrictive than the PDO specification itself, applying only to Olaszrizling and Furmint, with minimum TA 5.5 g L⁻¹, maximum RS 4 g L⁻¹, mandatory yield caps and minimum vine age. Explicitly described in `balatonfured-csopaki.md` as "a climate-resilience protocol written as a terroir code" — the clearest institutional response by any Hungarian district to climate-induced style erosion.
- **Villányi Franc PDO reform (2014):** tightened ripeness, oak and extraction parameters to resist alcohol and extraction creep [`villanyi.md`].
- **Kunság Bianca planting:** ~90 % of Hungarian Bianca is in Kunság; major-gene *P. viticola* resistance permits **2–4 sprays/season vs 8–14** for *V. vinifera* whites; Bianca is the district's single most important climate-adaptation lever [`kunsagi.md`].

### 8.3 Varietal substitution and Mediterranean technology import

- **Tolna (Tűzkő Birtok / Antinori):** Marchese Piero Antinori acquired Tűzkő in 2000; the estate is Hungary's clearest case of a single foreign-investor estate acting as a **Mediterranean technology import channel**, deploying night harvest, cold-chain transport, reverse osmosis dealcoholisation, drip irrigation, and a white portfolio (Grüner Veltliner, Pinot Grigio, Traminer, Chardonnay, Sauvignon Blanc) that is already a climate-adaptive hedge. Plausible next-step Mediterranean whites (Vermentino, Viognier) are within the Antinori group's experience [`tolnai.md`].
- **Mátra:** Syrah, Tempranillo and Gamay trials reported at producer level; not yet PDO-registered [`matrai.md` via district synthesis].
- **Furmint export:** **Austria has begun planting Furmint explicitly as a climate-change hedge**, ironically adopting Tokaj's flagship grape as Tokaj itself reconsiders its product mix [`tokaji.md`].
- **PIWI varieties:** Bianca, Souvignier Gris and Solaris are under consideration in Bükk and Kunság to reduce spray-round demand [`bukki.md`; `kunsagi.md`].

### 8.4 Identity-defence vs. adaptation tradeoff

The district files repeatedly surface a tension between **identity defence** (preserving indigenous varieties and traditional styles) and **adaptation** (replacing or modifying them). The extreme cases are:

- Nagy-Somló Juhfark: 80–150 ha of global ~100–170 ha. **Style drift at Somló is style drift for the variety worldwide.** There is no cool-climate refuge [`nagy-somloi.md`].
- Mór Ezerjó: Mór is the "spiritual home" of a variety that has already collapsed from >14,000 ha (1970) to 1,655 ha (recent), nationally [`mori.md`].
- Pécs Cirfandli: 13.9 ha, down 40 % in 5 years, despite producer-reported quality improvement from 2/10 to 6/10 vintages. The economic trend and the climatic trend are **decoupling** [`pecsi.md`].
- Tokaj aszú: noble rot reliability declining even as Furmint and Hárslevelű themselves remain relatively drought-tolerant [`tokaji.md`].

## 9. Discussion

### 9.1 A double-edged sword for cool-climate districts; an existential question for hot districts

The unified message from the 22 district reviews is that **climate change is not producing uniform degradation of Hungarian viticulture; it is producing two qualitatively different crises**.

**Cool-climate districts** (Sopron, Etyek-Buda, Mór, Nagy-Somló, Pannonhalma, Neszmély, Zala, Balaton north shore, and to a lesser extent Bükk and Eger) face a **double-edged sword**: the warming increases ripeness reliability and rescues historically marginal late-ripening cultivars (Juhfark, Cirfandli, Ezerjó) from the chronic under-ripe-vintage problem, but simultaneously erodes the very **coolness** on which those districts built their reputations for crisp acidity, moderate alcohol and classical structure. Etyek-Buda is the purest example: warming is **unambiguously a degradation signal** for sparkling base wine, because the district's *raison d'être* is its coolness [`etyek-budai.md`]. Nagy-Somló Juhfark gains short-term ripeness insurance but risks long-term loss of the piercing acidity that *is* its market identity [`nagy-somloi.md`]. Pécs Cirfandli is the clearest producer-quantified "winner" in the dataset (2/10 → 6/10 outstanding vintages), and yet its hectarage is collapsing [`pecsi.md`]. The "climate winners" verdict for these districts is therefore provisional: they are winning on grape chemistry and losing on stylistic identity, and it is not clear which axis dominates at mid-century.

**Hot districts** (Csongrád, Kunság, Hajós-Baja, and the hot end of Villány and Szekszárd) face a fundamentally different problem: **existential viability**. Anthocyanin thermal collapse at ≥35 °C, sugar/phenolic decoupling, pH rise past the microbial-stability threshold, Homokhátság groundwater drawdown of 2–5 m, and a rain-fed small-holder structure with no irrigation infrastructure combine to make the question not *"what style of wine?"* but *"can commercial viticulture still operate here?"*. Csongrád planted area has already contracted from ~2,500 ha to ~1,500–2,000 ha over two decades [`csongradi.md`]; the district review explicitly frames the honest long-term scenario as **"controlled retreat"**, with progressive vineyard abandonment of the driest Homokhátság cores.

### 9.2 Regulatory bottlenecks

Three bottlenecks recur:

1. **Irrigation is legally restricted.** Hungarian water law makes vineyard irrigation genuinely difficult in practice. Every district review touching on water (`tokaji.md`, `csongradi.md`, `kunsagi.md`, `tolnai.md`, `balatonfured-csopaki.md`) flags this as the single largest policy barrier to adaptation. The Hungarian viticultural research community is essentially unanimous on the need to ease irrigation legal conditions.
2. **PDO rigidity vs. needed variety shifts.** PDOs define permitted cultivars; under climate pressure some of those cultivars become climatically sub-optimal even as they remain the legally defining feature of the PDO. Lakatos & Nagy (2025) explicitly recommend modifying PDO borders and the land-use structure within them. The Villányi Franc 2014 reform and the Csopak Kódex are the two Hungarian examples of regulation moving in anticipation of climate; the broader PDO architecture has not.
3. **Lake mesoclimate is not resolved by EURO-CORDEX 12 km.** The six Balaton PDOs depend on lake-induced thermal buffering and humidity regulation that operates at sub-grid scales. Until the FORESEE / EURO-CORDEX ensembles are downscaled to resolve Balaton and Fertő mesoclimate, projections for Badacsony, Balaton-felvidék, Balatonfüred-Csopak, Balatonboglár, Nagy-Somló and Sopron will continue to carry an unquantified buffering uncertainty.

## 10. Contested findings

The district reviews repeatedly flag five headline contested findings where further work is needed before the synthesis can be hardened:

1. **Bene et al. (2023) Tokaj polyphenol non-trend.** Against the global warming-drives-phenolics expectation, this Tokaj-specific botrytised-wine study finds *no* detectable climate-driven polyphenol increase, and attributes the overall < 200 mg L⁻¹ Furmint phenolic level to deliberate winemaking choice. Either (a) a genuine terroir-specific exception, (b) a stylistic artefact masking a real climate signal, or (c) a sampling artefact. Independent replication needed [`tokaji.md`].
2. **"Climate winners" verdict for cool districts.** The Pécs Cirfandli (2/10 → 6/10), Nagy-Somló Juhfark, Bükk Kékfrankos and Eger Bikavér cases are invoked as climate-change ripeness-insurance beneficiaries, yet the corresponding hectarages and identity metrics are collapsing or at risk. Whether these districts are **net winners or losers** at mid- and end-century is genuinely unresolved [`pecsi.md`; `nagy-somloi.md`; `bukki.md`; `egri.md`].
3. **The dry-wine pivot in Tokaj — climate-driven or market-driven?** Both forces point the same way (lower autumn humidity favours dry styles; global consumer markets reward dry Furmint/Hárslevelű over sweet aszú). The literature treats them interchangeably. Separating them is essential for adaptation planning [`tokaji.md`].
4. **Per-district Lakatos & Nagy (2025) tables are not in the open-access main text.** Headline district-specific numbers (Csongrád > +2.5 °C AGST, Bükk +258.49 °C BEDD, Kunság +581.65 °C·d GDD, Sopron +66.16 and +475.98 GDD, Tokaj +173.96 and +2.96 °C AGST, Eger +566.15 Huglin, Pannonhalma +2.3 °C) are reliably cited in the district reviews but the remaining districts' values depend on supplementary material not yet extracted into a public dataset. The ranking of non-extreme districts is therefore provisional [`csongradi.md`; `bukki.md`; `etyek-budai.md`; `balaton-felvideki.md`].
5. **Móri-szél trend.** The Móri district's entire resilience narrative rests on a constant ventilating wind whose statistical climatology at vineyard elevation has **never been published**. Global-model "stilling" would be a silent, first-order degradation of the district's adaptive capacity — and it is currently unmeasurable [`mori.md`].

## 11. Knowledge gaps and a Hungary-wide research agenda

Each district review closes with 5–12 individually flagged knowledge gaps; consolidating these yields a prioritised Hungary-wide research agenda.

**Priority A — Data-release and reanalysis**
1. **Extract and publish the per-district Lakatos & Nagy (2025) AGST / GDD / Huglin / BEDD tables** for all 22 borvidék, with decadal granularity, under RCP4.5 and RCP8.5, as an open dataset. This is flagged as a top priority in at least 8 district files and would immediately unblock district-level decision-making.
2. **Systematic phenology series for the 20 districts without published multi-decadal records** (i.e. all except Sopron/Zala and Tokaj). Target variables: budburst, flowering, veraison, harvest for the district-defining cultivars. A federated effort using NAIK-SZBKI, PTE Pécs, Eger Research Institute and producer records (especially the Kreinbacher/Tornai/Fekete/Somlói Apátsági archives at Somló and the Antinori/Tűzkő records at Tolna) would fill most of the gap in ~3 years.

**Priority B — Mesoscale and local climatology**
3. **Coupled lake–vineyard mesoclimate downscaling for Lake Balaton** (and Fertő/Neusiedler See for Sopron). EURO-CORDEX 12 km does not resolve the lake buffering that underpins six PDOs.
4. **Statistical climatology of the Móri-szél at vineyard elevation** — direction roses, speed distributions, multi-decadal trend. Critical given the wind's central role in Mór's resilience narrative.
5. **OMSZ hail climatology for wine districts** — frequency and trend, using hailpad and radar archives.
6. **On-site OMSZ station reanalysis** to resolve Tokaj's baseline temperature discrepancy (9.8 °C 1961–1990 vs ~14 °C recent) and analogous reference-period issues in other districts.

**Priority C — Biological monitoring**
7. **District-level pest and disease monitoring** for *Drosophila suzukii*, *Halyomorpha halys* and *Scaphoideus titanus* (vector of Flavescence dorée). Hungary-wide presence confirmed; district-level damage surveys absent everywhere.
8. **Grape chemistry time series** (°Brix, pH, TA, YAN, anthocyanins, terpenes, thiols) for district-defining varieties — flagged as a gap in almost every district review. Critical for Juhfark at Somló (whose USP is acidity), Ezerjó at Mór, Cirfandli at Pécs, Olaszrizling on the Balaton shore, Kékfrankos at Eger/Sopron/Szekszárd, and Furmint/Hárslevelű at Tokaj.
9. **Soil-water-balance modelling linking Homokhátság groundwater drawdown to vine rooted-zone water deficit** at plot level for Kunság, Hajós-Baja and Csongrád.
10. **Quantitative soil erosion measurements** on Tokaj steep-slope vineyards under intensifying convective rainfall.

**Priority D — PDO architecture and adaptation policy**
11. **Systematic evaluation of PDO border and permitted-variety modification** as climate-adaptation instruments — a recommendation originating directly with Lakatos & Nagy (2025).
12. **Csopak Kódex compliance-rate time series** as a real-world climate-compatibility stress indicator [`balatonfured-csopaki.md`].

## 12. Conclusions

Climate change is not disrupting Hungarian viticulture uniformly. Across the 22 borvidék it is producing a **spatially inverted, varietally targeted, stylistically bifurcated** set of impacts. The Hungary-wide warming gradient runs **strongest** in the north and east (Csongrád, Kunság, Tokaj, Eger, Bükk, Mátra) and **weakest** in the west (Sopron, Pannonhalma) — the reverse of the traditional viticultural ordering of the country. The single largest projected absolute changes are Kunság GDD **+581.65 °C·d**, Bükk BEDD **+258.49 °C·d**, Eger Huglin **+566.15 °C**, Tokaj AGST **+2.96 °C**, Csongrád AGST **> +2.5 °C**; the smallest are Sopron GDD **+66.16 °C·d** (historical) / **+475.98 °C·d** (projected), and Pannonhalma AGST **+2.3 °C**.

The observed chemical signal — must-sugar surplus of 55–60 g L⁻¹ in cool-district whites, −1.5 to −2.5 g L⁻¹ TA in Chardonnay 1980–2020, alcohol creep from ~12 % to ≥14.5 % vol in flagship Pannonian reds, Mori-mechanism anthocyanin collapse in Kadarka and Kékfrankos — is already being written into Hungarian wine styles, and is driving early regulatory responses at Csopak (2013 voluntary Kódex), Villány (2014 Villányi Franc PDO reform) and Kunság (Bianca substitution). The variety-extinction taxonomy is uniquely acute: Hungary's indigenous cultivars have no cool-climate refuge outside the country (Somló Juhfark, Móri Ezerjó, Pécsi Cirfandli, Badacsonyi Kéknyelű) and their hectarages are either collapsing (Ezerjó, Cirfandli) or concentrated on single eroded basalt buttes (Juhfark).

Two strategic conclusions follow. First, the most politically sensitive but scientifically most robust adaptation lever is **modification of PDO architecture** to permit variety and style change without loss of origin-protected status — a recommendation made directly by Lakatos & Nagy (2025) and echoed across the district reviews. Second, the **easing of irrigation legal restrictions** is the single most-called-for policy change in the Hungarian viticultural research community, and would disproportionately benefit the most-exposed districts (Csongrád, Kunság, Hajós-Baja, Tolna, hot Villány).

The 22 district reviews consolidated here should be read as a snapshot — detailed, cross-referenced, and honest about what is known, contested and missing. The research agenda of §11 would transform this snapshot into an operational climate-adaptation decision-support system for Hungarian wine.
