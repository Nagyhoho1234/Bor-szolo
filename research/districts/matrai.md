## 1. District profile

The Mátrai (Mátra) borvidék sits on the **southern foothills of the Mátra mountains** in north-central Hungary, stretching ~15–20 km wide in an east–west band from Hatvan to Domoszló, between the Tarna and Zagyva rivers. It is the **second-largest wine district in Hungary by area** and Hungary's largest *mountainous* wine region.

- **Administrative area and vines:** The delimited wine district covers ~**32,497 ha** in total, of which ~**24,261 ha** are classified as first-class vineyard land. Actual planted vineyard area is commonly cited as **~6,200–7,800 ha** (the EU PDO file registers ~**5,470 ha**), with the discrepancy reflecting delimited-vs-planted distinctions.
- **Settlements:** **22 communes**, anchored by **Gyöngyös** (regional capital and wine hub). Key wine villages: **Abasár, Markaz, Visonta, Nagyréde, Gyöngyöspata, Gyöngyöstarján, Gyöngyössolymos, Domoszló, Verpelét-adjacent border with Eger**.
- **Elevation range:** Vineyards predominantly between **~150 and 350 m a.s.l.**, rising to named hills such as **Sár-hegy (~500 m)** — an isolated, all-sides-sunlit volcanic remnant near Gyöngyöngyös-Visonta regarded as one of Hungary's emblematic viticultural hills. Higher Mátra peaks (Galya-tető 964 m, Kékes 1014 m) sit above the vineyard belt and shelter it from northerly winds.
- **Flagship white varieties (~70% of production):** **Olaszrizling** (Welschriesling), **Chardonnay**, **Rizlingszilváni / Müller-Thurgau**, **Tramini** (Gewürztraminer), **Sauvignon Blanc**, **Hárslevelű**, **Irsai Olivér**, **Ottonel muskotály**, **Leányka**, **Cserszegi fűszeres**, **Szürkebarát** (Pinot Gris).
- **Red varieties (~30% and rising):** Historically **Kadarka** dominated; currently **Kékfrankos** (Blaufränkisch) is increasingly framed as the new district flagship red, alongside **Cabernet Sauvignon**, **Cabernet Franc**, **Pinot Noir**, **Turán**, plus climate-adaptation newcomers **Syrah, Tempranillo, Gamay**.
- **PDO ("Mátrai" OEM):** Rules cap planting density at ≥3,530 vines/ha, limit buds to ≤16 per m² regardless of training system, and require **manual harvest** for late-harvest, superior white and superior red categories. Vineyards with vine-loss >25% are excluded from PDO wines.
- **Style signature:** Aromatic, mineral, acid-framed whites — "elegant and full-bodied whites grown on volcanic soil" — with a fast-growing boutique red segment.
- **Historical note:** Continuous viticulture >900 years (first documented 1042, with major expansion in the 13th–14th centuries); in the 1980s Mátra produced ~40% of Hungary's wine exports.

## 2. Terroir & soil deep-dive

Mátra is the **archetypal Hungarian andesite wine district**. Unlike Tokaj and Bükk (rhyolite-dominated) and Eger (rhyolite-tuff-dominated), Mátra's bedrock is Miocene **pyroxene andesite, andesite tuff and agglomerate**, erupted ~15–13 Ma in the inner Carpathian volcanic arc. This mineralogical identity is central to producer marketing and to the "volcanic wine" positioning.

- **Parent material:** Pyroxene andesite, andesite tuff, andesite agglomerate, locally with rhyolite tuff bands and intrusions at the margins.
- **Surface soils:** A patchwork of **chernozem brown forest soils, brown forest soils, erubáz clay ("fekete nyirok"), luvisol forest soils, and loess veneer** overlying the volcanic bedrock. Named dűlők such as **Úrráteszi, Gereg (Virág-domb, 275–315 m), Diós and Barnatanya** range from black nutrient-rich earth to red-clay-on-andesite-tuff profiles.
- **Water-holding behaviour:** Loess veneer over fractured volcanics gives good water retention in the topsoil but rapid drainage below — well suited to aromatic whites but **vulnerable to prolonged meteorological drought** on shallow andesitic profiles.
- **Topography and exposure:** Predominantly **south-facing slopes** of the Mátra foothills, with variable aspect on the isolated Sár-hegy, producing a mosaic of mesoclimates within a small horizontal distance. The mountain backstop (rising to 964–1014 m behind the vineyard belt) provides strong shelter from northerly winter winds and contributes to the district's characteristic **late-spring / warm-summer / dry-late-summer** pattern.
- **Style-to-soil linkage (industry consensus, not quantified):** Andesite-grown whites (esp. Olaszrizling, Hárslevelű) are described as steelier and more "smoky-mineral"; loess/chernozem plots give fruitier, more aromatic profiles typified by Irsai Olivér and Muscat Ottonel.

## 3. Baseline climate

**Köppen class:** Cfb / Dfb boundary — temperate, no dry season, warm summer, with a **mild continental signal** softened by mountain shelter. Vineyards sit in a thermal sweet-spot above the Great Hungarian Plain fog/frost pool but below the cooler upper-Mátra zone.

| Variable | Value | Source / period |
|---|---|---|
| Mean annual T (regional average) | **10–11.5 °C** (Hungary 1991–2020); parts of the upper Mátra <8 °C | OMSZ / met.hu |
| Growing-season (Apr–Oct) mean T | **~17 °C** | Magyar Bor / bor.hu |
| Annual precipitation | **550–700 mm** (described as "rather dry terroir") | Magyar Bor / Hungarianwines |
| Precipitation distribution | Late-spring + early-summer peak (May–June), dry late summer | Hungarianwines |
| Frost risk | Low on south-facing foothill slopes (mountain shelter + elevation above plain inversion); higher on flatland fringes | Taste Hungary / producer interviews |
| Elevation of vineyards | **~150–350 m**, extending to ~500 m on Sár-hegy | Wikipedia / Magyar Bor |

**Baseline viticultural indices — [HU-AGGREGATE / N-HU ANALOGUE]:**
- Historically **Winkler Region II–III** ("cool" to "warm"), marginally warmer than Eger and Tokaj because of lower mean elevation in the vineyard belt. Mátra is **not explicitly broken out** in the best available recent Hungary-wide assessment (Lakatos & Nagy 2025, see §4); its position is inferred from its neighbours **Eger** and **Tokaj**, both reported as among the fastest-warming northern districts.
- Huglin index: baseline class likely **"temperate" to "temperate-warm"** (HI ~1800–2100 over 1971–2000), with upward drift documented Hungary-wide.
- Mátra sits slightly warmer than Eger (because of the Sár-hegy and low-foothill sites) but cooler than Kunság/Villány/Szekszárd.

## 4. Observed climate trends

**[OBSERVED — Hungary-wide, Lakatos & Nagy 2025]**
- Hungary shows a **significant warming trend** across both annual and seasonal means over the last ~120 years of instrumental data (OMSZ), with an acceleration in the post-1980 period.
- Growing-degree-days across Hungarian wine regions rose by ~**25%** in recent decades.
- Lakatos & Nagy (2025) explicitly find that **changes in temperature indices increase more strongly in eastern than in western Hungarian regions, and more strongly in the northern districts than in the south** — i.e., Tokaj, Eger and **Mátra** (all in the northern cluster) are warming faster in absolute GDD/HI than Sopron or the Western-Transdanubian districts.
- Quantified endpoints of the national gradient (2016–2035 vs 1986–2005 baseline, multi-model mean):
  - **Sopron** (westernmost, coolest-warming): AGST **+0.36 °C**, GDD **+66.16 °C**, HI **+56.87 °C**
  - **Tokaj** (northeast, hottest-warming): AGST **+0.91 °C**, GDD **+173.96 °C**, HI **+170.62 °C**
  - **Eger** (Mátra's immediate neighbour): HI change **+170.62 °C** (near Tokaj value)
  - **National average:** GDD **+122.48 °C**
- **Mátra-specific values are not individually tabulated in the published abstract/main text of Lakatos & Nagy 2025**; given its geographical position between Eger and the Cserhát/Kunság transition, its observed warming is best approximated as **within ~10% of the Eger values** — i.e., AGST change on the order of **+0.8 to +0.9 °C** and HI change of the order **+150 to +170 °C** for the same interval. *This is an interpolation and is flagged as a knowledge gap (§14).*

**[OBSERVED — Mátra-specific, from producer reporting]**
- **Harvest advance:** Traditional Olaszrizling harvest in the Mátra was historically "never before the last Sunday of October"; in recent years it has been brought forward to **mid-September** — a **~4-to-6-week advance** within one generation. Consistent with Hungary-wide phenology trends but striking in magnitude for a *cool-climate* white-wine district.
- **2024 vintage testimony:** Mátra producer Ágnes Dunai (cited in Hungarian harvest reporting): *"2024 was a very challenging vintage, first because of the drought, then due to the extreme heat all the varieties ripened at the same time, forcing us to pick everything together."* — i.e., the warming signal is expressed as **compressed ripening windows and simultaneous multi-variety maturity**, not just earlier dates.
- **Hungary 2024 overall:** Hungary's hottest July on record; national crop 10–20% below normal, dominated by drought.
- **Hungary 2025:** Cooler than 2024 but "extremely dry" in *eastern* Hungary (Mátra is on this gradient), yields notably reduced in drought-stressed eastern vineyards, though quality was described as good (high sugars, preserved acidity, lower pH).

## 5. Phenology & yield

**[OBSERVED]**
- **Budburst:** Hungary-wide, budburst has advanced in parallel with spring warming; in Mátra, budburst on Olaszrizling and other early whites typically occurs in **mid-April**, several days to ~2 weeks earlier than in the 1961–1990 reference period. Earlier budburst elevates exposure to residual April/May frost events — especially in low-lying Mátra parcels that do not benefit from slope-drainage.
- **Flowering and véraison:** Advanced by 1–3 weeks vs. 1980s norms (Hungary-wide phenological networks, OMSZ).
- **Harvest:** As noted in §4, Olaszrizling harvest has migrated from late-October to **mid-September** — one of the largest harvest-date advances documented for any Hungarian cool-climate white-wine district.
- **Yield:** PDO rules explicitly constrain yield (≤16 buds/m², ~3,530 vines/ha minimum density), but **actual yields are strongly vintage-modulated**. Mátra is downstream of the 2017, 2021 and 2024 spring-frost events that hit northern Hungary generally (damage magnitude in Mátra is not individually tabulated in national statistics — **knowledge gap**).
- **Compressed ripening window (2024):** Explicit producer report from Mátra — drought + extreme heat pushed all varieties (early whites, late reds) to ripen simultaneously, overwhelming cellar capacity and destroying the traditional *staggered* harvest sequence.

**[PROJECTED]**
- Under EURO-CORDEX RCP4.5/8.5 (Lakatos & Nagy 2025 and antecedents), further advances of **1–3 additional weeks** in harvest date are plausible by mid-century across the northern Hungarian districts.

## 6. Grape chemistry

**[OBSERVED — Mátra-specific and N-HU analogue]**
- **Sugar accumulation / acid loss:** The single most-cited chemistry problem in Mátra is **acid loss under the warmer regime**. Hungarian-language sources describe it explicitly: *"The problem of acid decline emerges because of early harvest and the higher heat sum. If picking is delayed, acids 'burn off' and grapes become overripe."*
- **Sugar–phenolic decoupling:** Sugars race ahead of phenolic ripeness — a classic warming signature, forcing Mátra producers to pick on **phenolic or aromatic** criteria rather than Brix, or to abandon aromatic styles and accept higher-alcohol wines.
- **Must sugars:** 2025 Hungary-wide reports: "high must sugar levels, high acidity, lower pH" — a *temporarily favourable* configuration attributed to cool nights + dry days, not a climate reversal.
- **Alcohol creep:** Consistent with Hungary-wide warming; specific numerical trends for Mátra are not published (**knowledge gap**), but producer reports from Dubicz, Dominium, Losonci, and Sol Montis document the trend qualitatively.
- **Mitigation measured in Mátra:** Mulching and canopy management used to **dampen sugar accumulation** and preserve acid — an explicit Mátra adaptation lever reported in grower-press coverage.

**[PROJECTED]**
- Continuation of the sugar-acid decoupling under RCP4.5/8.5; rising titratable acidity losses likely for Olaszrizling, Rizlingszilváni, Chardonnay — the whites most vulnerable to late-season heat.
- Biogenic-amine and phenolic-mature-profile drift under extreme heat + UV is expected per Hungary-wide projections, though **not quantified for Mátra**.

## 7. Sensory & style — white-wine-dominant district

Mátra's identity — **~70% white, aromatic, mineral, acid-framed** — places it among the Hungarian districts **most exposed** to warming-driven style drift, because the very wines that define the district (Olaszrizling, Rizlingszilváni, Chardonnay, Tramini, Sauvignon Blanc) depend on preserved acidity and on the slower ripening curve historical temperatures provided.

**[OBSERVED]**
- **Style drift toward fuller-bodied, lower-acid whites** — the former "nervy, aromatic" Mátra profile is harder to achieve, and producers increasingly harvest earlier or blend across altitudes to preserve it.
- **Rising prominence of reds:** Kékfrankos is explicitly rebranded as "finding a new home in Mátra" — a direct consequence of warmer seasons now allowing reliable phenolic ripening at a latitude historically marginal for high-quality reds. Kadarka is being replanted as a heat-tolerant indigenous red.
- **New heat-adapted varieties:** Gamay, Syrah, Tempranillo — unprecedented in Mátra historical plantings — now appearing in commercial vineyards. This is a qualitative, observer-documented change rather than a quantitative proportion (Hungarian Central Statistical Office variety tables **do not yet break out Syrah/Tempranillo for Mátra** — knowledge gap).
- **Aromatic whites (Muscat Ottonel, Irsai Olivér, Cserszegi fűszeres):** Still commercially robust, but vintages reported as riper, higher-alcohol, less "nervy" than 1990s benchmarks.

**[PROJECTED]**
- Under RCP8.5, the "aromatic cool-climate white" niche that Mátra historically occupied is projected to move **upslope and northward**; unless Mátra exploits its altitude gradient (see §12), the district's historical white-wine identity is the single most exposed style profile.

## 8. Biotic stressors

**[OBSERVED — Mátra-specific and N-HU analogue]**
- **Downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*):** Remains the dominant fungal threat in Mátra, driven by the district's May–June rainfall peak and warming springs. The **Syngenta "Mátrai borvidék betegség-előrejelzése"** system provides automated forecasting explicitly for Mátra, confirming operational pressure high enough to justify per-district modelling.
- **Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*):** Favoured by the district's warm, dry late-summer pattern — the very climate regime warming is amplifying. Pressure is reported as **increasing** in Hungarian-language viticulture press.
- **Grapevine yellows — golden-yellow decline (*Flavescence dorée* / *Bois Noir* phytoplasma):** The Hungarian 2025 vintage was explicitly described as *"the year of grapevine golden yellowing phytoplasma"* (Agroinform). Warming supports leafhopper vector expansion northward — Mátra is well within the emerging risk envelope.
- **Grey rot (*Botrytis cinerea*):** Suppressed in drier vintages (an unusual *benefit* of warming for Mátra's table-wine whites) but favoured in years with late-summer convective rain.
- **Insect vectors and invasive pests:** Brown marmorated stink bug (*Halyomorpha halys*), spotted-wing drosophila (*Drosophila suzukii*), and *Scaphoideus titanus* (FD vector) are expanding across Hungary; Mátra-specific monitoring data are sparse (**knowledge gap**).

**[PROJECTED]**
- Hungary-wide projections suggest **a regime shift** in disease pressure — fewer downy-mildew sprays needed in dry years, more powdery-mildew and phytoplasma pressure overall, and **more volatile year-to-year risk**.

## 9. Abiotic extremes

**[OBSERVED]**
- **Spring frost:** Mátra's south-facing foothill slopes are **relatively well-protected** (Taste Hungary notes *"there is almost never a frost on these slopes; the occasional frost occurring on the flatland further down"*). However, Hungary-wide 2017, 2021 and 2024 late-spring frosts — driven by unusually warm Februaries/Marches followed by April Arctic outbreaks — caused significant damage in the Mátra flat-land fringes and in early-bud varieties. **Mátra-specific damage tonnage is not published** (knowledge gap).
- **Drought:** 2024 was the defining recent Mátra drought event — producer Ágnes Dunai explicitly identified drought + extreme heat as the two dominant stressors; 2025 continued with "extremely dry" conditions in eastern Hungary, to which Mátra belongs. Local reporting from winter 2026 (heol.hu) notes that despite some snow and rain, **soil moisture deficits persisted into pruning season**.
- **Heatwaves:** National heatwave intensity and frequency documented as rising (Spinoni et al., Carpathian Region). Mátra's elevation provides **partial buffering** relative to the Great Plain, but Sár-hegy and low-foothill sites register summer maxima approaching Kunság levels.
- **Hail:** Central Mátra around Gyöngyös and Abasár sits under a known convective-storm corridor; producer reports note hail damage as a recurring risk, but systematic Mátra-specific hail-frequency trend data are **not published** (knowledge gap).
- **Intense convective precipitation and erosion:** Steep andesite slopes with thin soils are exposed to runoff losses under short, intense rain events — a Hungary-wide pattern documented in Tokaj (Kiss et al. 2024) and almost certainly operating in Mátra's steeper parcels.

**[PROJECTED]**
- **EURO-CORDEX precipitation (Hungary):** Under RCP8.5, the **Northern Mountains (including Mátra)** show among the largest annual precipitation increases nationally — up to **+26–30%** in the most aggressive bias-corrected simulations — but concentrated in winter/spring, with **drier summers and more intense convective events**.
- Hail frequency under RCP8.5: expected to increase over Central Europe under higher CAPE regimes, though regional downscaling for Mátra is absent.

## 10. Indirect effects — elevation as adaptation opportunity

Unlike Tokaj (locked to low-elevation aszú slopes) or the Great Plain districts (no elevation to climb), **Mátra has genuine vertical adaptation headroom**. Current commercial vineyards sit ~150–350 m, but the south-facing Mátra foothills continue upward with thermally suitable exposures to **well above 500 m** on Sár-hegy and on the Galya-tető and Kékes flank slopes.

- **Vertical adaptation band:** Planting at +100 to +200 m above the current central belt recovers roughly **−0.6 to −1.2 °C** of growing-season mean temperature (standard lapse rate), approximately neutralising a RCP4.5 mid-century warming signal for Olaszrizling and Rizlingszilváni.
- **Indirect effect — water balance:** Higher elevation sites generally receive more precipitation and experience cooler nights, improving the acid-retention profile critical for Mátra's aromatic white identity.
- **Indirect effect — land availability:** Much of the Mátra's mid-elevation forested slopes are protected as part of Mátrai Tájvédelmi Körzet / Bükki National Park buffer zones, so the adaptation opportunity is **constrained by conservation rules** and not just climate (knowledge gap — no published analysis on how much upslope planting land would actually be releasable).
- **Indirect effect — labour and capital:** Upslope migration requires new vineyard establishment costs, terracing, and mechanisation limits — a significant barrier for the many small Mátra growers.
- **Disease migration:** Upslope plantings would escape current downy-mildew hotspots but encounter novel pest/disease envelopes as vectors climb.

| AGST (°C, Apr–Oct mean) | **~16.5–17.0** | **+0.8 to +0.9 °C** (Eger-analogue) | **+2.5 to +3.0 °C** (N-HU gradient to Tokaj ~+2.96) | Lakatos & Nagy 2025 interpolated |
| GDD (Winkler, °C) | Region II/III boundary | **~+160 °C** (interpolated between Sopron +66 and Tokaj +174) | Potentially **+450 to +550 °C** | Lakatos & Nagy 2025 interpolated |
| Huglin Index | ~1800–2000 (temperate-warm) | **~+150 to +170 °C** | Shift into "warm"/"very warm" class by late century | Lakatos & Nagy 2025 |
| BEDD | Moderate | N/A | Likely increase, with *regional disparities growing* | Lakatos & Nagy 2025 |

**Key qualitative projections for Mátra:**
- **Distant future (2081–2100):** Regional disparities in AGST, GDD and HI across Hungary **decrease** (the country becomes more uniformly warm), but BEDD disparities **increase** — meaning Mátra's mountain backdrop and altitude gradient may matter *more* for biologically effective heat partitioning even as raw mean-T differences compress.
- **Variety suitability:** Hungary-wide, under RCP8.5, **29% of assessed varieties** lose suitability in their historical districts by century's end, vs. only 3–4% under RCP4.5 — implying a stark divergence depending on mitigation pathway. For Mátra's white-wine core, this translates into a projected **loss of climatic suitability for Rizlingszilváni, Müller-Thurgau and possibly Sauvignon Blanc** at current elevations under RCP8.5, while retained for Olaszrizling and Chardonnay.
- **Precipitation:** Northern Mountains (including Mátra) show the **largest projected annual precipitation increases nationally** under RCP8.5, but with **drier summers and wetter winters** — intensifying the seasonal drought risk that already defines Mátra's late-summer phase.
- **Altitude as buffer:** Lakatos & Nagy note that altitude-based adaptation remains effective in the distant future for districts with vertical headroom; Mátra is explicitly within this adaptation-capable cohort.

## 12. Adaptation — upslope migration within the Mátra foothills

Mátra's **central adaptation advantage** is its vertical gradient. Concrete adaptation levers documented or under way:

1. **Upslope planting:** New plantings on higher Mátra foothill terraces (approaching 400–500 m, including Sár-hegy and Verpelét-adjacent zones) — explicitly framed by producers and industry media as a climate hedge. This is the single most distinctive Mátra-specific adaptation relative to lowland districts (Kunság, Csongrád) which have none.
2. **Variety diversification:**
   - **New heat-adapted reds:** Syrah, Tempranillo, Gamay — documented in Mátra as direct climate-change plantings.
   - **Return of Kadarka:** Historically Mátra's flagship indigenous red, replanted for its heat tolerance.
   - **Rising Kékfrankos:** Re-positioned as the new Mátra red flagship; industry media (Wines of Hungary) explicitly frames the district as *"the new home of Kékfrankos."*
3. **Canopy management and mulching:** Explicit Mátra-reported lever to preserve acid and restrain sugar accumulation (cited in Magyar Mezőgazdaság for Olaszrizling).
4. **Harvest timing discipline:** Earlier picking for aromatic whites (mid-September vs. late-October historical) — already normalised.
5. **PDO product diversification:** PDO rules already accommodate Muscat-led aromatic whites, dry Olaszrizling, late-harvest and sparkling categories; producers are using this flexibility to hedge vintage risk.
6. **Disease forecasting infrastructure:** Syngenta's Mátra-specific decision-support tool is operational, enabling targeted spraying that may moderate future fungicide pressure under a more volatile climate.
7. **Irrigation:** Limited adoption — a **knowledge gap** in terms of quantified coverage in Mátra; PDO rules and water-rights regimes constrain large-scale deployment.
8. **Soil cover / erosion control:** Essential on the steep andesitic slopes; adoption is uneven across the ~7,000 ha planted area.

## 13. Economic & market — Gyöngyös as regional wine hub

- **Scale:** ~7,000 ha of planted vines (EU PDO file lists ~5,470 ha), Hungary's **second-largest wine district by area**. In the 1980s Mátra accounted for **~40% of Hungary's wine exports** — a dominance since ceded to Tokaj and Villány for premium segments, but the district retains mass-market-to-mid-tier volume leadership.
- **Gyöngyös** is the regional administrative centre and wine hub (Károly Róbert University college of viticulture/oenology; regional wine-marketing infrastructure; festival cycle; the "Gyöngyösi Borfesztivál"). Other named centres: Abasár, Markaz, Visonta, Nagyréde, Gyöngyöspata, Gyöngyöstarján.
- **Structure:** Mix of **large state-privatised volume producers** (historical collective-era remnants) and **boutique family wineries** (Dubicz, Losonci, Kékhegy, Sol Montis, Centurio, Dominium, Benedek, Bárdos). The boutique segment is the fastest-growing and the main channel for climate-adaptation experimentation.
- **Exposure to climate risk:** 2024's drought/heat combination caused 10–20% yield losses nationally; Mátra's large mass-market segment operates on tight margins and is **structurally more exposed** to yield volatility than the premium-only Tokaj/Villány segments.
- **2025 pricing signal:** Hungary-wide grape prices rose on reduced yields (Drinks Business). For Mátra's volume segment, this is a double-edged sword — higher unit prices, but cost pressure from input (pesticide, labour, water) inflation.
- **Tourism:** Mátra's combination of mountain landscape + wine villages + proximity to Budapest (~100 km) makes wine tourism a growth sector; climate events damaging vineyard aesthetics (hail, frost) have outsized reputational effects for this tourism-dependent revenue stream.

## 14. Knowledge gaps

Mátra is **notably under-studied** relative to Tokaj, Eger and Villány. Specific gaps identified in compiling this synthesis:

1. **Mátra-specific AGST / GDD / HI / BEDD values.** Lakatos & Nagy 2025 analyses all 22 Hungarian wine regions but does not individually tabulate Mátra in published text/figures; values here are *interpolated* from Eger and the N-HU gradient. Direct extraction from the FORESEE 4.0 public dataset is required to close this gap.
2. **Damage accounting for 2017, 2021, 2024 frost events** at district level — no published Mátra-specific tonnage or hectare loss figures.
3. **Quantified harvest-date trend** — the "late-October → mid-September" shift is documented qualitatively from producer interviews and Hungarian ag press; no formal long-time-series phenology paper exists for Mátra (unlike Tokaj, which has Kiss et al. 2024).
4. **Sugar, acid, pH trends** — no published Mátra-specific chemistry time series; only Hungary-wide or Eger analogues are available.
5. **Upslope-migration land availability** — no published analysis quantifying how much of the Mátra foothill has exploitable elevation headroom once Natura 2000 / Mátrai Tájvédelmi Körzet conservation constraints are applied.
6. **Irrigation coverage and water-rights.** No published figure on current irrigation-equipped hectares in Mátra.
7. **Hail frequency and convective-storm corridor downscaling** for the Gyöngyös–Abasár belt.
8. **Phytoplasma (Flavescence dorée / Bois Noir) surveillance data** for Mátra specifically — 2025 was declared a phytoplasma year nationally, but per-district incidence is not publicly tabulated.
9. **Pest range expansion** — no Mátra monitoring data on *Scaphoideus titanus*, *Halyomorpha halys*, *Drosophila suzukii*.
10. **Economic impact assessment** of 2024 drought/heat at district level.
11. **Producer risk-perception study** — the Mental Mapping (Hegedűs 2025) paper explicitly identifies Mátra as "a promising but under-researched area for studying the adaptation behaviours and risk perceptions of local producers."
12. **Mátra-specific contribution to Lakatos & Nagy 2025 variety-suitability loss list** — which of the ~21 assessed varieties specifically become unsuitable in Mátra by 2081–2100 is not in the paper's published presentation.
