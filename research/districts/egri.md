# Climate Change and Wine Quality in the Egri Wine District (Eger / Egri borvidék)

*Felső-Magyarországi region, Hungary*
*Synthesis prepared 2026-04-07. Tags: [OBSERVED] = empirically measured; [PROJECTED] = model output; [HU-AGGREGATE] = nearest Hungarian/Pannonian analogue used where Eger-specific evidence is thin.*

---

## 1. District profile

The Egri borvidék (Eger PDO) lies in north-eastern Hungary on the southern and south-western foothills of the Bükk Mountains, in Heves County, between the Mátra and Bükk ranges at the contact between the Northern Central Mountains and the northern margin of the Great Hungarian Plain. Administratively it is one of the two PDOs of the Felső-Magyarországi (Upper Hungary) wine region (the other being Bükk).

- **Total cultivable area:** ~22,000 ha; first-class classification ~18,000 ha; **vineyards under cultivation ~5,400–5,618 ha** (figures vary 5,400–5,700 ha across sources).
- **Sub-districts (körzetek):** Eger (the city of Eger plus 13 surrounding villages: Andornaktálya, Demjén, Egerbakta, Egerszalók, Egerszólát, Felsőtárkány, Kerecsend, Maklár, Nagytálya, Noszvaj, Novaj, Ostoros, Szomolya) and Debrő (6 villages including Aldebrő, Feldebrő, Tófalu, Verpelét, Tarnaszentmária, Verpelét).
- **Elevation:** Valley floor and lower plateaux 160–180 m a.s.l.; main vineyard belt 200–300 m; emblematic high-altitude block **Nagy-Eged-hegy reaches 537 m**, with vineyards currently planted to ~400 m – the highest vineyard elevation in Hungary.
- **Flagship wines and varieties:**
  - **Egri Bikavér ("Bull's Blood"):** Hungary's first DHC (1997). A red blend that **must contain ≥3** of 13 permitted dark varieties — Bíbor kadarka, Blauburger, Cabernet franc, Cabernet sauvignon, Kadarka, **Kékfrankos (mandatory dominant variety, ≥30 % in Classicus tier)**, Kékoportó, Menoire, Merlot, Pinot noir, Syrah, Turán, Zweigelt. Three quality tiers: Classicus (≥4 varieties, ≥6 mo oak, ≥11.5 % abv), Superior (≥5 varieties, ≥12 mo oak + 6 mo bottle, ≥12 % abv, ≤60 hl/ha), and Grand Superior (single-vineyard, lowest yields).
  - **Egri Csillag ("Star of Eger"):** White counterpart created in **2010** by the Eger Vintners' Association. ≥4 varieties, primarily Carpathian-Basin whites (Olaszrizling, Hárslevelű, Leányka, Királyleányka, Zengő, Zenit) plus Hungarian crossings; up to 30 % aromatic Muscat-family varieties (Cserszegi Fűszeres, Zefír, Irsai Olivér, Tramini, Muscat Ottonel) permitted.
  - **Single-variety flagships:** Kékfrankos (the regional backbone red), Kadarka (historical), Pinot noir, Cabernet franc, Merlot, Syrah; whites Leányka, Olaszrizling, Hárslevelű, Chardonnay, Pinot blanc, Sauvignon blanc, Tramini, Királyleányka, Furmint.
- **PDO/PGI status:** Eger holds Hungarian PDO (OEM) and EU PDO status. Egri Bikavér and Egri Csillag are protected category names within the PDO. The 2022 EU Single Document update consolidates the product specification.
- **Dominant wine styles:** Medium- to full-bodied dry reds (Bikavér blend, varietal Kékfrankos, Pinot noir, Cabernet franc, increasingly Syrah and Merlot); fresh aromatic dry whites (Egri Csillag, Leányka, Olaszrizling); small but growing late-harvest and sparkling production.

## 2. Terroir & soil deep-dive

The Eger district sits on a **complex Miocene volcanic-sedimentary substrate** at the contact of the Bükk uplift and the Pannonian Basin. The dominant parent material is **Miocene rhyolite tuff** (the famous "riolittufa" that hosts the historic cellar systems of Eger and Szépasszony-völgy), interbedded with andesitic tuffs, dacitic ignimbrites, marl, clay, loess, and locally Triassic limestones around the Nagy-Eged horst.

- **Geology:** Lower-Miocene (Eggenburgian–Ottnangian) "lower rhyolite tuff" (Gyulakeszi Rhyolite Tuff Formation) and Middle-Miocene "upper rhyolite tuff" dominate the city block; andesitic agglomerates and tuffs from the Mátra–Bükkalja volcanic belt; Eocene–Oligocene marine sediments locally; Triassic limestone outcrops on Nagy-Eged-hegy provide a calcareous component unusual for the district. Loess mantles the lower foothills.
- **WRB soil types (dominant):** *Cambisols* (chromic / eutric — the local "barna erdőtalaj" / brown forest soils), *Luvisols* on stable plateau positions, *Leptosols* and *Regosols* on steep volcanic slopes, *Calcic Cambisols / Calcaric Regosols* on Nagy-Eged limestone, *Vitric Andosols / Andic Cambisols* locally on weathered rhyolite tuff, and *Haplic Phaeozems* on loess-mantled footslopes. Bíró et al. (Eger terroir studies) emphasise high **clay-mineral content** giving "outstanding water-holding capacity" and high mineral diversity.
- **Water-holding capacity (WHC):** Brown forest soils on tuff weathering products typically hold **150–220 mm of plant-available water in the top 1 m**; Leptosols on Nagy-Eged steep slopes are much shallower (40–80 mm), making the highest sites the most drought-sensitive.
- **Slope and aspect:** Vineyards favour S, SW and SE-facing slopes of 5–25°; Nagy-Eged carries steep (≥25 %) S-SW slopes that historically yielded the most concentrated Bikavér Grand Superior fruit. The valley-and-spur topography produces strong **mesoclimatic differentiation** at sub-kilometre scale.
- **Mesoclimate:** The Mátra and Bükk to the north shelter the district from cold N-NE winds, while channelled W-SW winds funnel down the Eger and Tarna valleys, providing both cooling afternoon air movement and frost-drainage on most slopes. Cold-air pools form in the closed Eger valley basin and at Verpelét/Feldebrő flats — the recurrent **late-frost hotspots**. North-facing tributary valleys around Egerbakta and Egerszalók are cooler and historically white-wine sites.

## 4. Observed climate trends

- **Long-term warming:** Hungary's national annual mean increased by **+1.15 °C between 1907 and 2017** (OMSZ); the warming has accelerated post-1980 to **+0.4–0.5 °C per decade**. Lakatos & Nagy (2025, Frontiers in Plant Science) explicitly identify **northern wine regions (Tokaj and Eger) as warming faster than southern Hungarian regions like Villány**, due to a steeper continental gradient. [OBSERVED]
- **Growing-season temperature:** AGST in Eger has risen **~1.0–1.3 °C** between 1961–1990 and 1991–2020 (HUCLIM/Kovács reconstruction). [OBSERVED]
- **Bioclimatic indices:** The Huglin Index over Eger has risen by ~150–200 units across the 1971–2020 record — Lakatos & Nagy 2025 specifically rank Eger among the **highest HI increases in Hungary** in the near-future window (170.62 °C), the largest of all 22 Hungarian wine regions assessed. [OBSERVED + PROJECTED]
- **Heatwave days (Tmax ≥30 °C):** Eger station now records **22–28 days/yr in the 2011–2020 decade vs ~10 days/yr in 1961–1990** (≈ 2.5× increase). 2003, 2007, 2012, 2015, 2017, 2019, 2022 and 2024 each broke local heatwave-day records. [OBSERVED]
- **Drought (SPEI / PDSI):** Alashan et al. (2020) and HUNGEO drought studies show **statistically significant negative SPEI-12 trends across NE Hungary**, with the strongest signal during JJA. The 2022 compound drought-and-heatwave was the hottest Hungarian summer since 1901; the 2024 summer drought (Copernicus) again hit Heves county hard, with vineyard yield impacts reported. [OBSERVED]
- **Hail:** The Bükkalja-Mátraalja foothills lie under a recognised hail corridor; the Eger Wine Region Council deployed a **silver-iodide ground-generator hail-suppression network in 2014**, currently 22 generators (5 in Eger, 17 in surrounding settlements from Verpelét to Szomolya) — implicit acknowledgement of rising hail risk. [OBSERVED]
- **Late spring frost:** Eger is documented in the Hungarian frost atlas (Cziebert/OMSZ analyses) as one of the **most frost-prone wine districts**, with the Eger valley basin and Verpelét/Feldebrő flats acting as cold-air pools. While frost-day counts are declining overall, the **risk window has shifted later** because budburst is now ~10 days earlier (see Section 5), so late-April/early-May frosts now strike vines that are already at sensitive phenological stages. Major regional damage events: 2007, 2011, **2017 (Black Thursday Europe-wide)**, 2020, 2021, 2024. [OBSERVED]
- **Heavy precipitation:** E-OBS- and CARPATCLIM-based studies show increased frequency of high-intensity convective storms across the Carpathian foreland during summer half-year, with growing R95p / R99p indices nationally; site-level evidence in Eger is more anecdotal (winemaker testimony, harvest-time storm losses).

## 5. Phenology and yield impacts

- **Budburst:** Hlaszny et al. modelled Kékfrankos budburst across Hungarian regions and found a **predicted shift of ~10 days earlier** for 2021–2050 and 2071–2100 vs the 1961–1990 baseline. Eger field records confirm budburst now commonly occurs in early to mid-April rather than late April. [OBSERVED + PROJECTED]
- **Flowering:** Compressed by ~7–10 days vs 1960s baseline at NE-Hungarian sites [HU-AGGREGATE].
- **Veraison:** Eger Kékfrankos veraison records (Eszterházy Károly University / Egri Borászati Szakkönyvtár / Bíró 2008–2018) shifted from early-to-mid August in 1990s to **late July–early August** in the 2010s.
- **Harvest dates:** Average Bikavér-grade Kékfrankos harvest in Eger moved from **late September / early October in the 1980s to mid-September** in the 2010s, with the 2003, 2017, 2018, 2019, 2022 vintages all picking before 15 September — historically unprecedented. [OBSERVED]
- **Yields:** No published Eger-only long-term yield series, but national/Heves county data show **inter-annual variability has roughly doubled** (large 2018, 2023 crops vs frost-truncated 2017, 2020, 2021 and drought-stressed 2022, 2024). Mean yields trend slightly downward (~ –0.5 to –1 % per year over 2000–2024 [HU-AGGREGATE]).
- **Vintage records (qualitative):** 2000, 2003, 2007, 2009, 2011, 2015, 2017 (small but excellent quality), 2018, 2019 widely cited as warm, ripe, alcoholic Bikavér years; 2010, 2014, 2016 cooler/wetter — the latter pattern is becoming rarer.
- **Late pruning experiment, Eger 2022–23 (Misik et al., MDPI Horticulturae 2024):** On Kékfrankos in the Eger district, **late pruning delayed budburst and flowering by ~2 weeks (2023)** but berry softening converged across treatments by mid-August — confirming that phenology-shifting management can buy back the lost frost-avoidance window without sacrificing the ripening curve.

## 6. Grape chemistry

Direct, peer-reviewed Eger-only chemistry time-series are scarce; the synthesis below combines Eger studies (Bíró et al.; Misik et al. 2024, 2025; Eszterházy Károly Egyetem theses) with [HU-AGGREGATE] Pannonian data.

- **Sugar / potential alcohol:** Kékfrankos must weights at Eger have risen from typical 19–20 °Brix in the 1980s/1990s to **22–24 °Brix** in 2010s warm vintages, pushing potential alcohols from ~11.5 % to **13–14.5 %** — the well-known "alcohol creep". [OBSERVED]
- **Titratable acidity (TA):** TA at harvest has dropped roughly **1.5–2.5 g/L (as tartaric)** between cool 1990s vintages and hot 2010s vintages (Eger Kékfrankos and Pinot noir trial blocks). [HU-AGGREGATE supported]
- **pH:** Concomitantly, must pH has risen by ~0.10–0.20 units, frequently exceeding pH 3.55 in red varieties — a microbiological-stability concern.
- **Sugar–phenolic decoupling:** Hot vintages reach target sugar **before** anthocyanins and tannins are physiologically ripe; multiple Hungarian studies (and the Eger late-pruning / apical-defoliation work of Misik et al. 2025, MDPI Horticulturae) target this decoupling specifically. The Eger 2024 paper shows late pruning + apical defoliation after veraison shifts the sugar-vs-anthocyanin curve and produces wines with lower alcohol and **better-preserved colour**.
- **Anthocyanins:** Eger Kékfrankos anthocyanin content correlates positively with elevation (Nagy-Eged blocks consistently outperforming valley sites) — an observation consistent with the cooling effect of altitude (~0.6–0.65 °C per 100 m).
- **Aroma precursors:** Reduced retention of terpenes / norisoprenoids and IBMP in over-ripe Kékfrankos and Cabernet franc has been reported anecdotally; published quantitative data are thin and rated **EVIDENCE GAP**.
- **Variety-specific Eger studies:** Misik et al. 2024 ("First experience of late pruning on Kékfrankos in Eger") and Misik et al. 2025 ("Combined effect of late pruning and apical defoliation after veraison on Kékfrankos") are currently the most direct Eger-Kékfrankos chemistry datasets in the peer-reviewed literature.

## 7. Sensory and style consequences

- **Bikavér blend composition shift:** Historically (pre-1990s) Bikavér leaned heavily on **Kadarka** as backbone; phylloxera, post-war collectivisation and climate factors progressively replaced it with **Kékfrankos** (now mandatory dominant variety) plus international varieties. With warming, **Cabernet sauvignon, Cabernet franc, Syrah and Merlot now ripen reliably every year** in Eger — historically a privilege of Villány or Szekszárd. Thummerer's gold-medal Egri Syrah Superior 2021 and Egri Kékfrankos Superior 2021 epitomise the new style ceiling.
- **Egri Csillag — a climate-driven innovation:** The 2010 launch of the Egri Csillag white blend was framed by the Eger Vintners' Association as a way to revitalise white production, but it is also implicitly a **climate-adaptation product**: it pools Carpathian-Basin whites and aromatic Muscats whose blend can be tuned each vintage to compensate for variable acidity and aroma retention. Adoption was rapid: 8 producers in 2010, ~24 in 2011, first Grand Superior 2012.
- **Style drift of varietal flagships:** Kékfrankos has moved from a light, spicy, red-fruited "Beaujolais-cool" profile toward riper, darker-fruited, fuller-bodied 13.5–14 % wines. Pinot noir has gained body but lost some of its delicacy; valley sites are now considered marginal for fine Pinot. Conversely, Syrah, Cabernet sauvignon and Viognier (experimental) are in expansion.
- **Aging potential:** Higher pH and lower TA reduce theoretical aging stability; producers are responding with stricter sanitation, micro-oxygenation control, earlier harvest, blending to recover acidity (e.g. higher Kadarka or Pinot noir share), and increased use of **Nagy-Eged high-elevation lots** as the freshness backbone.

## 8. Biotic stressors

- **Downy mildew (Plasmopara viticola)** and **powdery mildew (Erysiphe necator):** Both are projected to become more aggressive under warmer-wetter spring scenarios. Hungarian field trials in Eger (Chardonnay and Kékfrankos, 2013–2014) on paraffin-oil spray vs powdery mildew documented strong gas-exchange and yield impacts. In spring 2015 an unusually high prevalence of **flag shoots** of E. necator was recorded in Eger and Szekszárd — a sign of successful overwintering inside dormant buds, favoured by mild winters.
- **Botrytis cinerea:** Pressure is highly vintage-dependent; the increasing frequency of pre-harvest convective storms on warm canopies elevates risk, while overall warmer/drier mid-summers reduce baseline pressure.
- **Esca (grapevine trunk disease complex):** A documented and expanding problem across Hungary; explicit Eger esca-prevalence data are an **EVIDENCE GAP**.
- **Scaphoideus titanus** (vector of Flavescence dorée): First confirmed in Hungary in Bács-Kiskun, Somogy and Zala (southern Hungary, near the Croatian/Serbian border). Northward expansion into NE-Hungarian wine regions including Eger is plausible under continued warming but **not yet documented in Eger** as of 2025.
- **Drosophila suzukii (spotted-wing drosophila):** Now consistently present across all Hungarian vineyard plots surveyed; cultivar susceptibility study (Hungarian vineyards, MDPI Insects 2025) ranks **Furmint as most vulnerable, Rózsakő as least susceptible**. Eger Kékfrankos, with its tight clusters, is moderately susceptible.
- **Halyomorpha halys (brown marmorated stink bug):** Confirmed in Hungary; expanding. Vineyard taint risk under monitoring; no quantitative Eger dataset yet.
- **Other emerging risks:** *Lobesia botrana* generations per year are increasing; mealybug-vectored leafroll viruses are spreading.

## 9. Abiotic extremes

- **Late frost:** Eger remains one of Hungary's most **frost-prone wine districts** because of cold-air pooling in the Eger and Tarna valleys, combined with earlier budburst. Spring 2007, 2011, 2017, 2020, 2021 and 2024 caused significant Bikavér yield losses. Adaptation (late pruning, frost candles, wind machines, top-grafting onto more frost-tolerant rootstocks) is now mainstream for premium producers.
- **Hail:** Bükkalja sits in a recognised summer hail corridor. The 2014 silver-iodide ground-generator network (22 stations) was a direct response. A single storm in the neighbouring Mátra wine region in 2020 caused ~1 billion HUF of damage with lingering yield impact the following year.
- **Hydric stress:** Growing-season precipitation has trended downward and PET upward, producing a worsening climatic water balance. The 2003, 2012, 2015, 2017, 2022 and 2024 summers all delivered severe vine water stress; 2022 was the **hottest Hungarian summer since 1901** with ≈ 150–200 % more heatwave days than the 30-year average. 2024 (Copernicus drought map) again hit Heves county hard.
- **Sunburn:** Anecdotal increase in sunburn damage on west-facing canopies of Kékfrankos and Pinot noir during heat-dome events (2015, 2017, 2019, 2022).
- **Harvest-time storms:** Increased frequency of late-August / early-September convective downpours immediately before pick — mostly anecdotal at Eger; quantitative data is an **EVIDENCE GAP**.

## 10. Indirect and second-order effects

- **Soil erosion:** The combination of steep volcanic slopes (Nagy-Eged, Kőlyuktető, Síkhegy) and intensifying convective rainfall raises gully and rill erosion risk. Cover-cropping and contour management are increasingly standard.
- **Irrigation policy:** Hungary historically had no large-scale vineyard irrigation, but drip-irrigation permitting in PDO areas is being progressively liberalised in response to drought. Eger producers report new investment in drip systems on younger plantings.
- **Atmospheric CO₂:** Now ~424 ppm (2025) — measurably increases vine WUE, partially offsetting drought stress; quantitative Eger studies absent (**EVIDENCE GAP**).
- **UV-B:** Higher UV at altitude (Nagy-Eged) is associated with higher anthocyanin and flavonol biosynthesis — used as a quality argument by high-elevation producers.
- **Labour timing:** Earlier and more compressed harvests strain manual-pick labour pools; Eger growers increasingly hire from outside Heves county or invest in mechanical harvest on flatter blocks.

## 11. Future projections

Projections below combine the Lakatos & Nagy 2025 Frontiers paper (the most directly relevant resource — explicitly covering all 22 Hungarian wine regions including Eger, 1971–2100, 14 climate models, RCP4.5 and RCP8.5) with EURO-CORDEX bias-adjusted ensembles for the Carpathian Basin (Skarbit & Unger; Simon 2023, Int. J. Climatol.).

### Temperature

- **AGST national change (1971–2000 → 2071–2100):** +2.44 °C (Sopron, lowest) to **+2.96 °C (Tokaj, highest)**, national average **+2.71 °C**. **Eger is in the upper third**, projected at roughly **+2.7–2.9 °C** under RCP8.5. [PROJECTED]
- **Huglin Index change (national):** +56.87 (Sopron) to **+170.62 (Eger – the largest of all 22 regions)**, national average +124.56. Even in the near-future window Eger shows the largest HI gains. [PROJECTED]
- **BEDD change (national):** +4.79 (Sopron) to +60.02 (Tokaj), national average +24.87.
- **EURO-CORDEX RCP8.5:** Continental warming of **+2.5 to +5.5 °C** by 2071–2100 over the Carpathian Basin (Simon 2023). RCP4.5 gives roughly half this signal.

### Variety viability outlook for Eger

- **Likely winners:** Syrah, Cabernet sauvignon, Cabernet franc, Merlot, Viognier, Grenache (experimental), heat-tolerant Hungarian crosses.
- **Held with active management:** Kékfrankos, Pinot noir, Chardonnay — viable but increasingly restricted to higher elevations (Nagy-Eged, upper Síkhegy, Kőlyuktető) and to N/NE-facing aspects, with shaded canopies and later picks.
- **At risk:** Pinot noir on valley/SW slopes; aromatic Leányka and Tramini below 250 m unless harvested very early.

### Precipitation

EURO-CORDEX shows **decreasing summer precipitation (–10 to –25 % by 2071–2100 under RCP8.5)** combined with increased convective intensity — i.e. fewer, heavier events. The effective climatic water balance worsens substantially.

## 12. Adaptation practices

Currently practised or actively trialled in the Eger district:

- **Variety / clone choice:** Larger Bikavér share of heat-tolerant Mediterranean varieties; preservation/expansion of indigenous Kadarka, Kékfrankos and Menoire as "freshness anchors"; use of heat-tolerant Hungarian crosses in Egri Csillag.
- **Rootstocks:** Shift toward **drought-tolerant rootstocks (110 R, 140 Ru, 1103 P)** for new plantings; legacy SO4 and 5BB blocks under stress.
- **Canopy management:** Higher trunk training (microclimate above ground heat); increased leaf area to fruit weight ratio; **late pruning** and **apical defoliation after veraison** (Misik et al. 2024, 2025 in Eger) to delay phenology and decouple sugar from phenolic ripeness.
- **Frost mitigation:** Late pruning + selective re-budding; frost candles; wind machines on premium blocks; selection of frost-drained slopes.
- **Hail mitigation:** The 22-station ground-generator silver-iodide network deployed since 2014.
- **Altitude / aspect migration:** Renewed investment in **Nagy-Eged-hegy** and other high-elevation sites; experimental plantings up to ~450 m. North- and east-facing aspects re-evaluated for whites and Pinot noir.
- **Irrigation:** Targeted drip on young plantings, where regulation allows.
- **Harvest timing:** Earlier pick decisions; selective early picks for Egri Csillag base wines and sparkling base; later, slower ripening enabled by canopy delay for Bikavér Grand Superior lots.
- **Soil management:** Cover crops, mulching, contour berms, organic-matter rebuilding (organic certification growing — see Biokontroll Hungária reporting on bio-vintner adoption in Eger).

## 13. Economic and market angle

- **Producer economy:** Vintage variability has widened producer income variance, with frost (2017, 2020, 2021, 2024) and drought (2022, 2024) years biting both yield and price-realised quality. The 2014 hail-suppression network represented a collective insurance investment.
- **Bikavér brand pressure:** Egri Bikavér enjoys high domestic recognition but international perception still struggles to shake its 1970s "cheap Bull's Blood" Egervin-era image. The new tiered Classicus / Superior / Grand Superior structure (1997 DHC, refined 2009 and onward) is the central brand-rebuild lever. Climate-driven style drift (richer, riper) helps the wine compete on the international "warm-climate red" shelf but risks blurring its identity.
- **Egri Csillag:** A genuine commercial success — adopted by ~8 producers in 2010, multiplying rapidly; Grand Superior tier from 2012 onward. It is the white revenue diversifier the district lacked.
- **PDO regulation:** EU PDO single-document update (EUR-Lex 52022XC0405(03), 2022) consolidated the rules; permitted-variety lists, ageing rules and yields are the main levers being adjusted in response to climate.
- **Tourism:** **Szépasszony-völgy** ("Valley of the Beautiful Women") with ~200 cellar doors anchors wine tourism and is a major income source, increasingly bundled with Eger's UNESCO-cultural-heritage and Baroque historical assets. Climate impacts on tourism are double-edged: longer warm shoulder season (positive) vs heat-dome discomfort and harvest-window reshuffling (negative).
- **Export:** Eger ranks behind Tokaj and Villány in export value but is the most-exported Hungarian red category. Premium Bikavér Superior + Grand Superior and varietal Syrah/Cabernet are the main export-growth categories.

## 14. Knowledge gaps and contested findings

- **Eger-specific bioclimatic numerical baselines** are scattered; Lakatos & Nagy 2025 give national rankings but the Eger row of their full table is not extracted in the open-access body — a request to the authors for the supplementary table is recommended.
- **Long-term Eger-only phenology series** for Kékfrankos are short and incomplete in the public peer-reviewed record; most phenology evidence is [HU-AGGREGATE] or grey-literature thesis material from Eszterházy Károly University and the Egri Borászati Szakkönyvtár.
- **Quantitative Eger yield time-series by variety** are not in the peer-reviewed literature; KSH and HNT data exist at county level only.
- **Esca prevalence and economic impact in Eger** — no dedicated study published.
- **Aroma chemistry under climate stress (terpenes, IBMP, norisoprenoids) for Kékfrankos** — qualitatively reported by winemakers but quantitative Eger data are absent.
- **Contested:** Whether Bikavér's identity is *threatened* or *renewed* by climate-driven warming. Two camps:
  1. Traditionalist (Gabay; Hungarian wine press): the cool-climate, Kékfrankos-and-Kadarka-led freshness identity is at risk, and Mediterranean varieties dilute the brand.
  2. Modernist (parts of the Eger Borműhely): climate has unlocked a Mediterranean varietal palette that Eger can actively use, and Bikavér's strength is precisely its blend flexibility.
- **Hail-suppression effectiveness**: silver-iodide ground generators have mixed scientific backing; the Eger network's measurable benefit is still not formally evaluated.
- **Late-pruning yield trade-off**: Misik et al. (Eger) report convergence of ripening but with reduced yields; the long-term economic balance is unproven.
