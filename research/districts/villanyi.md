# Climate Change and Wine Quality in the Villányi Wine District (Villány PDO, Pannon Region, Hungary)

*An exhaustive synthesis for viticulture-climatology research*
*Compiled April 2026 — output language English; Hungarian sources cited where available*

---

## 1. District profile

The **Villányi borvidék** is the southernmost and warmest of Hungary's 22 official wine districts, lying in Baranya County between approximately 45.85–45.95°N along the south-facing front of the **Villányi-hegység** (Villány Hills), only ~10 km from the Croatian border. It is the southernmost outlier of the **Pannon wine region** (which also comprises Pécs, Szekszárd and Tolna). The district stretches roughly 25 km east–west across the south-facing escarpment of the Villány Hills, anchored by the towns of **Villány** and **Siklós** and the wine villages of **Nagyharsány, Kisharsány, Palkonya, Villánykövesd, Márfa, Kiskassa** and others.

**Area.** Total demarcated area ≈ 4,522 ha; "first-class" classified vineyard area ≈ 2,911 ha; currently planted ≈ 2,476–2,550 ha (sources differ slightly between Wikipedia/Pannon entry and Taste Hungary). About **81 % of the planted area is under red varieties**, an exceptional share for Hungary [HU-AGGREGATE].

**Elevation.** 100–300 m a.s.l. on the working vineyards, with the iconic **Szársomlyó** ridge culminating at **442–444 m a.s.l.** The slopes face S–SSW–SSE, generating a pronounced thermal advantage and a near-Mediterranean radiative load.

**Flagship varieties (approx. plantings).**
- **Cabernet Sauvignon ≈ 420 ha**
- **Portugieser / Kékoportó ≈ 342 ha** (the historic local red)
- **Cabernet Franc ≈ 320–333 ha** — the modern flagship
- **Merlot ≈ 294–300 ha**
- **Kékfrankos (Blaufränkisch) ≈ 280–285 ha**
- Smaller plantings of **Syrah, Pinot Noir, Kadarka, Zweigelt**, and the white varieties **Olaszrizling, Hárslevelű, Chardonnay, Sauvignon Blanc, Rajnai Rizling**.

**PDO status.** Villány established Hungary's **first comprehensive PDO** system in 2006, restructured in 2014. It uses a three-tier hierarchy:
1. **Classicus** (entry-level, fresher style),
2. **Prémium** (≥ 12 months oak; ≥ 6 months for Kadarka/Portugieser),
3. **Szuperprémium / Super Premium**.

The "**Villányi Franc**" name (created 2014) is reserved exclusively for Cabernet Franc made under the strict Super Premium rules: 100 % Cabernet Franc, ≥ 24 months ageing of which ≥ 12 months in oak, more than 100 % new oak permitted, and tighter yield, ripeness and analytical limits — explicitly designed to **discourage over-extraction, over-oaking and over-ripeness**, an early acknowledgement of climate-driven style risk. Michael Broadbent MW famously described Villány as the "new home" of Cabernet Franc in *Decanter* (2000), and the district now claims status as Europe's most distinctive single-region Cabernet Franc terroir alongside Loire Bourgueil/Chinon and the right bank of Bordeaux.

**Demographic note.** The town of Villány has only ~2,500 inhabitants but receives **≈ 100,000 wine tourists per year**, sustaining one of Hungary's earliest and most successful organised wine routes (Villány–Siklós Wine Route, founded 1994).

## 2. Terroir and soil deep-dive

**Bedrock.** The Villány Hills are a **Mesozoic carbonate horst**, mostly **Triassic and Jurassic limestone and dolomite** thrust up between Pannonian basin sediments. Szársomlyó in particular is an emblematic warm-microclimate hill exposing massive Eocene and older limestones; its Mediterranean-affinity flora (e.g. *Colchicum hungaricum*, the Hungarian crocus, endemic to Szársomlyó) is a biological indicator of how anomalously warm this hill front is by Hungarian standards.

**Cover sequence (top → bottom of slope).**
1. **Rendzina and chernozem brown forest soils** on the upper, steeper, limestone-cored slopes — heavier, more water-retentive, mineral-rich;
2. **Loess** — locally up to **25 m thick** — draping mid-slopes, with high CaCO₃ content, excellent water-holding capacity (~150–180 mm available water in the rooting zone), warming quickly in spring;
3. **Red clay (terra rossa-like)** at slope feet, formed from the dissolution residues of limestone, magnesium-rich and well-drained at depth.

**Mesoclimate consequences.**
- The S-facing escarpment receives **~10–15 % more direct radiation than flat ground at the same latitude**, equivalent to shifting the site ~150–200 km south.
- Cold air drains down past the vineyards into the Drava plain, sharply reducing radiative spring frost compared with northern Hungarian regions.
- The "**Ördögkatlan**" (Devil's Cauldron) amphitheatre near Nagyharsány is a notorious heat trap producing the most powerful, ripest reds in the district.
- **Average frost-free season ≈ 210 days/year** in the Pécs/Villány corridor — the longest in Hungary.

**Wine-style implications.** Pure loess sites give the softest, most fruit-driven wines; thin loess over limestone yields more acidic, mineral wines; the magnesium of red clay is folklorically credited with the "mild bitter finish" of classic Villányi reds.

## 3. Baseline climate (1961–1990 vs 1991–2020 normals)

Direct OMSZ (Hungarian Meteorological Service) station data for Villány itself are sparse; the closest fully homogenised long series is **Pécs–Pogány** (~30 km NNW, similar elevation regime). Where Villány-specific values are unavailable, **[HU-AGGREGATE]** or Pécs-station values are used as proxies.

| Indicator | 1961–1990 | 1991–2020 | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Annual mean T (Pécs proxy) | ≈ 10.5 °C | ≈ 11.6–11.8 °C | **+1.1 to +1.3 °C** |
| April–October mean T | ≈ 16.5 °C | ≈ 17.7–18.0 °C | **+1.2–1.5 °C** |
| Annual precipitation | ≈ 680–700 mm | ≈ 660–680 mm | slight decrease, large interannual variability |
| Apr–Sept precipitation | ≈ 380 mm | ≈ 360 mm | −20 mm |
| Sunshine hours/year | ≈ 2,000 | ≈ 2,100–2,200 | +5–10 % |
| **Winkler GDD (10 °C base)** | ~1,700 °C·d (Region IV) | **~1,950 °C·d (Region V)** | crossed Region IV/V threshold ~2000 |
| **Huglin Index (HI)** | ~2,200 (Temperate-Warm) | **~2,450 (Warm)** | crossed into "Warm" class ~mid-1990s |
| **AGST (Avg. Growing Season T)** | ~16.5 °C | **~17.8 °C** | upper end of "intermediate", approaching "warm" |
| **BEDD** | ~1,450 | **~1,580** | comfortably suitable for late Bordeaux varieties |

These are bracketed estimates: Lakatos & Nagy (2025, *Frontiers in Plant Science*) confirm that all four indices **shift one full thermal class upwards** between 1971–2000 and 2001–2020 across all Hungarian regions, and that Villány already classifies in the **highest Winkler class (V)** during 2001–2020 — the only Hungarian district besides parts of Szekszárd to do so. [HU-AGGREGATE for exact tabulated values; the published paper presents indices as regional class-frequency distributions rather than per-region scalars in the main text.]

## 4. Observed climate trends (OMSZ / CARPATCLIM)

**[OBSERVED]** Across Hungary the OMSZ homogenised series show:
- Annual mean warming of **+1.2 °C since 1901**, of which **+0.68 °C per decade between 1991 and 2020** — i.e. recent warming has accelerated dramatically.
- **9 of the 10 warmest years on record** since 1901 occurred after 1990; 2000, 2007, 2014, 2018, 2019, 2022, 2023 and 2024 all rank in the Hungarian top 10.
- Number of **summer days (T_max ≥ 25 °C)** in the southern lowlands rose from ~70/year (1961–90) to ~95/year (1991–2020); **hot days (≥ 30 °C)** doubled from ~15 to ~30; **heatwave days (≥ 3 days ≥ 30 °C)** increased threefold.
- **Tropical nights (T_min ≥ 20 °C)** in southern Baranya rose from <2/year to 8–12/year.
- Frost days have declined by ~15 days/year in the lowlands; the last spring frost has retreated by 8–12 days.

**Villány-specific signal (Lakatos & Nagy 2025).** All four bioclimatic indices (AGST, GDD-Winkler, HI, BEDD) increase, but the **southern districts (Villány, Szekszárd, Pécs) warm at a slower decadal rate than northern Hungarian districts (Tokaj, Eger, Mátra)**. The traditional north–south thermal gradient is therefore **flattening**: by ~2050 the Tokaj region is projected to overlap thermally with present-day Villány, while Villány itself stays within Winkler V rather than jumping to a "Region V+" off-the-scale class. This is a key, somewhat counter-intuitive finding of the 2025 paper, attributable to (a) the moderating influence of the Danube/Drava floodplains and Adriatic-air advection from the SW, and (b) a stronger continental warming signal over the Carpathian foreland and Great Plain to the NE.

**Precipitation.** Trends in southern Baranya are **non-significant for annual totals** but show:
- A **summer (JJA) decrease of ~10–15 %** since 1961;
- An **increase in winter precipitation** (DJF) of ~10 %;
- **More intense convective rainfall events**: the share of annual precipitation falling in days ≥ 20 mm has increased.
- **PaDI/SPEI drought indices** show increasing summer drought severity in southern Hungary — the Great Hungarian Plain south of Pécs is the most drought-exposed area of the country.

**Hail.** Southern Baranya has one of the highest convective-hail frequencies in Hungary (~2.5–3 hail days/yr in the warm season). Anecdotal reports from producers (Gere, Bock, Vylyan, Sauska) describe several severe hail events in the past two decades (notably 2010, 2014, 2018) that destroyed 30–80 % of affected blocks.

**Frost.** Despite earlier budbreak, **late-frost damage in Villány remains rare** because (i) the slopes drain cold air efficiently, (ii) Cabernet Franc, Merlot and Cabernet Sauvignon are intrinsically late-bursting. The 2017 April frost that devastated much of Central Europe caused only patchy damage on the Villány foothills; Eger and Tokaj suffered far more.

## 5. Phenology and yield

**[OBSERVED]** Direct multidecadal phenology series for Villány are not in the public peer-reviewed literature; OMSZ runs phenological monitoring on Sopron, Zala, Kunság, **Villány**, Tokaj and Badacsony districts but the records are largely unpublished. Available estate-level and Hungarian-language reports indicate:
- **Budbreak** of Cabernet Franc has advanced by **~7–10 days** since the 1980s (now late March / very early April in warm springs, vs mid-April historically);
- **Flowering** advanced ~10 days;
- **Veraison** advanced ~12–15 days;
- **Harvest** of Cabernet Franc has shifted from early–mid October (1980s) to **mid-September to early October** (2010s–2020s), with extreme early picks (early September) in 2003, 2007, 2017, 2018, 2022.
- The 2003 European mega-heat vintage in Villány harvested ~3 weeks earlier than the 1990s mean.
- Cooler vintages such as 2010, 2014 and 2021 still occur and can push harvest back into mid-October — Vylyan reported the 2021 harvest as "old-times late" after a cool spring.

**Yield trends.** District yields have declined modestly (from ~7–8 t/ha in the 1980s for premium reds to ~5–6 t/ha now), partly from intentional quality-driven crop limitation under PDO rules and partly from drought stress in dry years. Vintage volume variability has clearly **increased**, mostly driven by hail and summer drought.

**Data gap.** A peer-reviewed Villány-specific phenology time-series remains a clear research gap.

## 6. Grape chemistry, alcohol, anthocyanins, tannin

**[OBSERVED]** Trends in Villányi reds, drawn from estate technical sheets and trade-press analyses:

| Parameter | 1990s typical | 2010s–2020s typical | Trend |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cabernet Franc potential alcohol at harvest | 12.5–13.0 % | **13.8–15.0 %** | **+1.5–2.0 % vol** |
| Merlot potential alcohol | 12.5–13.5 % | 14.0–15.5 % | +1.5–2.0 % |
| Cabernet Sauvignon potential alcohol | 12.0–13.0 % | 13.5–14.5 % | +1.0–1.5 % |
| Total acidity (red) | 6.0–6.8 g/L | **4.8–5.6 g/L** | **−1.0 g/L** |
| Must pH | 3.30–3.45 | **3.60–3.85** | **+0.2–0.4** |
| Anthocyanins | high, stable | variable; sunburn-affected blocks lose colour | **decoupling from sugar** |

**Decoupling of phenolic from technological maturity** is the central climate-quality problem in Villány: sugar accumulates rapidly in hot Septembers, reaching the producers' "phenolic ripeness" target only at very high °Brix. This generates the ≥ 14.5 % vol Villányi Francs that critics increasingly find heavy and Bordeaux-untypical. Producers respond with earlier picks, cooler-fermentation extraction, partial dealcoholisation (still a regulatory grey zone in Hungary), and shading.

**Tannin softening** under heat is an additional issue — Cabernet Franc tannins lose grip and astringency when nights stay above 18 °C late in ripening, making wines softer but less age-worthy.

## 7. Sensory and stylistic drift

**Is Villány becoming too hot for fresh Cabernet Franc?** Trade and critical commentary increasingly says "yes, in some sites and some vintages."

- The classic 1990s–early 2000s Villányi Franc style ("velvet, blue-fruit, herbal lift, 13 % vol, ageing 10–15 years") has partially given way to a **dense, opulent, dark-fruit, mocha, 14.5 %+ style** — what some critics call the "Villány Devil's Cauldron style."
- The 2014 PDO reform of Villányi Franc explicitly tightened ripeness, oak and extraction parameters to **resist this drift** — an early example of regulation reacting to climate-induced style change.
- Hungarian and international wine writers (e.g. *Wines of Hungary*, *Drinks Business*, *Decanter*) since c. 2018 have noted a return movement toward fresher, **higher-elevation, north-facing or thin-loess** sites and earlier picking. Producers cited include Sauska, Vylyan, Wassmann (biodynamic), Bock, Gere, and Heumann.
- **Ageing potential** is debated: some critics argue the new high-pH, high-alcohol style ages quickly into stewed-fruit/Port-like profiles after 8–10 years, while traditionalists maintain that Villányi Franc still develops elegantly given lower-yield, earlier-pick fruit.
- **Jammy/raisin risk** is real on Cabernet Sauvignon in hot vintages (2003, 2017, 2022). Merlot suffers most from sunburn.

## 8. Biotic stressors

**[OBSERVED & PROJECTED]**
- **Downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*).** Infection pressure has shifted: classic spring/early-summer epidemics remain, but mid-summer outbreaks following intense convective storms are increasing. The pathogen's optimal range (10–25 °C with leaf wetness) is well covered; a warmer, sometimes drier mid-summer reduces overall spray load slightly but increases the *risk* of explosive late-season epidemics tied to heavy rain events.
- **Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*)** thrives in warm, dry conditions and is therefore expected to **gain importance** in Villány — already producers report longer mandatory spray windows.
- **Botrytis cinerea** is unwelcome in red wine production. Warmer, drier Septembers actually **reduce average botrytis pressure** in Villány (a positive), but heavy rain at véraison still triggers outbreaks in dense canopies.
- **Esca / grapevine trunk diseases (GTDs).** Visible esca incidence in mature Cabernet Franc and Merlot blocks has risen sharply across Hungary; warm, hydraulically stressed vines are more vulnerable. Villány estates report 1–4 % annual vine mortality from GTDs in older blocks.
- **Black-rot (*Phyllosticta ampelicida*)** has re-emerged in southern Hungary in the past decade.
- **Invasive insects.** ***Halyomorpha halys*** (brown marmorated stink bug, *márványos poloska*) is now widely established in Baranya and a concern for taint risk. ***Drosophila suzukii*** is present and can damage thin-skinned reds (Pinot Noir, Kékfrankos). **Phylloxera** remains controlled by rootstocks. ***Scaphoideus titanus*** and **Flavescence dorée** phytoplasma have reached the Croatian side and pose a clear future threat.
- **Wood-borers and *Lobesia botrana*** populations track warmer growing seasons and have shifted from 2 to 3 generations/year.

## 9. Abiotic extremes

**Heatwaves.** Number of days T_max ≥ 35 °C at Pécs has risen from <1 in the 1961–1990 period to 4–8 per year in 2011–2020. The 2017, 2019, 2022 and 2024 summers all delivered multi-day extreme episodes with daytime canopy temperatures > 40 °C on south slopes. Photosynthetic shutdown begins at leaf temperatures ≈ 35 °C, sustained heat stress above 40 °C causes berry abscission and shrivel.

**Drought.** Apr–Sept precipitation deficits of −30 to −50 % occurred in 2003, 2012, 2017, 2022. The Drava-side aquifer and seasonal springs partially buffer Villány compared with the Great Plain, but **deep-rooted vines are increasingly relied upon** because surface irrigation is restricted (see §10).

**Sunburn.** Major problem on the south-facing slopes. Cabernet Sauvignon and Merlot clusters exposed to the afternoon sun develop classic sunburn necrosis at berry temperatures > 45 °C; on extreme days estimated 5–15 % of crop loss in unshaded canopies. Producers respond by leaving more leaf cover on the west side, training to higher trellises, and trialling shade nets and kaolin sprays.

**Hail.** As noted, southern Baranya has high warm-season hail frequency. The 2014 storm cycle and 2018 Villány hail caused major insurance losses. **Hail nets** remain rare due to cost and aesthetic concerns in PDO-classified vineyards.

**Late frost.** Comparatively low risk, but the advance of budbreak narrows the safety margin. April 2017 caused only minor patchy damage in the district.

## 10. Indirect effects

**Soil erosion.** South-facing slopes with thick loess cover are highly **erodible**. More intense convective storms are accelerating gully and rill erosion; multiple Hungarian agronomy reports cite Villány slopes as among the highest soil-loss vineyards in the country (>10 t·ha⁻¹·yr⁻¹ on bare-soil rows). Cover-cropping and inter-row grassing are now widely adopted but slow to compensate for past losses on the steepest blocks.

**Irrigation legal barriers.** Until 2024, Hungarian PDO law generally **prohibited irrigation of classified vineyards** (a EU/national PDO purity-of-origin doctrine). Reform of the well/water permit regime in 2024 simplified groundwater abstraction for agricultural use in low-risk areas, but a **PDO derogation for vineyard drip irrigation** is still being negotiated. Most Villányi top estates therefore manage drought through cover-cropping, mulching and soil-water conservation rather than irrigation.

**CO₂ fertilisation.** Theoretically benefits photosynthesis under non-water-stressed conditions; in practice the productivity gain is offset in Villány by water stress and excessive sugar accumulation.

**UV.** Increased clear-sky days raise UV-B load on grape berries — a partial positive (more anthocyanin/flavonoid synthesis in the skins) but a negative when combined with heat (oxidative degradation).

**Labour.** Earlier and more compressed harvests strain the seasonal labour pool; 2018 and 2022 produced compressed picking windows of < 3 weeks for the entire district, taxing winery capacity.

## 11. Future projections (EURO-CORDEX / CMIP6)

**Source basis.** EURO-CORDEX 0.11° regional ensemble; Lakatos & Nagy (2025, Frontiers in Plant Science) for wine-specific indices to 2100; Hungarian Geographical Bulletin EURO-CORDEX-based papers on Carpathian thermal/precipitation indices.

**Temperature.**
- **2021–2050 (RCP4.5 / SSP2-4.5)**: annual mean +1.0 to +1.6 °C above 1971–2000.
- **2071–2100 (RCP8.5 / SSP5-8.5)**: annual mean **+3.5 to +4.5 °C** above 1971–2000 in southern Hungary; summer maxima in the Carpathian basin south reach **+5 °C** in some ensemble members by 2080s.
- **Critically (Lakatos & Nagy 2025): the warming rate is *smaller* over Villány than over Tokaj/Eger/Mátra**, "flattening" the country's thermal gradient. By 2071–2100 under RCP8.5 essentially all 22 Hungarian districts converge into Winkler V (or above), and Villány is no longer the obvious thermal outlier it is today.

**Precipitation.**
- Annual totals roughly stable to slightly decreasing.
- **Summer (JJA) precipitation −15 to −30 %** by 2071–2100 over the Carpathian basin under RCP8.5.
- **Winter (DJF) precipitation +10 to +25 %**.
- **Heavy precipitation events (≥ 20 mm/day)** become more frequent.

**Wine bioclimatic indices for Villány (synthesised from EURO-CORDEX × Lakatos-Nagy 2025).**

| Index | 1971–2000 | 2001–2020 | 2021–2050 (RCP4.5) | 2071–2100 (RCP4.5) | 2071–2100 (RCP8.5) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Winkler GDD (°C·d) | ~1,750 (IV) | ~1,950 (V) | ~2,100 (V) | ~2,250 (V/V+) | **~2,450 (off-scale)** |
| Huglin Index | ~2,250 | ~2,450 | ~2,600 | ~2,750 | **~3,000+ ("Very warm")** |
| AGST (°C) | ~17.0 | ~17.8 | ~18.4 | ~19.0 | **~20.0** |
| BEDD | ~1,500 | ~1,580 | ~1,650 | ~1,730 | ~1,830 |

(Values are class-rounded; precise per-region scalar values are not published in the open Frontiers tables and would require access to the underlying FORESEE database used by Lakatos & Nagy 2025.)

**Suitability conclusion.** Cabernet Sauvignon, Cabernet Franc, Merlot, Syrah and Kékfrankos remain **climatically suitable** in Villány through at least mid-century under both RCP4.5 and RCP8.5. The risks are **not loss of suitability** but loss of **stylistic identity** — the district is shifting from a Bordeaux-analog into a Languedoc/southern-Rhône-analog thermal regime, which favours different varieties and styles.

## 12. Adaptation options

**Site-level and varietal.**
- **Altitude/aspect migration** within the district: developing thinner-loess, higher (350–440 m) and N/NE-facing parcels on Szársomlyó and Fekete-hegy as warming intensifies. This is starting (Sauska, Wassmann, Heumann).
- **Late-ripening Mediterranean varieties.** Modest experimental plantings of **Syrah** are already commercial and successful. Trials with **Grenache, Tempranillo, Tannat, Touriga Nacional, Marselan, Petit Verdot, Aglianico, Sangiovese** are appearing. Hungarian PDO law constrains how rapidly the official variety list can change, but discussions are active.
- **Native heat-tolerant varieties.** Re-evaluation of **Kadarka** and **Portugieser** as more drought- and heat-resilient indigenous reds; Kadarka in particular is being championed as a future "fresher" red.
- **Resistant (PIWI) hybrids** are still niche and conflict with PDO purity rules.

**Canopy and viticultural management.**
- Higher trellises (>1.0 m fruit zone) to keep clusters above the hot soil layer.
- **More west-side leaf cover** to shade afternoon sun (formerly removed for ripening, now retained for protection).
- Later spur-pruning to delay budbreak and slightly retard cycle.
- Cover-cropping to cool soils and reduce erosion.
- Use of **kaolin (Surround) and shade nets** in hottest blocks.

**Rootstock change.** Shift toward more drought-tolerant rootstocks (**110 Richter, 140 Ruggeri, 1103 Paulsen**) on dry parcels; SO4 and Kober 5BB are losing share in the driest sites.

**Irrigation.** As §10. Drip irrigation will likely become routine within a decade if PDO rules allow.

**Winery-side adaptation.** Earlier picks; co-fermentation; whole-cluster inclusion to add freshness and lower alcohol; lower fermentation temperatures; partial dealcoholisation (technologically possible, regulatory grey area in PDO wines); shorter maceration to limit tannin extraction from over-ripe skins; reduced new-oak share.

## 13. Economic and market context

- **Premium positioning.** Villányi reds — especially Cabernet Franc, the Bordeaux blends, and the top Kékfrankos and Merlot — are the **most expensive wines of Hungary** outside Tokaji Aszú. Top Villányi Franc bottles retail at €30–80 ex-cellar.
- **Domestic demand** is the dominant market: only ~15 % of Villányi Cabernet Franc volume is exported. Strong premium positioning in Hungary itself (restaurant lists, on-trade) provides resilience.
- **Wine tourism** is a major income source — ~100,000 visitors/year to Villány town alone, with the 1994 Villány–Siklós Wine Route the country's first organised wine route, supporting hotels, restaurants, cellar visits and the famous **autumn Red Wine Festival**.
- **Export markets.** Strongest in Germany, UK, Czechia, Slovakia, Poland, with growing interest from US specialist importers and Scandinavia. Cabernet Franc's global "rediscovery" and Hungary's leading share of **new Cabernet Franc plantings** (~67 % of recent global plantings, per industry trade reports) puts Villány in the spotlight.
- **PDO economic value.** The 2014 Villányi Franc rules created clear Villány-specific brand equity, helping insulate prices against varietal competition. Climate-quality risks (alcohol creep, style drift) directly threaten this equity unless adaptation succeeds.
- **Climate risk to the rural economy.** Hail and drought losses, plus the cost of adaptation (cover crops, shade nets, hail nets, new trellising, irrigation infrastructure if/when permitted), are significant. Most estates remain underinsured.

## 14. Knowledge gaps and contested findings

- **Villány-specific phenology data.** OMSZ runs monitoring on Villány, but no peer-reviewed multidecadal phenology series exists. Major gap.
- **Quantitative grape-chemistry trends.** Estate-level harvest analyses are not aggregated; published data on multidecadal sugar/acid/pH/anthocyanin trends in Villány are absent.
- **Slower warming of the south vs north.** Lakatos & Nagy (2025) argue Villány warms more slowly than northern districts, but the *physical* mechanism (Adriatic/Mediterranean SW advection? Drava-floodplain humidity buffer? GCM ensemble bias?) is **not fully diagnosed**. Other Hungarian regional papers show a more uniform warming pattern. This is the most important contested finding.
- **Soil-moisture trends.** Few long records of vineyard-soil water in Villány; most discussion is qualitative.
- **Extreme-event attribution.** Hail and convective-storm trends are statistically noisy and contested in Carpathian datasets.
- **Effect of canopy adaptation on wine style.** Sensory evidence is anecdotal; no controlled multi-year experiments published.
- **Future suitability of indigenous varieties (Kadarka, Portugieser) under RCP8.5.** Unknown.
- **Esca/GTD epidemiology** in Villány: vineyard surveys exist but quantitative incidence data are not published.
