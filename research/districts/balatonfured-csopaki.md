# Balatonfüred-Csopaki Wine District — Climate Change Impact Synthesis

**Region (borrégió):** Balaton
**District (borvidék):** Balatonfüred-Csopaki
**Status:** PDO (*oltalom alatt álló eredetmegjelölés*); one of six PDOs of the Balaton borrégió; north shore of Lake Balaton, east of Balaton-felvidéki and west of the Mór / Etyek-Buda districts; home of the voluntary **Csopak Kódex** terroir-quality code for dry Olaszrizling (and Furmint). Often described as the "**Olaszrizling capital of Hungary**".

## 1. District profile

Balatonfüred-Csopaki borvidék occupies an ~12 km strip of the north shore of Lake Balaton between roughly Zánka (W) and Balatonalmádi (E), wrapping around the Tihany peninsula and the amphitheatres of Balatonfüred, Csopak, Paloznak, Lovas and Alsóörs. It is sheltered to the north by the Bakony foothills and opens onto the lake to the south, giving it one of the most benign lake-mesoclimate settings in Hungary.

- **Total delimited vine area (borvidék):** ~6,340 ha of cadastral wine land, of which ~5,790 ha is classified first-class; **actually planted area ~2,130 ha** (HNT registry, early 2020s; bor.hu, Wikipedia HU). This makes it the largest-planted of the six north-shore Balaton PDOs.
- **Administrative backbone:** Hegyközségi Tanács Balatonfüred-Csopak; product-specification protected under Hungarian PDO law and EU register.
- **Core villages:** Balatonfüred, Csopak, Paloznak, Lovas, Alsóörs (the Csopak Kódex "five communities"); further: Aszófő, Balatonakali, Balatonszepezd, Zánka, Tihany, Balatonudvari, Balatonfűzfő, Balatonkenese, Balatonalmádi.
- **Variety mix (>90% white):** **Olaszrizling dominant (~40–50% of plantings and the regional identity variety)**, Rajnai rizling (Riesling), Szürkebarát (Pinot Gris), Chardonnay, Furmint, Tramini (Gewürztraminer), Sauvignon Blanc, Zöld veltelíni, Zenit, Cserszegi Fűszeres. Historic sub-variety **Kéknyelű** is now nearly absent from the district (shared with neighbouring Badacsony/Balaton-felvidék where it is concentrated). Reds (<10%): Kékfrankos, Pinot Noir, Cabernet Franc, Merlot, Syrah on the warmest south-facing red-sandstone slopes.
- **Flagship wine style:** dry, linear, high-acid, mineral-driven **Olaszrizling**, often with a salty-stony limestone finish, characteristic "bitter almond" aftertaste, and proven 10–15 year ageing potential on dolomite/marl sites. The Csopak sub-zone is internationally recognised as producing the quintessential site-expressive Olaszrizling in Hungary, paralleled only by Somló and Badacsony.
- **Csopak Kódex:** private, voluntary producer code launched in 2013, the strictest terroir/quality self-regulation in Hungarian wine (see §7); it covers only **Olaszrizling and Furmint** and defines a three-tier pyramid (Csopak Körzet → VND Csopak village-level / "premier cru" → VNDC Csopak vineyard-selected).
- **Mesoclimate driver:** direct south-facing lakeshore amphitheatre — Lake Balaton (~594 km²) reflects radiation onto the slopes, buffers extremes and delays both spring and autumn frost; the Bakony wall behind the district blocks cold northerlies and funnels katabatic nocturnal air drainage that preserves acidity.

## 2. Terroir & soil deep-dive

The Balatonfüred-Csopaki borvidék sits geologically at the **contact between the Permian red-sandstone / phyllite basement** (Balatonfelvidéki Homokkő Formation) and the overlying **Triassic dolomite, limestone and marl** of the Balaton Uplands. Unlike neighbouring Badacsony or the Káli basin, there are almost no basalt buttes — the district's geology is sedimentary and strongly **layered by altitude** on the lakeward slope:

- **0–160 m ASL — lakeshore terrace:** Permian red sandstone and red weathered forest/clay soils with high iron-oxide content (the "red Csopak" signature). Warm, well-drained, moderate water storage. Traditionally the reserve of fruitier, rounder, earlier-ripening Olaszrizling and of the district's modest red-wine plantings.
- **160–200 m — mid-slope transition:** Pannonian calcareous **marl** (*mészmárga*) and clay-marl; the soil colour abruptly pales from red to white. This is the classic Csopaki Olaszrizling substrate — brown forest soils on marl give body, extract and a salty, tightly wound mineral expression.
- **200–300 m — upper slopes:** Triassic **dolomite and limestone** with shallow rendzinas. Calcareous, stony, low water-holding capacity. Produces the tightest, most linear, highest-acid "limestone Olaszrizling" — the style on which Csopak's international reputation rests.
- **Phyllite / metamorphic basement** — crystalline schist outcrops in places beneath the red sandstone, adding trace-element complexity (notably at Lovas, where the "hematite" sub-terroir has become a named commercial style — see Szent Donát's *Hematit* Olaszrizling).
- **Basalt fragments / volcanic tuff** — limited, mainly on the Tihany peninsula (Pannonian-age alkaline basalt/tuff), which forms a micro-zone of its own inside the PDO.

This layered stratigraphy means that a single estate can move from red-sandstone to marl to dolomite within ~200 vertical metres, generating **within-estate terroir contrasts** that are the raison d'être of the Csopak Kódex single-vineyard tier.

**Mesoclimatic modifiers unique to Balatonfüred-Csopaki:**

- **Lake mirror effect** — the shoreline faces roughly S–SSW and sees ~6–8% additional incoming radiation via reflection off the lake surface, measurably extending the useful photosynthetic day in spring and autumn.
- **Thermal inertia of Balaton** — the ~2 m mean-depth, ~594 km² lake buffers daytime highs and night-time lows in spring and autumn, delaying last-spring and first-autumn frost by ~5–10 days relative to inland Transdanubia.
- **Katabatic cooling from the Bakony** — nocturnal cold-air drainage off the wooded Bakony slopes to the north restores low night temperatures during heatwaves, preserving malic acid and aroma precursors. This is the mechanistic explanation most often cited for why Csopaki Olaszrizling retains higher acidity than Egri or southern Hungarian Olaszrizling from nominally similar growing-season climates.
- **South-facing amphitheatre geometry** — the district is effectively a series of south-opening bowls; slope angles 5–20°, maximising solar exposure while nocturnal drainage keeps stagnation to a minimum.
- **Limestone water chemistry** — calcium-rich soils buffer pH and contribute to the perceived "minerality" of the wines (a property often conflated with terroir but chemically grounded in Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ uptake and acid speciation in must).

Because Balatonfüred-Csopaki is on the lake shore and slightly lower-elevation than its western neighbour Balaton-felvidéki, it runs consistently **~0.2–0.4 °C warmer** in growing-season mean temperature than the Káli basin or Szent-György-hegy elevations for the same year — a small but consistent offset that is reproducible in the FORESEE and OMSZ grids.

The shift from the 1961–1990 to the 1991–2020 normal implies a **growing-season warming of ~1.1–1.4 °C** and a **precipitation deficit of ~20–50 mm per growing season**, consistent with Hungary's national mean annual temperature rising ~1.2 °C between these two climate normals (OMSZ).

## 4. Observed trends — temperature, heatwaves, drought, hail, frost [OBSERVED]

### 4.1 Temperature
- **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Hungary's national mean annual T rose **~+1.2 °C** between 1901 and 2020, with ~0.8 °C concentrated in 1981–2020; summer warming dominates (OMSZ).
- **[OBSERVED local]** Lake Balaton surface water has warmed ~**2.2 °C over 150 years**, of which most occurred in the last 30–40 years at a recent rate of **+0.32 °C/decade**; the western Keszthely basin has warmed **+1.8 °C between 2000 and 2024**. The lake warming is a direct proxy for shoreline growing-season air temperature in Balatonfüred-Csopaki because the vineyards are within a few hundred metres of the water.
- **Heatwave days**: 2003, 2012, 2015, 2017, 2019, 2021, 2022, 2023, 2024 are the outstanding hot vintages. In 2022 the Csopak/Balatonfüred area saw lake surface-water temperatures **>28 °C**, air temperatures **>35 °C** for extended July–August periods, and **water-temperature heatwaves that were rare in the 1990s now occur up to 100 days per year**.

### 4.2 Drought
- **2022**: the driest January–July on record for the Balaton catchment, the worst negative water-balance extreme since 1921 (~100-year record). Lake lost ~300 × 10⁶ m³ to evaporation during summer 2022; water level dropped ~70 cm. This directly reflects vineyard evapotranspiration demand on the Csopaki dolomite and marl rendzinas.
- **2024**: another severe compound drought-heatwave. In Badacsony/north Balaton, harvest started **one month earlier than normal at the beginning of August** — the earliest harvest on record. Hungary-wide yield was ~10–20% below normal; official data showing a 19% increase was later revised to a **7% decline**.
- **[HU-AGGREGATE]** SPEI-based Carpathian-basin studies show the frequency of moderate-to-severe growing-season droughts has approximately **doubled** between 1961–1990 and 1991–2020 in Transdanubia.

### 4.3 Hail
- **[DATA GAP]** No published district-level hail time series for Balatonfüred-Csopaki. **[HU-AGGREGATE]** MABISZ insurance data show a rising trend in large-hail (>2 cm) claims on Hungarian agricultural land since ~2005; the Bakony foothills directly north of the PDO are a recurrent orographic hail corridor, particularly in late May–July convective events. Estate-level reports mention 2018 and 2023 as local damage years in the Balatonfüred–Paloznak sector.

### 4.4 Frost
- **[OBSERVED]** The median last-spring-frost date has moved ~7–10 days earlier since the 1961–1990 baseline (CarpatClim), but **phenology has advanced faster than the frost recession** — a classic de-coupling. Lake proximity and the south-facing amphitheatre mean absolute frost risk in the lakeshore zone is lower than in inland Transdanubia, but upper-slope sites (≥200 m on dolomite) remain exposed.
- **March 2024** brought a late snow/frost episode across the Balaton north shore with localised damage; it was followed by a warm April and very early flowering.
- **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Winter freeze-kill events have become rarer; the last major one was winter 2011/2012.

## 5. Phenology & yield [OBSERVED, HU-AGGREGATE where noted]

- **Budburst**: **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Observed advance of **~7–15 days** for main white cultivars since the 1980s in Transdanubian stations. Olaszrizling is naturally **late-budding** (~7 days after Chardonnay) and that relative lateness provides partial protection against spring frost; this is one of the main reasons it remains the district's dominant variety.
- **Flowering**: advanced by ~10 days in Olaszrizling at Balaton stations.
- **Véraison**: advanced by ~12–18 days for whites; in the hottest years (2003, 2015, 2017, 2022, 2024) véraison at lakeshore elevations has begun in mid-July — 2–3 weeks earlier than the 1980s norm.
- **Harvest**: The classical Csopak Olaszrizling harvest window of **late September – early October** has shifted to **mid-September** in typical 2010s vintages, with 2022 and 2024 starting in **late August to early September**. For 2024, north-shore producers (Badacsony, immediately west) reported the earliest harvest on record, starting early August for early whites.
- **Yield**: **[DATA GAP at district level]**. **[HU-AGGREGATE]** National Hungarian wine yield has declined from ~60 hl/ha (1990s) toward ~45–55 hl/ha (2010s–2020s), with 2022 reaching a multi-decadal low. Csopaki dolomite sites — with shallow rendzinas and no subsoil water reserve — are particularly yield-vulnerable in drought years. The Csopak Kódex tier explicitly caps yields at **1.5 kg/vine ≈ 40 hl/ha maximum extraction**, so the Kódex wines already operate at yields far below regional averages and are comparatively less affected by drought-driven yield variability (they operate at the low-yield end by design).

## 6. Grape chemistry — sugar, acid, pH, aroma, minerality

- **[OBSERVED, HU-AGGREGATE]** Must sugar at harvest for Hungarian whites has risen **~1.5–2.5 °KMW/decade** since the 1980s; for Balaton Olaszrizling this translates from a classical ~17–18 °KMW (~11.5–12% vol. potential) to a recent ~19–21 °KMW (~13–14% vol.), with individual hot-year lots reaching 14.5% vol.
- **Titratable acidity** at harvest has fallen by ~1.0–1.5 g/L (as tartaric) over the same period for Olaszrizling at Balaton stations; pH at harvest has risen by ~0.1–0.15 units. The Csopak Kódex sets a **recommended minimum TA of 5.5 g/L** and a **maximum residual sugar of 4 g/L**, which means Kódex wines must sit in a narrow, climate-exposed window — and Kódex compliance itself is becoming a de facto **climate-stress indicator**.
- **Malic acid** — the heat-labile acid — declines disproportionately. This is the main mechanism behind the drift toward softer, flatter Olaszrizling in hot years and is particularly visible on red-sandstone lakeshore sites where nocturnal cooling is least effective.
- **Calcium / minerality proxies**: Csopaki limestone/marl soils give high Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ uptake, buffering must pH and contributing to the characteristic **saline, stony, "bitter almond" finish**. The chemical basis of "Csopak minerality" is plausibly: (i) Ca-bound organic acids, (ii) sulphate and chloride contributions from deep marl water sources, (iii) preserved thiol and monoterpene precursors owing to nocturnal cooling. All three are climate-sensitive.
- **Aroma chemistry**: heat-stressed Olaszrizling loses thiol precursors (reducing its "fresh citrus/nettle" top notes) and produces more C13-norisoprenoids (TDN, β-damascenone), which push the style toward kerosene/honey/petrol notes more familiar from mature Rajnai rizling. Csopaki dolomite and marl sites are comparatively resilient because of strong nocturnal drainage (§2), which is the mechanistic case repeatedly made by local producers and cited in Taste Hungary / WinEncsy reporting.
- **Szürkebarát (Pinot Gris)**: most chemistry-vulnerable variety of the district — tendency to rapid raisining and pre-véraison sugar accumulation in hot vintages; musts >24 °Brix at pH >3.6 in August have been recorded in 2022 and 2024.
- **Furmint** (the second Kódex variety): behaves well under warming because of its naturally very high titratable acidity; a deliberate choice by Kódex founders to provide a "future-proof" second pillar alongside Olaszrizling.

## 7. Sensory & style — Olaszrizling drift, and the Csopak Kódex as a quality-defence response

The **classical Csopaki Olaszrizling style** is tight, linear, mid-weight, with 6.5–8.0 g/L titratable acidity, a characteristic "bitter almond" aftertaste, and — on marl and dolomite — a salty-stony mineral spine and notable ageing potential (10–15 years under screwcap). Freshness is the commercial identity marker that buyers recognise.

**Observed style drift** under 1991–2020 warming:
- Fuller body, higher glycerol, higher alcohol (13–14% vs. classical 12%).
- Rounder, less linear palate; reduced "tension" and reduced malic contribution.
- Earlier picking decisions trade sugar for acid retention and aromatic precision.
- Increased site differentiation: dolomite/limestone upper-slope wines retain classical style; red-sandstone lakeshore wines drift toward a warmer, fuller profile.

### The Csopak Kódex as a climate-quality-defence instrument

The **Csopak Kódex** (founded 2013) is the most explicit institutional response by any Hungarian wine district to the erosion of classical style by climate warming. It is a **voluntary, private terroir code** more restrictive than the PDO specification itself. Key features (Taste Hungary, WinEncsy, Winesofa):

- **Varieties restricted to Olaszrizling and Furmint only** (Furmint added as a climate-future-proof high-acid second pillar).
- **Five communities**: Balatonfüred, Csopak, Paloznak, Lovas, Alsóörs.
- **Three-tier quality pyramid**:
  1. *Csopak Körzet* — generic district-level wine from any of the five villages.
  2. *VND Csopak* ("Védett Nevű Dűlő") — village/"premier cru" level from ~26 first-class vineyard areas (dűlő).
  3. *VNDC Csopak* — vineyard-selected top tier, single dűlő named on the label.
- **Viticultural rules (stricter than any other Hungarian PDO):**
  - Minimum vine age 10 years.
  - Planting density ≥3,000 vines/ha (≥4,000 for new plantings).
  - Maximum **1.5 kg/vine**; maximum extraction **40 hl/ha**.
  - Only green (organic-permitted) plant-protection products; no synthetic fertilisers, insecticides or herbicides.
- **Wine specification:**
  - Actual alcohol **12–13% vol.** (a ceiling that is itself a climate-adaptation statement).
  - Residual sugar **≤4 g/L** (strict dry).
  - Recommended minimum titratable acidity **5.5 g/L**.
- **Grape selection from 30 classified first-class vineyards** across the five communities.

The Kódex therefore functions simultaneously as (i) a terroir/quality marketing code, (ii) a style-preservation tool defending classical high-acid, low-alcohol dry Olaszrizling, and (iii) an implicit adaptation roadmap through its mandatory yield reduction (which reduces per-berry stress), its ban on synthetic pesticides (forces soil-and-canopy management emphasis), and its inclusion of Furmint as a hedge variety. In this sense the Csopak Kódex is the most developed climate-defence PDO instrument in Central Europe and deserves separate treatment in adaptation policy analyses.

**Reds**: small rising plantings of Kékfrankos, Cabernet Franc and Syrah on the warmest red-sandstone shoreline slopes — a direct adaptive response to warming, but still <10% of the district.

## 8. Biotic stressors — mildews, botrytis, invasive pests, lake humidity

- **Downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*)**: the dominant mildew threat in Balatonfüred-Csopaki because the lake mesoclimate maintains elevated relative humidity and strong dew formation on the shoreline slopes. The Csopak Kódex ban on synthetic fungicides makes DM management a key operational constraint for Kódex producers. Experimental and European data show DM infection severity increases under combined warmer and wetter conditions.
- **Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*)**: rising relative importance under dry hot years (2017, 2022, 2024); shallow rendzinas on the upper dolomite slopes with high diurnal thermal amplitude are particularly exposed.
- **Botrytis (*Botrytis cinerea*)**: the classical warming paradox — véraison has shifted earlier (into early August) and now coincides with late-summer thunderstorms, reducing the window of "clean" ripening. Unlike Tokaj, noble rot is not the style signature here; all botrytis is treated as grey-rot damage and is a direct quality threat for dry Olaszrizling.
- **Esca / trunk diseases**: Hungary-wide rising concern, no district-specific published survey [DATA GAP].
- **Spotted-wing drosophila (*Drosophila suzukii*)**: first recorded in Hungary in 2012; widespread by 2014; now established across Transdanubia including Balatonfüred-Csopaki. Affects soft-skinned thin whites (Pinot Gris, Tramini) and the few red plantings, driving earlier harvest decisions.
- ***Scaphoideus titanus* / Flavescence dorée**: an expanding Transdanubian concern.
- ***Lobesia botrana*** (European grapevine moth): generations per year are projected to increase under continued warming.
- **Lake-humidity effect**: dew persistence on lakeshore vineyards is longer than inland Transdanubia at equivalent altitudes — historically favoured upper-slope dolomite/limestone sites for premium wine (better drying) and lakeshore sandstone sites for everyday wine. Under warming with the Kódex organic-fungicide constraint, this spatial gradient is sharpening.

## 9. Abiotic extremes [OBSERVED]

- **Compound drought-heatwave events**: 2022 and 2024 are the Balatonfüred-Csopaki benchmark years. Lake surface >28 °C, evaporative loss ~220 mm above inflow in 2022, water level −70 cm. These are direct proxies for vineyard evapotranspiration stress because the district's upper-slope dolomite and marl rendzinas have very low soil water storage. 2024 produced the earliest harvest on record on the north shore.
- **Extreme convective rainfall**: OMSZ data show a statistically significant increase in ≥20 mm daily precipitation events over western Transdanubia; under RCP8.5 these are projected to exceed 5 per year by 2071–2100. On steep lakeshore slopes this directly threatens soil loss.
- **Lake water-level extremes**: 2022 and 2025 both produced record low water levels; 2025 saw people "walking on the Balaton lakebed" locally (Telex). The wine-tourism reputation damage is a real economic risk (§10, §13).
- **Wind / derecho**: occasional, not a primary concern.
- **Wildfire**: rising ignition risk in the Bakony foothills directly behind the PDO; no direct vineyard loss reported to date [DATA GAP].
- **Heat-wave human-health / labour stress**: Hungarian Geographical Bulletin work on heatwave resilience in Balaton municipalities flags mortality and tourism-labour risk — directly relevant to cellar-door operations.

## 10. Indirect effects — Lake Balaton, erosion, irrigation, tourism

- **Lake water budget**: governed by (i) precipitation on the lake surface (~45% of input), (ii) catchment runoff mainly via the Zala (~50%), (iii) evaporation (~75% of loss), and (iv) outflow via the Sió sluice. Climate projections indicate a drastic mid-century decrease in Zala inflow and rising summer evaporation; years without outflow become an order of magnitude more likely. Algal blooms — a record bloom in 2019 being the first in 25 years of successful management — are expected to return more often as stratification strengthens (EC 2023, Frwb 2019 paper).
- **The lake is Balatonfüred-Csopaki's unique selling proposition**: even though it does not irrigate the vineyards, it defines the district's mesoclimate, its landscape aesthetic, and its wine-tourism economy. A warmer, lower, algae-prone lake therefore simultaneously threatens (i) the mesoclimatic buffering that sustains classical Olaszrizling style, (ii) the brand narrative on which Csopak Kódex marketing rests, and (iii) the direct-sales tourism business of most of its ~2,100 ha of producers.
- **Erosion**: shallow dolomite and marl rendzinas on slopes of 5–20° plus intensifying convective rainfall create a high erosion-amplification risk; no district-specific trend analysis published [DATA GAP].
- **Irrigation**: Hungarian viticulture is overwhelmingly rainfed. On dolomite and marl rendzinas there is no subsoil reserve to draw upon, but installing deficit irrigation is constrained by infrastructure cost and by Balaton water-use politics (any large lake-water withdrawal conflicts with the tourism and lake-ecology agendas). No district-scale irrigation exists as of 2026.
- **Tourism linkage**: Balatonfüred is one of Hungary's most historic spa/resort towns; wine tourism at Paloznak, Csopak and Balatonfüred is tightly coupled to summer lake tourism. Hot summers increase visitor numbers in the short run but threaten the long-run product (lake ecology, reliable vintages, the green landscape). Kódex producers report growing concern about vintage variability in organic-winery surveys (2025).

## 11. Future projections — EURO-CORDEX and wine-region studies [PROJECTED]

### 11.1 Lakatos & Nagy 2025 (*Frontiers in Plant Science*) — the key paper

The reference study for Hungarian viticulture under climate change is Lakatos & Nagy 2025 (with Lakatos as co-author; sometimes referenced as "Lakatos & Nagy 2025"), *"Assessment of historical and future changes in temperature indices for winegrape suitability in Hungarian wine regions (1971–2100)"*, published 7 February 2025 in Frontiers in Plant Science (DOI 10.3389/fpls.2025.1481431). It uses the **FORESEE 4.2 bias-adjusted ensemble of 14 CMIP5 / EURO-CORDEX regional climate models** under **RCP4.5 and RCP8.5**, evaluates four temperature indices (**AGST, GDD/Winkler, Huglin Index, BEDD**) across **all 22 Hungarian PDOs** over 1971–2100 relative to a 1971–2000 reference, with 300 pixel grid points dedicated to the wine regions (~15 per PDO for Balatonfüred-Csopaki's footprint). Balatonfüred-Csopaki is explicitly named in the paper's introduction as a region where "the moderating effects of the lake support the production of high-quality whites such as Olaszrizling."

**Headline Hungary-wide findings (apply directly to Balatonfüred-Csopaki):**
- **AGST increase** of **+1.0 °C (most optimistic model, RCP4.5) to >+4.0 °C (hot-end RCP8.5)** across seven decades.
- **Viticultural-class transition**: Hungarian regions predominantly "warm" in 1971–2000 → projected **"hot"** by end-century under AGST; GDD-Winkler moves from "intermediate" (Region II) toward **"warm" (III)** in the near future and **"hot" (IV–V)** by end-century.
- **BEDD**: frequency of vintages with BEDD 1200–1400 (the optimum band for cool-style whites, current Balaton default) **decreases by >25% by mid-century**, with displacement toward 1400–1600 and 1600–1800 bands.
- **Northern Hungarian wine regions (including the Balaton north shore) warm faster than southern regions** — counter-intuitive but robust in the ensemble, attributed to continental-latitude amplification; this matters directly for Balatonfüred-Csopaki.
- Hungary is still projected to remain among regions with "good quality grapevine growing conditions", but **variety and cultivation structure must change**; the authors recommend PDO boundary revisions and land-use re-planning.
- **[GAP]** Per-PDO numerical tables for Balatonfüred-Csopaki specifically are not published in the Lakatos & Nagy paper — only Hungary-wide aggregates and maps. This is the single most important data product still missing for district-level planning.

### 11.2 EURO-CORDEX projections for north-Balaton grid cells
- **Temperature**: multi-model ensemble **+1.5–2.5 °C by 2021–2050** and **+3.0–4.5 °C by 2071–2100 under RCP8.5**; greatest warming in summer, with heatwave days potentially **40–50/year** in the warm end of the ensemble.
- **Precipitation**: small annual-total changes over central Transdanubia (**–2 to –4% in 2021–2050** in some bias-corrected products); end-of-century RCP8.5 shows redistribution toward wetter winters, drier summers, and more days with ≥20 mm precipitation (flashy convective regime).
- **Drought**: SPEI-6 and SPI-12 agricultural drought frequency increases substantially in the Transdanubian Midlands, with late-summer water deficits dominating.

### 11.3 Earlier Hungarian studies
- **Szabó & Pongrácz 2018** (RegCM4.3): Hungarian warming greatest in summer; all four seasons warm.
- **Gaál et al.** (Random Forest modelling of Hungarian wine regions): Balaton retains good general suitability but with mandatory variety re-allocation.

### 11.4 Implications for Balatonfüred-Csopaki specifically
Under RCP8.5 mid-to-late century the district is projected to cross **Winkler Region IV ("hot")** at lakeshore elevations — climatically comparable to today's Apulia or inland Languedoc. Upper-slope dolomite and marl sites above ~250 m ASL cool slightly slower and retain the best residual nocturnal-drainage advantage. Olaszrizling as a species remains physiologically viable (it is heat-tolerant), but the **classical linear, 12–13% vol., >5.5 g/L TA Csopak Kódex style becomes increasingly difficult to produce on the red-sandstone lakeshore** by ~2050 under RCP8.5. The Kódex's inclusion of Furmint as second variety is a prescient hedge; late-ripening Olaszrizling clones and selection of cooler north-facing or upper-slope parcels become the main within-variety strategy.

## 12. Adaptation

**Varietal and clonal strategies**
- **Csopak Kódex institutional strategy**: already embodies the core adaptation move — restrict cultivar to high-acid Olaszrizling and Furmint, cap yields, cap alcohol, require high-density mature plantings. The Kódex is effectively a climate-resilience protocol written as a terroir code.
- Selection of **later-ripening clones of Olaszrizling and Rajnai rizling**; work on **massal selection** from old vines on dolomite/marl.
- Furmint, Hárslevelű and late-ripening Riesling clones increasingly used as hedge varieties.
- Exploratory trials of **PIWI disease-resistant varieties** (Souvignier Gris, Cabernet Blanc) for the organic-fungicide-constrained Kódex context; still rare.

**Canopy and vineyard management**
- Raised fruiting zone (from classical ~70 cm toward ~100–110 cm) to reduce reflected heat from the soil and lake.
- Delayed / partial leaf removal on the east side to shade clusters from afternoon sun.
- Later pruning to delay budburst and re-couple phenology to the frost-free window.
- Cover cropping for water conservation, erosion control and soil organic carbon.

**Soil and water**
- Pilot discussions on **deficit irrigation** (no district-scale implementation yet); Balaton water politics constrain withdrawals.
- **Mulching** (straw, local dolomite gravel) to reduce evaporation on shallow upper-slope rendzinas.
- Restoration of historic dry-stone terraces to reduce runoff erosion.

**Winemaking**
- **Earlier, phased harvest** by parcel and aspect — standard since ~2015 among top Csopak producers.
- Tartaric acidification now routinely permitted in hot vintages (a clear climate-era change).
- Lower fermentation temperatures, partial whole-bunch pressing, use of **concrete eggs and large neutral oak** to preserve freshness without stripping texture.
- Style experimentation: skin-contact Olaszrizling, amphora, traditional-method sparkling base from early picks (an adaptation that also capitalises on the broader Balaton sparkling boom).

**Boundary and land-use adaptation**
- Lakatos & Nagy 2025 explicitly recommend **modifying PDO borders and the land-use structure within them** — politically sensitive but scientifically supported. For Balatonfüred-Csopaki this would mean promoting upper-slope dolomite and marl sites into a protected premium tier (largely already done de facto by the Kódex), and possibly retreating from the hottest red-sandstone lakeshore.

## 13. Economic & market — wine tourism, premium Olaszrizling, Kódex producers, PDO

- **Market position**: Balatonfüred-Csopaki is the largest planted of the six north-shore Balaton PDOs (~2,130 ha) and the most visible in the premium-Olaszrizling segment. The Balaton borrégió together accounts for roughly **one third of Hungarian sparkling-wine output** and a large share of the premium dry-white market; Balatonfüred-Csopaki and Badacsony are the two leading premium volume contributors.
- **Csopak Kódex producers**: ~20–30 wineries participate in varying tiers, including Szent Donát, Homola, Jásdi, Petrányi, Figula, Villa Gyetvai, Koczor, Bodó; the Kódex acts as a collective quality signal that travels far better on international markets than the individual estate names.
- **Export**: modest in absolute terms; primary export markets are Germany, Austria, the UK, Benelux, Scandinavia and Japan. The dominant revenue channel remains **domestic wine tourism** — cellar-door sales, Balatonfüred summer festivals, and the spa-tourism crossover market.
- **Brand identity**: inseparable from Lake Balaton and the Csopak Kódex, both of which are directly exposed to climate change. A lower, warmer, algae-prone lake therefore simultaneously threatens brand, mesoclimate and tourism revenue — a triple exposure unusual in European viticulture.
- **PDO regulation**: the cultivar list is being reviewed across the Balaton PDOs to allow slower, structured variety adaptation; the Kódex provides a faster-moving private-code overlay.
- **Tourism-climate coupling**: hot summers drive record short-term visitor numbers but threaten the long-run product (lake ecology, vintage reliability). The 2025 ResearchGate study on Hungarian organic wineries documents rising producer concern about vintage variability and visitor-season shifts.
