# Balaton-felvidéki Wine District — Climate Change Impact Synthesis

**Region (borrégió):** Balaton
**District (borvidék):** Balaton-felvidéki (Balaton Uplands)
**Status:** PDO; part of the six-district Balaton wine region on the north shore of Lake Balaton; neighbours Badacsony (W), Balatonfüred-Csopak (E) and Káli PDO (embedded sub-appellation); shortlisted on the UNESCO World Heritage Tentative List as "Balaton Uplands Cultural Landscape".

## 1. District profile

The Balaton-felvidéki borvidék stretches along the north shore of Lake Balaton, west of the Balatonfüred-Csopak PDO and east of Badacsony, reaching inland from the lakeshore onto the second and third terraces of the Balaton Uplands. It is a mosaic landscape of volcanic buttes ("witness hills" / *tanúhegyek*), dolomite plateaus, inner basins and wooded ridges of the Bakony foothills. The flagship sub-appellation is the **Káli medence (Káli Basin) PDO**, a small inner basin framed by basalt cones to the north and dolomite ridges to the east; the Tapolca basin to the west is morphologically similar and hosts most of the classical volcanic buttes (Szent György-hegy, Csobánc, Gulács, Tóti-hegy, Hegyestű).

- **Total vine area (borvidék):** ~940 ha (MBSZ/HNT registered area, early 2020s), one of the smaller Hungarian PDOs by planting surface but one of the most concentrated by volcanic-soil prestige producers.
- **Flagship varieties (white, >85% of plantings):** Olaszrizling (Welschriesling — dominant, typically 35–45% of plantings), Szürkebarát (Pinot Gris), Rajnai rizling (Riesling), Zöld veltelíni (Grüner Veltliner), Rizlingszilváni (Müller-Thurgau), Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay, Tramini, Cserszegi Fűszeres. Very small pockets of **Kéknyelű** — a Hungarikum-status indigenous variety shared with neighbouring Badacsony, total national planting area only ~46 ha.
- **Red varieties (<15%):** Pinot Noir, Kékfrankos, Cabernet Sauvignon in minor quantities, mainly on warmer southern aspects and red-sandstone slopes.
- **Flagship wine styles:** Crisp-to-full-bodied dry Olaszrizling with pronounced acidity and a distinct "bitter almond"/smoky/mineral finish on basalt; aromatic Szürkebarát; rare varietal Kéknyelű with high acidity, smoke and gunpowder notes; increasingly serious single-vineyard ("dűlő") Rieslings; a small traditional-method sparkling segment.
- **Mesoclimate driver:** Lake Balaton's ~594 km² water surface buffers thermal extremes, reflects light onto south-facing slopes, and — together with the sheltering Bakony Hills to the north — gives the lower terraces a **sub-Mediterranean character** unique in Hungary.

## 2. Terroir & soil deep-dive

The geological patchwork of the Balaton-felvidék is among the most complex in any European PDO of comparable size. Within a ~40 km-long strip along the lake the following parent materials alternate on a kilometre scale:

- **Basalt / basalt tuff / basanite** — Upper-Miocene to Pliocene alkaline basalt capping the witness hills of the Tapolca basin (Szent György-hegy, Csobánc, Badacsony — the latter administratively in the neighbouring Badacsony PDO but geologically continuous). Soils are shallow stony rendzinas and colluvial brown earths with high skeletal content, rich in Fe, Mg, K and trace elements; strong heat retention by day, rapid night cooling. Most prestigious for white wine; Kéknyelű and the best Olaszrizling are almost always on basalt.
- **Permian red sandstone** (Balatonfelvidéki Homokkő Formation) — outcropping on the lower southern slopes especially around Révfülöp-Kővágóörs-Balatonrendes; weathered red silty loams, acidic, moderate water storage, historically prized for reds and for late-ripening whites with colour and extract.
- **Triassic dolomite and limestone** — forming the eastern rim of the Káli basin and ridges around Vászoly, Pécsely, Dörgicse. Shallow rendzinas, calcareous, give tight, high-acid, linear Olaszrizling and Riesling styles closely related to those of the neighbouring Balatonfüred-Csopak PDO.
- **Pannonian marl, clay and loess cover** — infilling the basins, often overlying any of the above; provides the deeper, more water-retentive sites; luvisols and brown forest soils dominate.
- **Volcanic tuff and pyroclastics** — the Hegyestű and Tihany peninsula formations, small surfaces but distinctive for single-vineyard wines.

This mosaic produces **intra-district terroir contrasts within a few hundred metres**: basalt vs. dolomite vs. red sandstone can coexist inside a single estate. **Mesoclimatic modifiers** are equally strong:

- **Lake mirror effect** — Balaton reflects ~6–8% of incoming radiation onto south-facing lower slopes, measurably lengthening the useful radiation day in spring and autumn.
- **Thermal buffering** — the lake's ~2 m mean depth gives modest thermal inertia but the very large surface-to-volume ratio means lake water warms and cools faster than a deep lake; surface water temperatures reached >28 °C during the 2022 heatwave, warming by ~0.32 °C/decade over the past 30–40 years (see §9).
- **Nocturnal drainage** — cool katabatic flow from the wooded Bakony at night preserves acidity and aromatic precursors, a factor repeatedly invoked to explain why Balaton-felvidéki Olaszrizling holds acidity better than Egri or southern-Hungarian versions.
- **N–S trending valleys** — channel daytime south-southeasterly breezes off the lake into vineyards, promoting canopy drying (disease suppression) but also increasing evapotranspiration during heatwaves.

## 4. Observed trends — temperature, heatwaves, drought, hail, frost [OBSERVED]

### 4.1 Temperature
- **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Hungary's national mean annual T rose by ~+1.2 °C between 1901 and 2020, with ~0.8 °C of that warming concentrated in the 1981–2020 window; summer warming dominates (OMSZ).
- **[OBSERVED local]** Lake Balaton surface water has warmed by **~2.2 °C over the last ~150 years**, with most of that increase concentrated in the last 30–40 years; rate of recent warming ~**+0.32 °C/decade**. The lake thermal regime is a good proxy for shoreline growing-season air temperature.
- **Heatwave days**: 2012, 2021, 2022, 2023 each saw >15 heatwave days in Hungary (OMSZ/HungaroMet definition). In 2022, a prolonged July–August heatwave saw daytime air temperatures >35 °C along the north shore for extended periods; this heatwave is the single most cited climatic extreme in recent Balaton literature.

### 4.2 Drought
- **2022** was the driest January–July on record in the Balaton catchment — the **worst negative water-balance extreme since 1921** (~100-year record). The lake lost ~300 million m³ to evaporation during summer 2022 alone; water level fell ~70 cm.
- **2024** produced another severe drought-heatwave compound event, with ~75,000 ha of Hungarian agricultural land reported drought-damaged by mid-August 2024 (Euronews/Hungarian government).
- SPEI-based studies of the Carpathian Basin show that the frequency of moderate-to-severe growing-season droughts has approximately **doubled** between 1961–1990 and 1991–2020 in the Transdanubian Midlands, which include Balaton-felvidék.

### 4.3 Hail
- **[DATA GAP]** There is no published district-level hail frequency time series for Balaton-felvidéki. **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Hungarian insurance-industry data (MABISZ) show a clear increase in large-hail (>2 cm) claims on agricultural land since ~2005; the Bakony foothills north of the lake are a recurrent orographic hail corridor. Estate-level reports from 2018, 2020 and 2023 describe heavy hail events affecting localised slopes (e.g. around Köveskál, Mindszentkálla).

### 4.4 Frost
- **[OBSERVED]** The median date of the last spring frost has shifted earlier by ~7–10 days since the 1961–1990 baseline (CarpatClim), but **phenology has advanced even faster than the frost recession** (§5), so **frost-damage risk has not decreased and has in places increased** — a classic "de-coupling" pattern seen across European viticulture.
- **March 2024** brought an unexpected late snow and frost episode that caused localised but not catastrophic damage in Balaton vineyards; it was immediately followed by unusually warm April temperatures and early flowering, foreshadowing an early harvest.
- **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Winter freeze damage (extreme cold kill events) has become rarer — the last major one was winter 2011/2012 for the region.

## 5. Phenology & yield [OBSERVED, HU-AGGREGATE where noted]

- **Budburst**: **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Observed advance of **~7–15 days** for main white cultivars since the 1980s in Transdanubian stations; similar for late-budding Olaszrizling (which naturally buds ~7 days after Chardonnay) but the relative lateness provides partial protection against spring frost, not reduced.
- **Flowering**: advanced by ~10 days.
- **Véraison**: advanced by ~12–18 days for whites; in very hot years (2003, 2015, 2017, 2022) véraison in early-ripening varieties at lake level has been observed in early-to-mid July, ~3 weeks earlier than the 1980s norm.
- **Harvest**: The Balaton-felvidéki Olaszrizling harvest has shifted from a classical late-September–early-October window to a **mid-September start**, with 2022 and 2024 seeing an end-August start for early whites — a ~2–3 week advance vs. the 1961–1990 baseline.
- **Yields**: **[DATA GAP at district level]** National Hungarian wine statistics (HNT) show high inter-annual yield variability increasingly dominated by drought and hail. **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Hungary's average yield has declined from ~60 hl/ha (1990s) toward ~45–55 hl/ha (2010s–2020s), with 2022 reaching a multi-decadal low. The Balaton PDOs tend to fall below the national average in drought years due to shallow soils on basalt/dolomite rendzinas (no subsoil reserve).

## 6. Grape chemistry — sugar, acid, aroma

- **[OBSERVED, HU-AGGREGATE]** Must sugar at harvest for Hungarian whites has risen by ~1.5–2.5 °KMW (Klosterneuburger Mostwaage) per decade since the 1980s; translated: potential alcohol rose from ~11.5–12% vol. typical Olaszrizling to ~13–14% vol., sometimes 14.5%.
- **Titratable acidity** at harvest has fallen by ~1.0–1.5 g/L (as tartaric) over the same period for Olaszrizling at Balaton stations; pH at harvest has risen by ~0.1–0.15 units.
- **Malic acid** — the more heat-labile acid — has fallen disproportionately; this is the direct mechanism behind the "softer, flatter" dry-white style many growers are trying to counteract.
- **Aroma and precursor chemistry**: heat-stressed Olaszrizling loses thiol precursors and produces more C13-norisoprenoids (TDN, β-damascenone). Balaton-felvidéki basalt sites are considered comparatively resilient because of **nocturnal cooling** (§2) that preserves aroma precursors; this is the leading candidate explanation for why Balaton Olaszrizling still shows higher acidity than Eger Olaszrizling from similar baseline climates.
- **Kéknyelű**: its naturally very high acidity and late ripening (mid-October) mean it is still harvestable at balanced pH in most years, though malolactic conversion issues and "hot-vintage bitterness" have been reported in 2017, 2019 and 2022.
- **Pinot Gris (Szürkebarát)**: most affected in drought years — tendency to raisining and premature veraison; sugar can exceed 24 °Brix at pH >3.6 in August.

## 7. Sensory & style — freshness risk, aging potential

- The **classical Balaton-felvidéki Olaszrizling style** is medium-bodied, crisp, with 6.5–8.0 g/L titratable acidity, a distinctive "bitter almond" finish, subtle smoky/stony notes on basalt and a clear mineral backbone on dolomite. **Freshness is the commercial hallmark** — the cue tourists and domestic buyers use to distinguish it from flatter Alföld or Eger versions.
- **Freshness risk is the central climate-quality concern**. The combined effects of earlier harvest, higher sugar, and lower malic acid push the style toward fuller-bodied, lower-tension wines. Producers respond by (i) harvesting earlier and accepting lower sugar, (ii) co-fermenting or blending dolomite and basalt parcels, (iii) using partial skin contact and amphora/large oak to add texture and reduce apparent heat.
- **Aging potential**: on dolomite/limestone substrates (Csopak-style), well-made dry Olaszrizling can age 10–15 years, gaining honey, toast and hazelnut notes while keeping structure. Climate change ambivalently affects this: higher extract gives more matter to age, but lower acid and higher pH increase microbial instability and oxidative ageing risk.
- **Kéknyelű**: the return from the brink (46 ha total; Hungarikum status since 2025) is partly a **climate-opportunity story**: its very late ripening that made it marginal in cool decades is now well matched to the warmer growing season, so the old "gentleman's grape" is finally ripening reliably.
- **Reds**: rising plantings of Pinot Noir, Kékfrankos and even Syrah on red-sandstone slopes — all a direct adaptive response to warmer summers.

## 8. Biotic stressors — mildews, botrytis, invasive pests, lake humidity

- **Downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*)**: the dominant mildew threat in the Balaton-felvidék, because the lake mesoclimate maintains **elevated relative humidity and dew formation** on lower slopes. **[HU-AGGREGATE]** Experimental evidence shows DM infection severity increases under combined elevated CO₂ and warmer conditions; longer growing seasons and more frequent convective rainfall both extend the risk window. Growers around Balaton report more erratic rather than simply more severe disease pressure.
- **Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*)**: thrives under dry, warm conditions, so its relative importance is rising in drought-heatwave years (2017, 2022, 2024). Balaton-felvidéki basalt buttes, with strong daily thermal amplitude, are considered comparatively exposed.
- **Botrytis (*Botrytis cinerea*)**: in a paradox classic to warming viticulture, bunch rot risk has **shifted earlier** (véraison in early August) and now often coincides with July–August thunderstorms, reducing the "clean late-harvest" window. Unlike Tokaj, noble rot is not the product signature here, so all botrytis is treated as grey-rot damage.
- **Black rot, phomopsis, esca trunk disease**: esca is the fastest-rising trunk-disease concern in Hungarian literature; no district-specific Balaton-felvidéki survey published [DATA GAP].
- **Spotted-wing drosophila (*Drosophila suzukii*)**: first recorded in Hungary in **2012**, widespread outbreak by **2014**; now established across Transdanubia including the Balaton region. Its optimal activity at ~20 °C and preference for softened, late-ripening fruit make Balaton-felvidéki red and thin-skinned white varieties (Pinot Noir, Pinot Gris, Kéknyelű) particularly exposed; it is a growing driver of earlier harvest decisions.
- **Other emerging pests**: *Scaphoideus titanus* (vector of Flavescence dorée) is an expanding concern in the broader Transdanubian region; European grapevine moth (*Lobesia botrana*) generations per year are projected to increase under warming.
- **Lake-humidity effect** (distinctive to Balaton): dew persistence on lower vineyards is longer than on equivalent altitudes elsewhere in Hungary; this historically favoured basalt-top sites for premium wine (clean, drier) and dolomite-valley sites for everyday wine.

## 9. Abiotic extremes [OBSERVED]

- **Compound drought-heatwave events**: 2022 and 2024 are the benchmark years. Lake surface temperatures >28 °C during the 2022 heatwave; evaporation stripped ~220 mm more moisture from the lake than inflow could replace; water level dropped ~70 cm. These numbers are best viewed as **proxies for vineyard evapotranspiration stress**, because on basalt/dolomite rendzinas there is no deep water reserve to draw on.
- **Extreme convective rainfall**: OMSZ reports statistically significant increases in the frequency of ≥20 mm daily precipitation events over western Transdanubia; under RCP8.5 these days may exceed 5/year by 2071–2100.
- **Wind damage / derecho**: occasional, not a primary concern.
- **Wildfire**: increasing ignition risk in the wooded Bakony foothills adjacent to vineyards; no direct vineyard loss reported to date [DATA GAP].
- **Forest-vineyard interface**: heat-stressed forest (2022 compound event caused detectable forest damage in Bakony monitoring sites) affects cold-air drainage and katabatic cooling of upper-slope vineyards — a second-order feedback with no published quantification yet.

## 10. Indirect effects — Balaton water budget, erosion, irrigation, tourism

- **Lake water budget**: the lake's water balance is governed by (i) precipitation on the lake (~45% of input), (ii) runoff from the catchment, chiefly via the Zala (~50%), (iii) evaporation (~75% of loss), and (iv) outflow via the Sió sluice. Climate projections (Honti; Lake Balaton hydrology and climate change — ResearchGate 2020) indicate a drastic drop in inflow by mid-century combined with increased evaporation; years without outflow and serious water-level drops become more probable by an order of magnitude. This matters for the vineyards even though the lake itself does not irrigate them — it is the **reputational and micro-climatic asset** on which the whole borvidék brand rests.
- **Erosion**: water erosion measurement on the Balaton Upland is an active research theme; the combination of shallow rendzinas, steep basalt cones, and more intense convective rainfall under warming is a classic erosion-amplification setup. No published district-specific erosion-rate time series [DATA GAP].
- **Irrigation**: historically rare; Hungarian vineyards are overwhelmingly rainfed. Drought stress 2022 and 2024 has triggered the first serious discussion of irrigation permits for Hungarian wine, but on basalt and dolomite rendzinas the water-holding capacity is so low that deficit-irrigation strategies require new infrastructure (reservoirs, drip) that does not currently exist at scale in Balaton-felvidék.
- **Tourism linkage**: Balaton tourism and wine tourism are deeply coupled. A lower, warmer, algae-prone lake directly threatens the summer tourism base — and hence the local market for cellar-door sales and wine experiences that sustain small producers. Sparkling-wine festivals, Borbarangolás (Badacsony), Kékszalag regatta, and Lake Balaton Wine Week are key revenue events whose future depends on both the lake and favourable summer weather.

## 11. Future projections — EURO-CORDEX and wine-region studies [PROJECTED]

### 11.1 Lakatos & Nagy 2025 (*Frontiers in Plant Science*) — the key paper
The reference study for Hungarian wine viticulture under climate change uses the **FORESEE 4.2** bias-adjusted ensemble of 14 CMIP5 / EURO-CORDEX climate models under RCP4.5 and RCP8.5, evaluating four temperature indices (AGST, GDD/Winkler, Huglin Index, BEDD) across all **22 Hungarian PDOs** over 1971–2100 relative to a 1971–2000 reference.

**Headline Hungary-wide findings (apply to Balaton-felvidék as one of the 22):**
- **AGST (Average Growing Season Temperature) increase** ranging from **+1.0 °C (most optimistic model, RCP4.5)** to **>+4.0 °C (hot-end RCP8.5)** over seven decades.
- **Viticultural-class transition**: Hungarian wine regions predominantly **"warm"** in the 1971–2000 reference → projected to become **"hot"** by the end of the 21st century under AGST classification.
- **GDD-Winkler**: historical **"intermediate"** (Region Ib–II) → **"warm"** (Region III) in the near future → **predominantly "hot"** (Region IV–V) by end of century.
- **Northern wine regions (including the Balaton north shore) warm faster than southern regions** in the Hungarian ensemble — a counter-intuitive but robust result, attributed to continental-latitude amplification.
- Hungary is still projected to remain among the regions with "good quality grapevine growing conditions", but **variety and cultivation structure must change**.

### 11.2 EURO-CORDEX projections for Transdanubia / Balaton grid cells
- **Temperature**: multi-model ensemble +1.5–2.5 °C by 2021–2050, +3.0–4.5 °C by 2071–2100 under RCP8.5; greatest warming in **summer** (June–August), with heatwave days potentially reaching **40–50/year** in the warmer part of the ensemble.
- **Precipitation**: small changes in annual totals for Transdanubia — 2021–2050 ensemble shows **–2 to –4% in central Transdanubia** (including Balaton-felvidék) under some bias-corrected products, modest increases elsewhere. End of century under RCP8.5: lowland annual precipitation +10% on average but with strong seasonal redistribution — **summers drier, winters wetter** — and more days with ≥20 mm precipitation.
- **Drought**: the frequency and intensity of meteorological and agricultural droughts (SPEI-6, SPI-12) increase substantially across Transdanubia, with late-summer water deficits projected to dominate growing-season stress.

### 11.3 Earlier studies
- Szabó & Pongrácz (RegCM4.3, 2018): Hungarian warming greatest in summer, all four seasons affected.
- Gaál et al. (Random Forest modelling of Hungarian wine regions): shifting suitability envelopes already by mid-century, Balaton region retained good general suitability but with variety re-allocation needed.

### 11.4 Implications for Balaton-felvidék
Under RCP8.5 mid-to-late century the district is projected to cross into **Winkler Region IV ("hot")** — comparable to today's Apulia or southern Rhône — at lakeshore elevations. Upper slopes and the Káli basin stay slightly cooler because of nocturnal drainage but also move up one class. Olaszrizling remains physiologically viable (it is tolerant of heat), but its classical crisp style becomes increasingly difficult below ~250 m. Kéknyelű, Furmint, Hárslevelű, and late-ripening Riesling clones become the natural "future-proofing" set for premium wine.

## 12. Adaptation

**Varietal and clonal strategies**
- Re-emphasis of **late-ripening indigenous varieties**: Kéknyelű (lake comeback, now Hungarikum), Furmint, Hárslevelű — all late-budding, late-ripening, high-acid varieties whose "handicap" under the 20th-century climate becomes an asset.
- Selection of **later-ripening clones** of Olaszrizling and Rajnai rizling.
- Evaluation of **alternative varieties** adapted to warmer climates (Assyrtiko trial plantings in Hungary, PIWI varieties such as Souvignier Gris for disease resistance).

**Canopy and vineyard management**
- Higher training (raising fruiting zone from classical ~70 cm toward ~100–110 cm) to reduce reflected heat from the soil and from Balaton's shoreline.
- Increased leaf-area retention on the eastern (morning-sun) side; delayed leaf removal to shade clusters.
- Later pruning to delay budburst and reduce frost exposure in spring (the phenology-frost de-coupling problem).
- Cover cropping for water conservation, erosion control, soil carbon.

**Soil and water**
- Pilot **deficit-irrigation** projects using Balaton water or reservoirs — still in early discussion.
- Mulching with basalt gravel or straw to reduce evaporation on shallow rendzinas.
- Terracing restoration on steep basalt cones to reduce runoff-driven erosion.

**Winemaking**
- **Earlier harvest and phased picking** by parcel and aspect — already standard practice among the top Balaton-felvidék estates since ~2015.
- Acidification (tartaric addition) now routinely permitted for Hungarian whites in hot vintages.
- Lower fermentation temperatures, partial whole-bunch pressing, use of **concrete eggs and large old oak** to preserve freshness perception without stripping texture.
- Style experimentation: orange wine, skin-contact Olaszrizling, traditional-method sparkling base from earlier picks.

**Boundary and land-use adaptation**
- Lakatos & Nagy 2025 explicitly recommend **modifying PDO borders and the land-use structure within them** — a politically sensitive but scientifically supported option.
- Recovery of abandoned vineyard terraces on cooler north- and east-facing slopes of the Káli basin.

## 13. Economic & market — wine tourism, Balaton brand, PDO, export

- **Market structure**: the Balaton borrégió (six PDOs) accounts for approximately **one third of Hungarian sparkling-wine output** and a substantial share of the premium dry-white market. The Balaton-felvidéki PDO itself is smaller in absolute volume than Balatonfüred-Csopak or Badacsony but disproportionately prestigious thanks to Káli basin producers.
- **Export**: modest in absolute terms; principal export markets are Germany, Austria, the UK, Scandinavia and Japan. The dominant revenue channel remains **domestic wine tourism** around the lake.
- **Brand identity** is inseparable from Lake Balaton as a geographic and cultural object. The climate-change threat to the lake (lower water levels, algal blooms, warmer water) is therefore simultaneously a **brand and a production threat**.
- **PDO regulation**: rigid cultivar lists restrict rapid varietal adaptation. The six Balaton PDOs have begun revising allowed varieties, but the process is slow.
- **Tourism-climate coupling**: heatwaves drive record summer tourism in the short term (more visitors, longer season) but threaten the long-term product (lake ecology, green landscape, reliable vintages). Wine-tourism researchers (2025 studies on Hungarian organic wineries) emphasise growing concern among producers about vintage variability and visitor-season shifts.
- **Kéknyelű Hungarikum status (2025)** gives the variety new marketing weight — a climate-adaptation story that doubles as a heritage narrative, which is commercially valuable.

## 14. Knowledge gaps

1. **District-level gridded climate normals** for Balaton-felvidéki (distinct from the broader north-shore average) are not publicly available; §3 values are assembled from OMSZ national grids.
2. **No published district-specific phenology time series** for Olaszrizling or Kéknyelű; estate records exist but are not in the literature. §5 relies on HU-aggregate data.
3. **No long-term must-composition database** linking Olaszrizling sugar/acid to climate covariates at Balaton-felvidéki level. §6 uses national and Transdanubian averages.
4. **Hail frequency time series** at the district level are missing — a critical omission given the Bakony orographic hail corridor.
5. **Soil-erosion rates** on basalt and dolomite rendzinas under changing convective-rainfall regimes: methodology exists (Balaton Uplands erosion mapping) but no district-wide trend analysis.
6. **Pest-disease Hungarian-field data**: Hungary-specific climate-disease coupling studies for DM/PM/esca/SWD on vines are scarce; most evidence is extrapolated from Italian, Austrian and broad European sources.
7. **Vineyard microclimate modelling with lake coupling**: the single most distinctive terroir factor of the district — the lake's mesoclimatic effect — has not been explicitly represented in any published wine-climate study for Balaton-felvidéki.
8. **Adaptation-strategy efficacy**: no published controlled trials of canopy management, irrigation, or variety substitution specifically in Balaton-felvidék conditions.
9. **Socioeconomic impact modelling**: wine-tourism coupling to lake water quality and heat-wave frequency is invoked qualitatively but not yet modelled quantitatively.
10. **Frost risk under phenology-frost de-coupling**: the direction of change is debated; no local-scale validation for Balaton-felvidék.
11. **Regional downscaling**: Balaton-felvidék's lake mesoclimate is finer than standard EURO-CORDEX (~12 km) grid resolution; bespoke downscaling or large-eddy simulation work is needed but has not been published.
12. The flagship **Lakatos & Nagy 2025** study presents only aggregated Hungary-wide visualisations — per-PDO numerical tables would be the single most useful data product for planning.
