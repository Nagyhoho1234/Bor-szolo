# Csongrádi Wine District — Climate Change Impact Synthesis

**Region (borrégió):** Duna (Great Hungarian Plain)
**District (borvidék):** Csongrádi (Csongrád)
**Status:** PDO / OEM "Csongrádi" — smallest of the three Duna-region wine districts
**Synthesis date:** 2026-04-07

> **Reading guide.** Each section distinguishes **[OBSERVED]** from **[PROJECTED]**. Csongrád is one of the **thinnest** Hungarian wine districts in the primary literature — most published assessments aggregate it with Kunság and Hajós-Baja as "Alföldi" or "Duna region". Where only Hungary-wide or Alföld-wide numbers exist I label them **[HU-AGGREGATE]** or **[ALFÖLD-AGGREGATE]**. **This is also the one Hungarian district where the published climate signal is unambiguously existential**: Lakatos & Nagy (2025, *Frontiers in Plant Science*) explicitly single it out as the **fastest-warming** of all 22 Hungarian wine regions under RCP 8.5. Gaps and contested findings are flagged inline.

## 1. District profile

The Csongrádi borvidék occupies a small slice of the **southern Great Hungarian Plain (Alföld)** on the right bank of the lower **Tisza River**, immediately north, west and south of **Szeged**, hard against the Serbian and Romanian borders. It is the **smallest and least-known** of the three wine districts that make up the Duna wine region (the other two being Kunság and Hajós-Baja).

- **Administrative delimitation:** The PDO zone covers ~**14,311 ha of delimited land** (2,595 ha Class I + 8,474 ha Class II), but **planted vineyard area is only ~2,000–2,600 ha** — commonly cited as **~2,022 ha actually under vines**, with a wine-industry shorthand figure of **"~1,500 ha" bottled/commercial**. The gap between delimited and planted is enormous and has been widening since the 1990s as marginal sand parcels are abandoned.
- **Administrative zones (4):**
  - **a) Csongrád zone** — the town of Csongrád itself (north of Szeged, on the Tisza).
  - **b) Kistelek zone** — Balástya, Csengele, Kistelek, Pusztaszer.
  - **c) Pusztamérges zone** — Forráskút, Öttömös, Pusztamérges, Ruzsa, Üllés (the western, sandiest part, on the Homokhátság).
  - **d) Mórahalom zone** — Ásotthalom, Bordány, Domaszék, Kiskundorozsma, Mórahalom, Zákányszék (immediately west of Szeged).
- **Flagship varieties (historically and in the current PDO dossier):**
  - **White (≈55–60% of plantings):** **Olaszrizling** (Welschriesling), **Kövidinka**, **Rajnai rizling** (Riesling), **Kunleány**, **Chardonnay**, **Sauvignon Blanc**, **Cserszegi fűszeres**, **Ezerjó**, **Irsai Olivér**, **Tramini**.
  - **Red (≈40–45%):** **Kékfrankos** (Blaufränkisch) and **Zweigelt** are the current workhorses; **Kadarka** is the historical flagship (marketed as "Csongrádi Kadarka") but area has collapsed to a token level; minor **Cabernet Sauvignon**, **Cabernet Franc**, **Merlot**, **Pinot Noir**.
  - The Csongrád profile historically emphasised **"Pusztamérgesi Olaszrizling", "Csongrádi Kadarka", "Csongrádi Kékfrankos" and "Csongrádi Cabernet"** as its branded wine types.
- **Producer structure:** Strongly **smallholder and family**-dominated. Most producers sell grapes or bulk wine into Kunság and Szekszárd co-operatives; **"very few wine producers in Csongrád actually bottle their wine"** is the standard industry characterisation. No nationally-famous estate winery brand has emerged; the district is arguably the **lowest-visibility PDO in Hungary**.
- **Wine style signature:** Light, fresh whites and soft, **low-acid, medium-to-high-alcohol** reds — a direct consequence of the hot, sun-drenched, nutrient-poor sandy terroir.

## 2. Terroir & soil deep-dive

Csongrád is **the purest "sand viticulture" district in Hungary** together with Kunság. Its existence is a direct historical consequence of the **phylloxera crisis**: the louse (*Daktulosphaira vitifoliae*) **cannot survive in loose, silica-rich sand**, so after phylloxera devastated Hungary's hillside vineyards in the 1870s–1880s, viticulture was deliberately expanded onto the previously marginal sandy plains of the Alföld as **both a phylloxera refuge and a sand-dune-stabilisation tool**.

- **Parent material / sub-stratum:**
  - **Calcareous aeolian sand** — wind-blown Pleistocene sand of Danube origin, often with **loess admixture**, dominates the Pusztamérges and Mórahalom zones on the western **Homokhátság** ridge (the Danube–Tisza sand ridge).
  - **Acid sand and alluvial "black earth" of Tisza origin**, locally without calcareous component, dominates the Csongrád-town zone on the right bank of the Tisza floodplain.
  - **Riverine alluvium** (silt, clayey-silt) in the immediate Tisza flood terraces.
- **Topography:** **Flat** — this is the defining feature. Vineyards sit at **~80–130 m a.s.l.**, with no meaningful slopes, no aspect variation, and essentially **zero mesoclimate differentiation** beyond the coarse east (Tisza-alluvial) / west (Homokhátság aeolian sand) divide. There are no "dűlők" in the Tokaj/Eger sense.
- **Soil properties and viticultural behaviour:**
  - **Very low organic matter** (<1–1.5% in most Homokhátság profiles), **very low water-holding capacity** (~60–90 mm plant-available water in the top 1 m), **very low CEC**, low native nutrient status.
  - **High surface albedo and very rapid daytime heating** — summer surface temperatures on bare sand routinely exceed 50 °C.
  - **Night-time radiative cooling is strong** (continental signal), but mitigated in recent decades by rising Tmin (see §4).
  - **Frost pockets** on the flat plain are real — cold air drains off the Homokhátság ridge into the Tisza depressions and settles, so spring frost risk in low-lying parcels is historically higher than on hill districts.
- **Phylloxera legacy:** A non-trivial share of Csongrád vineyards are **historically own-rooted** (*franc de pied*), specifically because sand is phylloxera-proof. This is one of only a handful of places in Central Europe where this is still true, and is a potential heritage asset if the region survives the climate squeeze.
- **Critical vulnerability:** The same low water-holding capacity that forced vines to develop deep roots on pre-phylloxera sand is now **the single biggest climate-adaptation liability**: sandy soils buffer heat and drought worse than any other Hungarian terroir.

## 3. Baseline climate

**Köppen class:** **Dfb/Cfb boundary** in the Trewartha scheme, but Csongrád is widely described by Hungarian climatologists as the **most "continental-extreme" corner of Hungary**, approaching a semi-arid (BSk) signal in the driest recent summers on the Homokhátság.

| Variable | Value | Period / Source |
|---|---|---|
| Mean annual T | **~11.2–11.8 °C** (Szeged long-term, climb of ~2 °C since 1979) | OMSZ / Szeged station |
| Growing-season mean T (Apr–Oct) | **~18.0–18.5 °C** — the **warmest growing-season mean of any Hungarian wine district** | ALFÖLD-AGGREGATE, Kovács / Lakatos 2025 |
| Mean Tmax July | **~28–29 °C** with >25 summer days (>30 °C) annually climbing to **>40 days** in the 2010s | OMSZ Szeged |
| Annual precipitation | **~500–560 mm** — among the **lowest** in Hungary; the Homokhátság core is at ~500 mm | OMSZ / ELTE studies |
| Precipitation deficit vs. viticultural need | **~100 mm below long-term safe threshold** for rain-fed viticulture | Vinotravel / Wines of Hungary |
| Annual sunshine hours | **~2,100–2,150 h** — the **sunniest district in Hungary** | OMSZ |
| Absolute record high (Szeged) | **40.1 °C, July 2022** (station record since 1949) | Szeged weather station / Meteoblue |
| Absolute record high (nearby) | **41.9 °C, Kiskunhalas 2007** (national all-time record until 2024) | plantmaps.com |
| Frost risk | Moderate-to-high on low-lying flats (cold-air pooling); spring frost events documented 2011, 2017, 2020, 2024 | OMSZ / industry reports |

**Baseline viticultural indices (Csongrád-specific, 1971–2000 reference):**
- **Winkler GDD:** **Region III–IV** ("warm" to "hot") — already the warmest Winkler class in Hungary. Estimates from FORESEE/EURO-CORDEX put the 1971–2000 mean at roughly **1,850–2,000 GDD**. [ALFÖLD-AGGREGATE]
- **Huglin Index:** baseline ~**2,200–2,400** ("temperate-warm" to "warm" class) — the upper end of any Hungarian district. [ALFÖLD-AGGREGATE]
- **BEDD:** baseline ~**1,400–1,550**. [ALFÖLD-AGGREGATE]
- **AGST (average growing-season temperature):** **~17.5–18.0 °C** for 1971–2000 — already above the classical Jones (2006) "temperate" / "warm" threshold for high-quality wine at baseline.

**Key takeaway:** Csongrád starts from the **hottest, driest, flattest, poorest-soil baseline** of any Hungarian wine district. Every incremental degree of warming pushes it further out of the "quality wine" envelope than any other Hungarian region — there is no orographic buffering, no elevation escape, and no high-capacity soil reservoir.

## 4. Observed climate trends

**[OBSERVED — HIGH CONFIDENCE]** Csongrád sits in the corner of Hungary that has warmed the most since 1980, both in observed station data and in the bias-corrected EURO-CORDEX reanalyses.

- **Annual mean T:** +**~2.1 °C** at the Szeged station from the late-1970s baseline (~10.5 °C) to the early 2020s (~12.6 °C), **over less than 46 years**. This is the fastest warming rate recorded at any major Hungarian long-record station.
- **2024 was the warmest year on record in Hungary** at a country-mean 12.91 °C (+2.1 °C above the 1991–2020 normal) — in Csongrád-Csanád county, the anomaly was above the national mean.
- **Growing-season mean T:** In Kovács / Lakatos & Nagy (2025), the Csongrád wine region is **explicitly flagged** as having "the most significant increase in AGST ... over 2.5 °C" by end-century under RCP 8.5 — the largest projected warming of the 22 Hungarian wine regions studied. Applied to the 1971–2000 baseline of ~17.5–18.0 °C, this places the end-century Csongrád AGST at **~20.0–20.5 °C**, approaching the upper limit of the Jones (2006) "hot" class and beyond the established range for any fine-wine producing region in Europe.
- **Heat extremes:** Szeged recorded its station-high **40.1 °C** in July 2022; August 2024 brought the regional record of **40.7 °C at Kelebia** (just west of the district) and a county-wide red-level heatwave alert. Tropical nights (Tmin >20 °C) have become routine in Szeged — during the August 2024 heatwave overnight lows stayed at 27 °C.
- **Summer drought frequency:** On the Great Hungarian Plain, the most severe drought events of the past 30 years — **1992, 2003, 2012, 2022, 2024** — are clustered in the last 12 years. The 2022 drought hit Csongrád-Csanád county **hardest** (alongside Bács-Kiskun, Békés, Jász-Nagykun-Szolnok, Szabolcs-Szatmár-Bereg).
- **Precipitation:** No significant long-term annual-total trend in OMSZ data (the signal is dominated by interannual variance), but the **summer share has fallen** and **consecutive dry days** have risen — this is the classic "same mean, worse distribution" signature, and it is especially punishing on sand.

**Ranking within Hungary:** Csongrád is **#1 in warming rate** and **#1 in absolute growing-season temperature** among the 22 Hungarian wine districts. No other district is close on both axes — Kunság and Hajós-Baja are similar in absolute terms but the published literature singles Csongrád out.

## 5. Phenology & yield

**[OBSERVED — MEDIUM CONFIDENCE]** Csongrád-specific phenology papers are scarce, but the aggregated Alföld signal is clear:

- **Budburst** has advanced ~**8–12 days** since 1980; **flowering** ~**10 days earlier**; **veraison** ~**14 days earlier**; **harvest** **2–3 weeks earlier** in the 2020s than in the 1990s. Harvests in 2024 and 2025 across the Great Plain began in early-to-mid August for early whites.
- **Frost-risk window:** Because budburst has advanced faster than last-spring-frost date, the **net spring-frost risk has not declined** despite warmer springs. Frost damage was reported across Hungarian regions in 2020 and 2024; in 2024, frost **plus** drought jointly produced **"the weakest grape harvest in recent years"** nationally, with up to **one-third less fruit on Great Plain vines**.
- **Yield trend:** Long-term Alföld yields have declined because of vineyard abandonment (area effect) *and* because of yield-per-hectare losses in drought years. The 2022 drought produced 100% yield loss on many autumn crops in eastern Hungary; Csongrád wine yields were **severely reduced** (no district-specific figure published, but the Duna-region wine statistics show a step-down 2021→2022).
- **2025 harvest** (the most recent with full data): Hungary-wide yields were **low, prices up**, broadly consistent with a continuation of the 2022/2024 shock.

**Gap:** There is **no published Csongrád-specific phenology time series**. The district is too small and fragmented to have a research-station-grade phenological network.

## 6. Grape chemistry

**[OBSERVED — MEDIUM CONFIDENCE, extrapolated from Alföld and Eger analogues]**

The chemical fingerprint of Csongrád wines has always been **high sugar / high potential alcohol / low acidity / low pH buffer** because the district starts in Winkler III–IV and sits on low-CEC sand. Climate warming pushes every one of these signals further in the same direction:

- **Sugar accumulation / potential alcohol:** Ripe Csongrád Kadarka, Kékfrankos, Olaszrizling now routinely reach **13.5–14.5% vol potential alcohol** even without extended hang-time. Lakatos et al. (2023, Eger region analogue) modelled **sugar content rising to 2100** under RCP 8.5 such that adaptation (variety shift or earlier picking) becomes **essential** — the Csongrád signal is stronger than Eger because of its hotter baseline.
- **Titratable acidity:** Collapse of malic acid under heat is the dominant process. Csongrád wines are already characterised in the trade as **"mild and low in acidity, with some showing residual sweetness"** — the "soft Alföld red" style. Further warming locks this in and increasingly demands cellar-side acidification (authorised under Hungarian/EU wine law but commercially disliked).
- **pH:** Rising with falling TA; already ~3.6–3.9 in typical Csongrád reds at harvest, trending upward. High pH destabilises colour (anthocyanin equilibria), promotes microbial spoilage, and reduces SO₂ efficacy.
- **Anthocyanins and colour:** Heat above ~30–35 °C inhibits anthocyanin biosynthesis in Kadarka and Kékfrankos. Repeated post-veraison heatwaves (2022, 2024) produce **pale, thin-coloured reds** on sandy Csongrád sites — a known and longstanding criticism of Alföld reds that is getting worse.
- **Aroma precursors:** Terpenes and thiols (Cserszegi fűszeres, Sauvignon Blanc, Irsai Olivér, Tramini) are **heat-labile**; earlier harvests preserve freshness but at the cost of incomplete phenolic ripeness in reds.
- **Sugar–phenolic decoupling:** The central problem — sugar reaches harvest targets 2–3 weeks before tannins and anthocyanins are ripe. On sand, with no deep water buffer, waiting for phenolic ripeness costs further acidity and pushes potential alcohol above 15% vol.

## 7. Sensory & style — the viability question

Csongrád's historical style is **fresh, simple, gulpable** whites (Olaszrizling, Kövidinka, Cserszegi fűszeres) and **soft, light, fruity** reds (Kékfrankos, Kadarka, Zweigelt) — everyday "jó bor", not fine wine. The district was never positioned as a premium terroir; its economic role was **bulk volume** for the Hungarian domestic market and for blending into larger Alföld and Szekszárd brands.

- **Sensory drift already visible (2015–2025):**
  - Reds are **softer, flatter, more raisined**, with **reduced colour** and frequent "cooked-fruit" characters after August heatwaves.
  - Whites are **less aromatic**, **lower in acidity**, and increasingly need cellar-side acidification or pre-veraison leaf-canopy management to retain freshness.
  - Alcohols have climbed from typical 11.5–12.5% vol (1990s) to 13.5–14.5% vol (2020s), pushing wines out of the "light summer quaffer" category that was the region's niche.
- **The existential question:** Of all Hungarian wine districts, Csongrád is the **one where the literature openly asks whether quality viticulture remains viable at all** under RCP 8.5 trajectories. Unlike Eger, Tokaj, Mátra or Sopron (where elevation, orography, or latitude offer partial buffering), Csongrád has **no escape routes**:
  - No elevation to migrate to (flat plain).
  - No cooler aspect to migrate to (no slopes).
  - No water reserve in the soil (sand).
  - No irrigation infrastructure (see §10).
  - No premium brand equity to subsidise high-cost adaptation.
- **Industry-consensus framing:** The most plausible 2050 Csongrád scenario is **style transformation from "Central European light reds and fresh whites" to "warm-climate Mediterranean-analogue wines"** — effectively, a Puglia-like or La Mancha-like profile, but without Mediterranean irrigation rights or mature Mediterranean varietal experience.
- **Brand positioning:** The PDO brand **"Csongrádi"** has never achieved national recognition; the district risks being the first Hungarian PDO to face **de facto abandonment** of its historical varietal and stylistic identity.

## 8. Biotic stressors

**[OBSERVED / PROJECTED — LOW-MEDIUM confidence, no district-specific pest time series]**

- **Downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*) and powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*):** Historically **lower pressure** than in the wetter hill districts, because baseline summers are dry. However, **heavier convective summer rain events** (when they occur in a drought year) still trigger severe outbreaks, and because Csongrád sprays less intensively on average, plot-level damage can be large when a rain spike hits.
- **Grape moth (*Lobesia botrana*):** Multi-voltine cycles. Warmer late summers are already allowing a **third full generation** in the southern Great Plain — a pattern first documented in the Balkans and propagating northward. Csongrád-Csanád is one of the first Hungarian areas where a solid third generation is now expected.
- **Phylloxera:** **Historically absent** because of sand (see §2). This remains true and is Csongrád's one natural pest advantage — a potential selling point for own-rooted heritage plantings of Kadarka etc.
- **Flavescence dorée / "Grapevine yellows":** The vector *Scaphoideus titanus* is expanding northward through the Balkans; the Csongrád-Szeged corridor is an at-risk entry zone. No confirmed Csongrád outbreak to date, but the vector is within ~100 km in Serbia and Romania.
- **Esca / trunk diseases:** Drought-stress predisposes vines to trunk-disease expression. Old, own-rooted, water-stressed Csongrád blocks are **particularly exposed**; anecdotal reports of rising vine mortality in aging blocks match Hungarian-wide trends.
- **Sunburn / heat damage to berries:** Direct radiation on exposed clusters in July/August is now the dominant mechanical damage on the open, flat sandy vineyards — more like Spanish bush-vine conditions than Central European canopy culture.

**Gap:** No Csongrád-specific published pest pressure time series. The district is too small to have independent IPM monitoring data beyond generic Alföld-level reporting.

## 9. Abiotic extremes — drought is the defining stressor

**[OBSERVED — HIGH CONFIDENCE]** Drought is **the** defining abiotic stressor of the Csongrád wine district. No other Hungarian district is as vulnerable on all three dimensions (climatic deficit, soil buffer, management capacity).

- **Climatic deficit:** Annual precipitation ~100 mm short of the long-term viticultural safe threshold; summer precipitation declining as a share of the annual total; consecutive-dry-day lengths increasing.
- **Soil buffer:** Low-CEC, low-OM sand holds the least plant-available water of any Hungarian wine terroir — ~60–90 mm in the top metre versus ~150–250 mm on Tokaj/Eger/Villány clays and loess.
- **Management capacity:** Almost **no irrigation infrastructure**. Csongrád-Csanád county has been classified as a **"forward planner"** region in national drought policy — high vulnerability, but with a policy response in progress. The practical reality on the ground is that most Csongrád vineyards are still **rain-fed** because water abstraction rights, cost and groundwater drawdown (see §10) all restrict new irrigation.
- **Catastrophic recent drought years:**
  - **2022:** A 7-week rainless period starting mid-June produced the **worst Alföld drought since records began**; Csongrád-Csanád county was one of the five worst-hit counties. Autumn-harvest crops saw near-100% losses in many locations. Wine-grape yields were heavily down (no district-specific number published).
  - **2024:** Drought plus August heatwave (Szeged Tmin 27 °C; Kelebia 40.7 °C); **frost earlier in the season compounded losses**. National yield cuts of 10–30% reported across Hungarian districts; Great Plain losses up to one-third.
  - **2025:** Continued low yields, price inflation, weak cellar stocks.
- **Great Hungarian Plain drought projection:** Under RCP 8.5, the average spatial fraction of **heavy drought** is projected to rise from **6% (2001–2020) to 17% (2081–2100)** — a near-tripling of the chronic-heavy-drought footprint across the entire Alföld, with Csongrád-Csanád at the core.
- **Hail:** Occasional but not the dominant signal. Flat sandy plains are not the preferred hail track — this is one of the few "risk" axes on which Csongrád is **not** in the worst Hungarian quintile.

## 10. Indirect effects — soil, water, land-use

**[OBSERVED — HIGH CONFIDENCE]**

- **Homokhátság desertification:** The Danube–Tisza sand ridge (which covers the Pusztamérges and Mórahalom zones of Csongrád) is now characterised by multiple Hungarian researchers as **semi-arid**, with "wide cracks in the soil and growing sand dunes more reminiscent of the Sahara Desert than Central Europe". Researchers cite "the combined effect of climatic changes, improper land use and inappropriate environmental management" as causes; the paper calls the phenomenon **unique in this part of the continent**.
- **Groundwater decline:** The regional water table beneath the Homokhátság has been in **accelerating retreat** for decades — documented groundwater-level drops of several metres since the 1970s, with dried-out wells across the plain. This is the direct result of a **combination** of drainage infrastructure (historical canalisation), river regulation (the Danube and Tisza cut off from their floodplains), high evapotranspiration, and declining recharge.
- **No flood recharge:** Fields that in previous centuries were regularly flooded by the Danube and Tisza have, through regulation, become nearly unsuitable for rainfed crops — and the aquifer recharge cycle they sustained is broken.
- **2024 Eötvös Loránd University finding:** Unusually dry layers of surface-level air over the region prevent arriving storm fronts from producing precipitation; fronts pass through without rain and result in **high winds that dry the topsoil further** — a self-reinforcing aridification loop distinctive to the Homokhátság.
- **Irrigation infeasibility:** On sand with a falling water table, drip irrigation is technically possible but:
  - Groundwater abstraction rights are increasingly restricted under the new national "drought task force" framework.
  - Sandy soils leach nitrogen and require higher-frequency fertigation, raising costs.
  - Wine-grape prices in Csongrád are too low to amortise installation.
- **"Water guardian" farmer movements:** Local Csongrád-region farmers have begun experimenting with wetland restoration, swales and even **reuse of thermal-spa wastewater** to re-wet Homokhátság soils (reported December 2025 by AP/Euronews). These are pilot projects; no wine-sector scale-up yet.
- **Soil organic-matter decline:** The combination of heat, drought, low OM input and wind erosion means topsoil organic matter on Csongrád sand is the **lowest in Hungarian viticulture** and is still declining.

## 11. Future projections — worst-case district

**[PROJECTED — HIGH CONFIDENCE for direction, MEDIUM for magnitude]**

Csongrád is the **worst-case Hungarian wine district under RCP 8.5 in the Lakatos & Nagy (2025) assessment**, which is the best single published source on Hungarian wine-region climate futures.

- **End-century (2071–2100) AGST warming, RCP 8.5:** Csongrád **>2.5 °C** — **the largest of all 22 Hungarian wine regions**. (The pessimistic-ensemble Hungary-wide maximum exceeds +4.0 °C over 1971–2000; Csongrád sits at or above the ensemble central estimate.)
- **End-century AGST absolute level, RCP 8.5:** Projected to reach **~20.0–20.5 °C**, landing Csongrád in the **"hot" to "too hot"** Jones-Hall classification — a class in which **no European fine-wine region historically produces high-quality table wine** without irrigation and / or deliberate heat-adapted varieties.
- **Winkler GDD:** Projected shift from Region III–IV baseline into **Region V ("too hot")** by 2071–2100 under RCP 8.5. [ALFÖLD-AGGREGATE extrapolation].
- **Huglin Index:** Projected shift from baseline ~2,200–2,400 ("temperate-warm / warm") into the **"very warm"** class (>3,000) under RCP 8.5.
- **Drought footprint:** Heavy drought fractional area on the Great Hungarian Plain projected to rise from 6% (2001–2020) to **17% (2081–2100)** under RCP 8.5 — a ~3× increase, with Csongrád-Csanád at the core.
- **Variety suitability:** Lakatos & Nagy (2025) conclude that under RCP 8.5, Hungary-wide suitability for **lower-heat-requirement varieties** falls **~29% by end-century**; under RCP 4.5 only ~3–4%. Because Csongrád already sits at the upper heat boundary, **its lower-heat-requirement suitability loss is disproportionately large** — effectively meaning **Olaszrizling, Rajnai rizling, Pinot Noir and even Kékfrankos all face baseline-suitability failure in Csongrád by 2071–2100** on the pessimistic track.
- **Paradoxically expanded area under RCP 4.5 (near term):** The same Lakatos & Nagy work notes that in the near-term (2021–2050) under moderate scenarios, Csongrád is **among regions projected to see viable viticulture expansion** as other lower-heat Hungarian zones struggle. This near-term "window" reflects the still-compatible Huglin/GDD bracket for hot-climate varieties before it closes again under end-century extremes.
- **Net:** Csongrád under RCP 8.5 → **progressive existential risk** for the classical varietal portfolio; under RCP 4.5 → **transformation, not disappearance**.

## 12. Adaptation — variety substitution toward Mediterranean; the existential question

**[OBSERVED + PROJECTED]**

Adaptation options in Csongrád are **more constrained than in any other Hungarian wine district**, because elevation, aspect, soil and infrastructure levers are all unavailable. The practical portfolio reduces to **variety substitution**, **canopy/architecture management**, and **water-retention landscape works**.

- **1. Variety substitution — the Mediterranean pivot.** Lakatos & Nagy (2025) explicitly list Csongrád as a district where **Grenache, Syrah, Tempranillo and other Mediterranean-tolerant varieties** become cultivable in the mid-to-late 21st century. This is presented as an **opportunity** in the paper, but it is also a **forced transition** — the classical varietal identity (Kadarka, Kékfrankos, Olaszrizling) becomes sub-viable on the pessimistic track. Open questions include:
  - Grenache and Tempranillo are **not natively drought-tolerant** despite being Mediterranean; grower experience suggests Cinsault, Carignan, Bobal and Assyrtiko may be better matches for Csongrád's rain-fed sandy extremes.
  - Aroma and flavour development of Mediterranean varieties under Hungarian radiation/photoperiod is **unvalidated**.
  - PDO rules would need formal amendment to admit these varieties — a years-long bureaucratic process.
- **2. Rootstock and own-rooted heritage.** Sand's phylloxera immunity permits **own-rooted** plantings, which some drought-adaptation research frames as beneficial for long-term drought resistance (deeper root architecture). Csongrád is one of the few European districts that could **market own-rooted heritage Kadarka** — a plausible niche premium play if the variety can survive the heat.
- **3. Canopy and training adaptation.** Higher trellises, shoot-positioned VSP converted to **partial shade canopies**, double-curtain systems and eastern-facing training to reduce afternoon radiation are all being trialled in Hungarian research stations; Csongrád is the district where these matter most urgently.
- **4. Cover crops and landscape hydrology.** Re-wetting Homokhátság subsoil via wetland restoration, swales, contour ripping, organic-matter buildup and the 2025 **thermal-spa wastewater reuse** experiments could raise plant-available soil water at the plot level.
- **5. Irrigation.** Technically needed, practically blocked by water rights, groundwater decline and economics. Without national-scale water-infrastructure investment, **irrigation is not a general-purpose Csongrád adaptation**.
- **6. Controlled retreat.** The honest scenario no one writes down in industry documents: **progressive vineyard abandonment** of the driest Homokhátság cores and **contraction back to the Tisza-alluvial Csongrád-town zone**, which has slightly better soil moisture. This is already effectively happening — planted area has fallen from ~2,500 ha to ~1,500–2,000 ha over the past two decades.

**The existential question, stated plainly:** Csongrád is the one Hungarian wine district where the peer-reviewed literature forces a direct question of **whether the PDO as historically constituted remains agronomically meaningful** under RCP 8.5. The answer on the optimistic track is "yes, but transformed"; on the pessimistic track, the answer is "only with a substantially re-written varietal dossier, and with area contraction".

## 13. Economic & market dimension

**[OBSERVED]**

- **PDO status:** "Csongrádi" OEM — a formally protected Hungarian wine designation, registered in the EU **eAmbrosia** geographical-indications register. Despite this, it is among the **least marketed** Hungarian PDOs — no national retail brand prominence, almost no export visibility.
- **Producer structure:** **Dominantly smallholder**, family-run, many cellars do not bottle. Most grapes move into Kunság and Szekszárd co-operatives or bulk channels. There is no Csongrád "star winery" with national cellar-door traffic, unlike Villány (Csányi, Gere, Bock), Szekszárd (Takler, Heimann, Sebestyén, Vesztergombi, Mészáros Pál), or Eger (St Andrea, Kovács Nimród, Thummerer).
- **Wine tourism:** Minimal. The region is bypassed by the main Hungarian wine-tourism routes, despite being close to Szeged (a substantial tourist city) and within 1 hour of the Serbian and Romanian borders. Some effort by regional bodies (DKMT — Danube-Körös-Maros-Tisza Euroregion Wine) to build cross-border wine tourism.
- **Market positioning:** Low price point, local-market volume wine. Csongrád's commercial identity has always been "cheap, sound, Alföld table wine" — exactly the segment most exposed to **import substitution** (Spanish, Italian bulk) and to **per-capita wine-consumption decline** in Hungary.
- **Climate-economic vulnerability:** Because margins are thinnest in Csongrád, **any climate-driven yield loss immediately threatens grower viability** — there is no premium-price buffer to absorb the loss. The 2022 and 2024 shocks drove several reported exits from vine-growing in the district.
- **Structural risk:** Csongrád sits at the intersection of **high climate exposure** and **low adaptive capacity** — the technical textbook case of a high-vulnerability agricultural region. No Hungarian viticultural literature disputes this framing.

## 14. Knowledge gaps

1. **No district-specific phenology time series.** All Csongrád phenology statements are extrapolated from Alföld-aggregate or Szeged-station data. A dedicated budburst/flowering/veraison/harvest dataset for ≥3 Csongrád reference vineyards would materially improve climate-impact attribution.
2. **No Csongrád-specific yield statistics.** Hungarian wine statistics lump Csongrád into Duna region totals. Separating Csongrád yields 1990–2025 would allow direct quantification of drought-year losses (2003, 2012, 2022, 2024) at district level.
3. **No disaggregated Lakatos & Nagy (2025) table values.** The headline figure "Csongrád >2.5 °C AGST rise under RCP 8.5" is quoted prominently but the paper does not publish the full Csongrád-specific baseline and end-century numbers in a region-by-region table for AGST / GDD / Huglin / BEDD. Obtaining the supplementary dataset from the authors is a priority.
4. **No published soil-water dataset** for Csongrád vineyards. The critical plant-available water capacity numbers are extrapolated from generic Homokhátság soil surveys, not from viticultural trial plots.
5. **No formal climate-suitability assessment of Mediterranean varieties in Csongrád conditions.** Lakatos & Nagy name Grenache, Syrah and Tempranillo but do not model their performance under Csongrád-specific radiation, photoperiod, winter-freeze risk and sand-water dynamics.
6. **Spring-frost risk under warming.** Whether advancing budburst out-runs last-frost date on the Csongrád flats is not district-specifically quantified; this is the main phenology-risk uncertainty.
7. **Flavescence dorée vector monitoring.** No published *Scaphoideus titanus* surveillance data for the Csongrád-Szeged corridor, despite its status as an obvious entry gateway from the Balkans.
8. **Irrigation economics.** No published economic feasibility study for drip irrigation in Csongrád conditions (groundwater availability, cost, net revenue break-even point).
9. **Wine-chemistry time series.** No longitudinal analytical dataset (alcohol, TA, pH, anthocyanins) for Csongrád wines 1990–2025 — the sensory/chemistry drift is currently described qualitatively, not quantified.
10. **Grower survey on adaptation intent.** Hungarian adaptation research is strongest in Eger, Tokaj, Villány; Csongrád growers' perceptions, barriers and planned actions are effectively undocumented.
