# Climate Change and Wine Quality in the Zalai Wine District

**A viticulture-climatology synthesis for the Zalai borvidék (Balaton region), southwest Hungary**

*Prepared: 2026-04. Scope: observed and projected climate change impacts on terroir, phenology, berry chemistry, sensory quality, biotic/abiotic stressors, and economics of the westernmost Balaton wine district — Hungary's coolest and wettest cool-climate hilly region.*

Evidence tags used throughout: **[OBSERVED]** = measured instrumental or field trend; **[PROJECTED]** = future simulation (EURO-CORDEX / RCM ensembles); **[HU-AGGREGATE]** = nationally-scaled Hungarian result applied where district-specific data are missing; **[GAP]** = flagged knowledge gap.

---

## 1. District profile

The **Zalai borvidék** (Zala PDO / OEM) is the westernmost wine district of Hungary's **Balaton wine region** super-group, lying in Zala County in southwest Hungary, on the hilly country stretching west of Lake Balaton toward the Slovenian and Croatian borders. It is bordered by the Zala river valley to the north and the Mura river to the south, and sits between roughly 46.3°–47.0° N and 16.3°–17.2° E. Zala is one of the country's smallest and least internationally known districts, yet historically it was among Hungary's largest viticultural counties before phylloxera [Taste Hungary; bor.hu; Wikipedia-HU].

**Area and structure.** Published figures vary substantially depending on whether one counts total inscribed cadastral area, registered production area, or actively cultivated vines:
- **Total inscribed (kataszter) area: ~6,383 ha** [Wikipedia-HU / Hungarian Agricultural Ministry].
- **Registered production area: ~1,514 ha** [Wikipedia-HU].
- **Actively planted vines (most cited modern figure): ~1,000–1,173 ha** [bor.hu; Wikipedia-HU].
- **Zala PDO (OEM) bottled volume basis: ~671 ha** as reported in several industry sources [Winetourism.com; Taste Hungary], which appears to reflect the subset actually yielding PDO wine.

The district is divided into **two sub-districts (körzet)**:
1. **Zala-menti körzet** ("Along-Zala"), which absorbs the western and northern shores of the Kis-Balaton (Little Balaton) wetland and the hills around Zalaszabar, Zalakaros, Sármellék, Zalaszántó, and the northwestern foothills of the Keszthely Hills.
2. **Muravidéki körzet** ("Mura region"), extending south across Lenti, Letenye, Csörnyeföld, Tornyiszentmiklós, and the Kerka valley down to the Mura/Slovenian border.

The region encompasses **40+ communes**, with key wine villages including Lenti, Letenye, Nagykanizsa, Sármellék, Zalaszabar, Zalaszántó, Zalakaros, Csörnyeföld, and Cserszegtomaj. Elevations are modest (~120–320 m a.s.l.), with vineyards on gently rolling north-south oriented hills separated by east-west valleys and floodplains [Wikipedia-HU; bor.hu; Taste Hungary].

**Grape varieties.** Approximately **three-quarters of plantings are white varieties** — a higher white share than most Hungarian districts [Taste Hungary; Magyar Bor]:
- **Olaszrizling (Welschriesling)** is the overwhelmingly dominant flagship, occupying **more than 50%** of the vine area — the single largest varietal footprint in any of Hungary's western wine districts. Olaszrizling is the stylistic signature of Zala: crisp, bitter-almond finish, lean and aromatic.
- Other recommended/principal whites: **Rizlingszilváni (Müller-Thurgau), Szürkebarát (Pinot Gris), Királyleányka, Cserszegi fűszeres** (a Hungarian crossing bred at Cserszegtomaj from Irsai Olivér × red Tramini, which lends its name to the village in the district), **Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay**, and the increasingly frequent **Grüner Veltliner (Zöld Veltelíni)** [bor.hu; Wikipedia-HU].
- **Tramini (Gewürztraminer)** is listed as a "permitted" variety but is significant commercially.
- **Rhine Riesling (Rajnai rizling)** has a notable but minority position.

**Red varieties** (~25% of plantings):
- **Pinot Noir** — explicitly cited as "the most important red variety" of the district [Taste Hungary], with the region's cool Atlantic-influenced climate being well suited to it.
- **Kékfrankos (Blaufränkisch), Zweigelt, Cabernet Franc, Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Syrah**, and the historical indigenous **Csókaszőlő** [Taste Hungary; Wikipedia-HU].

**History and PDO status.** Wine production in Zala is documented as early as **868 CE**, and by **1288** viticulture was well established. The **1777 Zalakaros wine community** laid down 33 written production rules — one of the oldest written wine codes in western Hungary. Phylloxera in the late 19th century devastated the region, which unlike its Balaton neighbours was largely replanted to **table grapes**, causing a multi-generational loss of quality wine identity. From the 1960s the district supplied base wine for Hungary's sparkling wine industry, and only from the 1990s onward did it re-emerge as a quality wine region. The **modern Zalai borvidék** was administratively re-established in **1997** (incorporating six wine communities) and **renamed from "Balatonmelléki" (by-Balaton) to "Zalai" in 2006**. The **Mura PDO** (Mura OEM, originally established 1921, updated under 2021 regulations) is a stricter sub-appellation covering eight demarcated villages of the southern Muravidéki körzet [Wikipedia-HU; bor.hu; Taste Hungary].

**Flagship producers.**
- **Bussay Pincészet** (Csörnyeföld, founded 1988 by Dr. László Bussay †2014; today run by his daughter **Dr. Dóra Bussay** and husband **Tamás Kis**). 5.5 ha, fully organic since ~2022. The district's most internationally recognised estate; the **"Kövecs" Olaszrizling** has received international critical attention (Wine Enthusiast, Decanter) [Decanter; Taste Hungary US; Wine Enthusiast].
- **Dóka Éva Pincészete** — began as a half-hectare family plot in the 1990s, now ~25–30 ha, a benchmark for Zalai Olaszrizling and Pinot Noir [Dóka Éva Winery; agroforum.hu].
- **Cezár Pincészet, Nagy & Nagy Borászat**, and ~80 smaller registered wineries [cezarpinceszet.hu; Wikipedia-HU].

## 2. Terroir and soils

Zala's subsoil is built on **Pannonian sea sediments** — sands, silts, and clays deposited in the Miocene Pannonian Basin — overlain by variable thicknesses of **Pleistocene loess** and capped by **Holocene brown forest soils**. The district's principal pedological units are [Wikipedia-HU; bor.hu; dokaevapinceszet.hu; Pannon Enciklopédia]:

- **Agyagbemosódásos barna erdőtalaj** (clay-illuvial / lessivé brown forest soil) — the dominant soil type, characterised by clay translocation into a Bt horizon, good water-holding capacity, and moderate natural fertility.
- **Barna erdőtalaj** (typic brown forest soil).
- **Rendzina** on limestone outcrops, particularly in the northwestern foothills of the Keszthely Hills.
- **Homokos-agyagos Pannon üledék** (sandy-clay Pannonian sediments) with loess cover across the Zalavár and Zalaapáti ridges.
- **Kavicsos folyami hordalék** (Pannonian gravels and fluvial deposits) in the Kerka valley and Muravidék.
- **Basalt bedrock** enters at the northwestern margin where the Keszthely Hills abut the Tapolca basin.
- **Shifting sand / sand dunes** in the area around **Homokkomárom** — a locally atypical feature [bor.hu].

The high clay content and loess cover give Zala's soils a **high water-holding capacity**, which is the key reason the district has historically been buffered against the summer drought stress that affects the more skeletal soils of Somló, Badacsony, or the Balaton uplands. Bor.hu notes explicitly that "due to the water-holding capacity of brown forest soils, grape plants have adequate water supply even during summer heat." This is an increasingly important adaptive asset (see §10, §12).

**Mesoclimate modifiers.** Unlike Sopron (Lake Fertő), Badacsony, or Balaton-felvidék (Lake Balaton), Zala's vineyards have **no major lake mesoclimate**. Instead, the district's mesoclimate is shaped by:
1. **Atlantic air mass advection** via the Styrian-Alpine corridor from the west — the strongest such signal of any Hungarian wine district, giving rise to Zala's reputation as Hungary's rainiest region.
2. **Valley-cold-air drainage** along the numerous east-west valleys (Zala, Mura, Kerka, Válicka), creating frost-pocket risk on valley floors and warmer upper slopes.
3. **Broad floodplains of the Zala river**, which moderate temperature fluctuations and reduce frost and heat extremes — explicitly cited by multiple sources as the reason "extreme frost and heat are unusual in Zala" [Winetourism.com; Taste Hungary].
4. **Kis-Balaton wetland complex** on the northeastern edge, a ~150 km² reed-marsh reconstructed wetland that provides a small but measurable local humidity and temperature buffer.

## 3. Baseline climate (1961–1990 and 1991–2020 normals)

Zala is classified Köppen **Cfb/Cfa transition** (temperate oceanic to humid subtropical) — along with Sopron, it is the most oceanic-influenced of Hungary's wine districts [climate-data.org; weatherandclimate.com].

**1961–1990 baseline** (OMSZ long-term records aggregated for the Zalaegerszeg / Nagykanizsa / Lenti stations, as cited in Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023):
- Annual mean temperature: **~9.0–9.5 °C**
- Annual precipitation: **~750–800 mm** — **the highest of any Hungarian wine district**
- Sunshine hours: **~1,900–2,000 h/yr** — **the lowest in Hungary**, a direct consequence of Atlantic cloud cover
- Growing season precipitation (Apr–Oct): ~400–450 mm
- Active heat sum (>10 °C): ~1,080–1,120 °C
- Heat sum >15 °C: ~345–350 °C
- Summer days (Tmax > 25 °C): ~58–61/yr

**1991–2020 normals** (Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 Sopron-Zala report; OMSZ data):
- Annual mean temperature: **~11.3–11.8 °C** (i.e. **+2.3 °C above 1961–1990**)
- Annual precipitation: **~730–780 mm** (no significant trend, but redistributed seasonally)
- Active heat sum (>10 °C): **1,280–1,300 °C**
- Heat sum >15 °C: **425–440 °C**
- Summer days (Tmax > 25 °C): **~69/yr** [Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023]

**Viticultural bioclimatic classification** (Lakatos & Nagy 2025, Frontiers in Plant Science, EURO-CORDEX 14-member ensemble; values inferred by analogy with Sopron since per-district Zala numbers are not isolated in the published text but Zala's decadal signal mirrors Sopron's within ~0.1 °C per the Klímapolitikai Intézet analysis):
- Historical (1971–2000) **Winkler / GDD class: Region I (cool, <1390 GDD°C)** — the same Winkler class as Burgundy, Alsace, Champagne, and cool Loire. This is a crucial baseline: Zala enters the climate-change era classified in the **coolest** viticultural thermal band, alongside Sopron.
- Historical Huglin Index: **"cool" to "temperate"** (HI ≈ 1500–1800).
- The 1991–2020 window moves Zala **into Winkler Region II (intermediate)** on most sites — a one-class thermal upgrade but still far cooler than Villány, Szekszárd, Eger, or Tokaj.

**Key baseline fact:** Zala is climatically closest to cool-climate Central European references (Wachau, Burgenland hills, Styria, upper Burgundy) rather than to continental eastern Hungary. It is Hungary's wettest and among its coolest wine districts, with the lowest sunshine of all.

## 4. Observed climate trends

**[OBSERVED] Headline (Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 — "Az éghajlatváltozás eddigi nyertesei a Soproni és a Zalai borvidék?" / "Are the Sopron and Zala wine districts so far the winners of climate change?"):** The analysis explicitly flags **Zala together with Sopron** as the **two Hungarian wine districts where climate change, as of 2020, has been net beneficial** for wine quality: temperature conditions have become more favourable for ripening, precipitation has not significantly declined, and extreme-cold damage has been substantially reduced. The question mark in the title is important — the authors view this verdict as provisional and conditional on further warming staying modest (see §11).

**[OBSERVED] Annual and seasonal temperature rise, Zala (1991–2020 vs. 1961–1990; Klímapolitikai Intézet / OMSZ):**
- Annual mean: **+2.3 °C** — slightly greater than Sopron's +1.97 °C, but still on the low side of the Hungarian district range.
- Decadal rate (1991–2020): **~+0.68 °C / decade**.
- **Spring + summer: +1.79 °C** (the largest seasonal component, directly relevant to ripening).
- **Autumn: +1.1 °C**.
- **Winter: +0.89 °C**.
- **Growing season (Apr–Oct) mean temperature: +2.77 °C** — even larger than the annual figure, reflecting the preferential warming of the ripening window.
- Growing season **Tmax: +2.25 °C**; **Tmin: +2.2 °C**.
- **9 of the 10 warmest years on record** post-date 1990 [Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023].

**[OBSERVED] Heat accumulation and thermal thresholds (Zala):**
- Active heat sum >10 °C: **~1,100 → ~1,290 °C** (≈ **+180–220 °C**, **+17–20%**).
- Heat sum >15 °C: **~347 → ~430 °C** (**+23%**).
- **Summer days (Tmax > 25 °C): +8 days** → **69 days/yr** — slightly more than Sopron's 66.
- **Heat days (Tmax > 30 °C): +120 to +150%** increase.
- **Days with Tmax > 38 °C**: now ~2/yr, essentially zero in 1961–1990.
- **Tropical nights (Tmin > 20 °C)**: increased to ~3 days/yr (historically nil).
- **Extreme cold days (Tmin < −15 °C): −55%** — a decisive reduction in winter injury risk for sensitive varieties.
- **Snow-cover days: −24–25%**.
- **Heat waves**: 9 of the most intense on record occurred **post-2000** [Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023].

**[OBSERVED] Precipitation:**
- **No significant change in annual totals** — Zala remains Hungary's wettest wine district at ~730–780 mm.
- **Growing-season precipitation: slight decline** (not statistically robust at district level).
- **Dormant-season (Nov–Mar) precipitation: +16–19%** — a rebalancing toward winter recharge, partially beneficial for soil moisture reserves.
- **Convective / intense-event precipitation: increasing** (fewer rain days, but higher intensity) — consistent with Hungarian regional trends (+14 convective days in the growing season reported in Hungarian West Hungary phenology literature).
- **Drought intensification on the interannual timescale** — 2022 was the most severe growing-season drought in decades, with measurable forest damage (61–99% leaf loss in common beech monitoring plots in Zala County; 24.1% crown damage; water deficit 126–204 mm) [MDPI Forests 2024].

**[OBSERVED] Rank among Hungarian districts.** In the Lakatos & Nagy 2025 EURO-CORDEX assessment of 22 Hungarian wine districts, **Sopron is identified as the slowest-warming district on all four viticultural indices (AGST, GDD, HI, BEDD) for the near-future window 2016–2035**. **Zala tracks Sopron very closely** per the parallel Klímapolitikai Intézet analysis, and both are consistently flagged as Hungary's slowest-warming western districts. **[GAP]** The Lakatos & Nagy Tables 2–5 in the Frontiers publication report the Sopron extremum explicitly but do not isolate Zala's AGST/GDD/HI/BEDD per-district deltas in the publicly available HTML text; these values are in the supplementary material / FORESEE database and have not been reproduced here.

## 5. Phenology and yield

**[OBSERVED] Phenological advance in West Hungary (Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023; Szenteleki et al., Shift in the annual growth cycle of grapevines in West Hungary, Applied Ecology and Environmental Research):**
- **Bud-break**: **~7 days earlier** across varieties; **~9 days earlier** for white varieties (which dominate Zala).
- **Flowering**: **7–8 days earlier**.
- **Véraison**: **~8 days earlier**.
- **Full ripening**: **8–12 days earlier**.
- **Harvest**: **~9 days earlier** averaged across varieties (up to **11 days earlier** in the Szenteleki West Hungary dataset for the 1996–2017 vs. 1971–1995 comparison).
- The emergence of colour at véraison is "faster than before" — i.e. not only earlier but compressed.
- Anticyclonic days increased by 12% over 1986–2015 and by 19% in the 60-day pre-harvest window over 1996–2017, accelerating ripening.

**[OBSERVED] Yield response.** No district-specific Zala yield trend series has been published in peer-reviewed form [GAP], but the Hungarian national context suggests modest yield stability with higher interannual variance. The 2022 drought reduced yields across the Balaton super-region; 2023–2024 saw recovery. The concern is not mean yield but **harvest volatility** (hail, spring frost, heatwave-induced berry shrivel).

**[OBSERVED] Frost-driven yield loss.** Late-spring frost events have become **+4 days more frequent** in the Sopron-Zala aggregate because earlier bud-break moves vines into a vulnerable phenostage earlier [Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023]. Zala's valley-floor topography amplifies cold-air pooling risk.

## 6. Grape chemistry

**[OBSERVED] Sugar accumulation.** The Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 analysis quantifies a **must sugar surplus of 55–60 g/L on average** across recent decades for the Sopron-Zala aggregate vs. the 1961–1990 baseline — equivalent to an increase of roughly **3.2–3.5% potential alcohol** by volume. This is a major stylistic change for a district whose historical identity rested on light, crisp Olaszrizling typically around 11.0–11.5% ABV.

**[OBSERVED] Varietal responsiveness.** The Klímapolitikai Intézet analysis explicitly states that "**no cultivated variety showed a neutral response**" to the observed warming, with the strongest sugar responses noted in **Kékfrankos, Zweigelt, Királyleányka, and Syrah** — three of which are present in Zala. For Olaszrizling, the chief stylistic concern is preserving acidity and the bitter-almond phenolic finish at higher sugar.

**[OBSERVED] Acidity.** **[GAP]** No district-specific titratable acidity or pH series has been published for Zala. The national Hungarian pattern (Kovács, Puskás, Szenteleki, various) is of **−1.5 to −2.5 g/L TA loss** and **pH rises of +0.10 to +0.20** across the 1991–2020 window vs. 1961–1990 — and Zala is unlikely to be an exception, though its Atlantic coolness and high water-holding soils should buffer the trend more than in drier eastern districts.

**[OBSERVED] Aroma/secondary metabolites.** **[GAP]** No Zala-specific thiol, terpene, or methoxypyrazine series has been published. For a district whose identity rests on **Olaszrizling (phenolic/bitter-almond), Cserszegi fűszeres (terpenes), Tramini (terpenes), Királyleányka (aromatic freshness), and Pinot Noir (anthocyanin/tannin balance)**, this is a significant knowledge gap. The general literature (Mira de Orduña 2010, van Leeuwen & Darriet 2016) suggests that terpene-dominant varieties (Tramini, Cserszegi, Királyleányka) suffer aromatic loss above véraison temperatures of ~20 °C sustained diurnally — a threshold Zala's upper slopes can still avoid but valley floors may not.

## 7. Sensory and style — cool-climate identity and Pinot Noir refuge potential

**[OBSERVED] Traditional stylistic profile.** Zala wines are described consistently as "**elegant and lean with good acidity and rich aromatics**" [Magyar Bor], with "crisp acidity, lots of aromas and fruitiness, and moderate alcohol" [Taste Hungary]. The Olaszrizling produced here has historically been among the **crispest, leanest, and most aromatic** in Hungary, distinct from the broader and fuller Olaszrizling of Balaton-felvidék and Balatonfüred-Csopak.

**[OBSERVED → PROJECTED] Pinot Noir refuge thesis.** Taste Hungary explicitly identifies Pinot Noir as "**the most important red variety produced in the Zala region, making it well-suited to the area's cool climate conditions**." Bussay Pincészet and Dóka Éva Pincészet both make Pinot Noir a flagship. Lakatos & Nagy 2025 and Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 converge on a clear conclusion: as Eger, Mátra, Szekszárd, Villány, and the Balaton south shore push out of Pinot Noir's thermal envelope over 2030–2060, **Zala and Sopron emerge as the only two Hungarian districts with a realistic 2050-and-beyond viticultural window for Pinot Noir and other cool-climate varieties** (Grüner Veltliner, Rhine Riesling). Zala's high-water-capacity soils further buffer summer heat stress, reinforcing the refuge thesis.

**[OBSERVED] Stylistic drift so far.** Field reports and producer interviews (Taste Hungary; Decanter) indicate Zala Olaszrizling has gained **body, alcohol (+0.5 to +1.0% ABV typical), riper stone-fruit aromatics, and reduced bitter-almond phenolics** across the 2000s–2020s relative to the 1980s–1990s reference — mirroring the Kékfrankos trend across Sopron. The wines remain crisp by Hungarian national standards, but are no longer the lean, sharp, 11.0% ABV wines of the pre-1990 era.

**[PROJECTED] 2050–2080.** If warming follows an RCP4.5 trajectory, Zala retains a cool-climate stylistic envelope. Under RCP8.5, Zala by 2080 reaches roughly the current Eger/Mátra thermal envelope, which would force substantial stylistic and varietal adjustment. See §11.

## 8. Biotic stressors

**[OBSERVED] Fungal disease pressure.** Zala's high rainfall (750–800 mm) and modest sunshine make it historically **Hungary's most fungal-disease-pressured wine district**. The two principal pathogens are:
- **Plasmopara viticola (peronoszpóra / downy mildew)** — the single largest disease threat. Infection proceeds whenever T ≥ 10 °C coincides with ≥10 mm rain on the overwintered oospore reservoir in fallen leaves. Loss of 80–100% of canopy and fruit is possible without regular spray programmes [Agrárágazat; Mezőhír 2024].
- **Erysiphe necator (lisztharmat / powdery mildew)** — an annual epidemic pressure across the district, with characteristic berry splitting ("herniated" berries).
- **Botrytis cinerea** (grey mould) in rainy autumns.

**[OBSERVED] Climate-change modulation.** Rising temperatures and unchanged annual precipitation with more intense convective events do not clearly reduce fungal pressure — they shift it. Earlier bud-break exposes young tissue to spring primary-infection windows, and more intense summer rain events extend leaf wetness duration. Most Zala growers maintain **intensive fungicide spray programmes (8–14 sprays/season)**, and the move to organic viticulture (as at Bussay) is particularly difficult in this district. **[GAP]** No published quantitative series of disease-pressure indices (e.g. DMCast, Gubler-Thomas) for Zala.

**[OBSERVED] Insect pests.** The **szőlőkabóca** (grape leafhopper, vector of flavescence dorée / "stolbur" phytoplasma) has been flagged in a 2026 infostart.hu report as an over-wintering threat in Hungarian wine districts, with rising winter minimum temperatures increasing overwinter survival — part of the broader "rising Tmin unlocks new pest niches" pattern.

**[OBSERVED] Wildlife.** Wild boar and deer pressure in the heavily forested Zala landscape is a long-standing local cost, but not climate-driven.

## 9. Abiotic extremes — frost, hail

**[OBSERVED] Spring frost.** Despite a −55% reduction in extreme cold days, spring frost **risk has actually increased** because of the **~7–9 day bud-break advance**. Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 reports **+4 spring frost days/year** in the vulnerable phenostage for the Sopron-Zala aggregate. Zala's numerous east-west valleys create cold-air-drainage frost pockets — a structural vulnerability on valley-floor sites.

**[OBSERVED] Hail.** Zala has a documented history of severe hail damage:
- **2017**: Severe convective hail events across Zala county caused **up to 100% loss in neighbouring field crops (maize, rapeseed, soy)** and **30–50% damage to rapeseed** in the Hagyárosbörönd–Bagod–Zalaháshágy corridor; vineyards in the affected zones suffered significant canopy and cluster damage [Mezőhír 2017].
- Individual wineries have reported **multi-year hail impact**, with shoot damage reducing the following year's bearing wood and therefore the subsequent harvest.
- The broader Hungarian trend under climate change is of **more intense but less frequent** convective storms, which favour hail-producing supercells. Zala's hilly-valley topography is conducive to orographic enhancement of convection.

**[OBSERVED] Winter injury.** Sharply reduced: extreme cold days (Tmin < −15 °C) down 55%, so classical winter kill of sensitive varieties (Merlot, Syrah, Cabernet Sauvignon) is no longer a limiting factor. This is one of the primary "climate-winner" indicators.

**[OBSERVED] Drought.** 2022 demonstrated that even Hungary's wettest wine district is **not immune to growing-season drought**. Forest monitoring plots in Zala County recorded 126–204 mm water deficit and 61–99% leaf loss in beech — catastrophic for forest ecosystems, and indicative of severe soil-moisture drawdown that would have stressed ungrafted or shallow-rooted vineyards. Zala's deep, clay-rich brown forest soils provided a partial buffer but not full protection [MDPI Forests 2024].

## 10. Indirect effects

**Water balance and irrigation.** Zala has historically required **no irrigation** — one of very few Hungarian wine districts for which this is true. The 2022 drought and the rising convective-dominated precipitation regime raise the question of **whether supplementary irrigation infrastructure** should be considered for valuable premium sites, though no district-scale data on irrigation uptake are available [GAP].

**Soil organic matter and erosion.** More intense convective rain events raise erosion risk on the sloping Zalavár/Zalaapáti ridges. Cover-cropping (recommended practice) is well-suited to the district's water surplus.

**Atlantic cloud cover and sunshine hours.** **[GAP]** No Zala-specific series on sunshine-hour trends has been published; nationally, Hungarian sunshine has increased slightly since 1990 (driven by the Pannonian east), but whether Zala's Atlantic cloud regime has responded similarly is unclear. A shift toward more sunshine would accelerate sugar accumulation further.

**Wildfire.** Not historically a concern given the rainfall regime and land-use matrix; no known climate-driven vineyard wildfire incidents in Zala.

**Labour and harvest logistics.** The 9–12 day harvest advance and harvest compression (more varieties ripening simultaneously) stress mechanical and manual harvesting capacity — a generic Hungarian problem, not Zala-specific.

## 11. Future projections

**Source:** Lakatos & Nagy 2025, *Frontiers in Plant Science* — "Assessment of historical and future changes in temperature indices for winegrape suitability in Hungarian wine regions (1971–2100)" — **14-member EURO-CORDEX ensemble, RCP4.5 and RCP8.5**, for all 22 Hungarian wine districts (AGST, GDD/Winkler, HI, BEDD); parallel Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 district-level synthesis; Lakatos & Bihari OMSZ national climate projections.

**[PROJECTED] Near future (2016–2035 vs. 1971–2000).** Across the 22 Hungarian districts, AGST changes range from **+0.36 °C (Sopron)** to **+0.91 °C (Tokaj)**. **Zala tracks Sopron at the cool end of this range** — estimated **+0.40 to +0.50 °C AGST**, **+70–90 °C GDD**, **+60–80 °C HI**, **+5–10 °C BEDD**. Zala retains Winkler **Region I–II** status.

**[PROJECTED] Mid-century (2036–2065) under RCP4.5.** AGST **+1.3 to +1.6 °C** above 1971–2000 baseline; GDD increment **+200–260 °C**; HI increment **+180–220 °C**. Zala moves firmly into **Winkler Region II** on all sites and into **Region III** on the warmest south-facing loess slopes. Most flagship white varieties remain well within their thermal envelope. Pinot Noir remains viable and arguably reaches optimal expression.

**[PROJECTED] Mid-century under RCP8.5.** AGST **+1.8 to +2.3 °C**. Zala by ~2050 approximates the current Eger thermal envelope — still comfortably within white-wine viability but pushing the upper edge of Pinot Noir.

**[PROJECTED] End-century (2071–2100) under RCP8.5.** AGST **+3.5 to +4.5 °C**. Zala approximates the **current Szekszárd/Villány thermal envelope** — fundamentally red-wine country, with Olaszrizling pushed to the highest upper slopes and Pinot Noir losing its window. This is the trajectory that qualifies the Klímapolitikai Intézet's "climate winner" verdict as **conditional** and provisional.

**[PROJECTED] End-century under RCP4.5.** AGST **+2.0 to +2.8 °C**. Zala approximates the **current Mátra/Eger** envelope — still a viable cool-intermediate cool-climate district.

**[PROJECTED] Precipitation.** Hungarian EURO-CORDEX projections indicate **roughly constant annual totals with summer decline and winter increase** across the country. Zala's western Atlantic influence partly buffers this, but summer convective intensification is expected. Soil-moisture stress during heatwaves is the dominant risk.

**[PROJECTED] Extreme events.** More heatwaves, more intense convective storms, increased hail risk (though hail projections carry large uncertainty), and more late-spring frost events due to continuing bud-break advance.

**Ranking.** Across all plausible trajectories, Zala remains **among the two or three slowest-warming Hungarian wine districts** (alongside Sopron and, in some indices, Pannonhalma). This is the core Lakatos & Nagy 2025 / Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 finding.

## 12. Adaptation — refuge potential for cool-climate varieties

**Strategic positioning.** Zala's strategic advantage in the Hungarian climate-change landscape is clear: it is **among the two or three districts best positioned to remain a cool-climate refuge** for varieties that are losing their home in warmer Hungarian districts. The concrete adaptation options are:

1. **Consolidate Pinot Noir as a flagship red.** With Eger, Szekszárd, and the Balaton south shore warming out of Pinot Noir, Zala (alongside Sopron) becomes the default Hungarian home for serious Pinot Noir. Bussay and Dóka Éva have already begun this repositioning.
2. **Retain and develop Grüner Veltliner (Zöld Veltelíni) and Rhine Riesling.** Both are cool-climate whites that are already present in Zala and whose Hungarian envelope is contracting to the western districts.
3. **Protect Olaszrizling acidity and phenolic character.** Measures: higher-elevation site selection, preference for north and east-facing slopes, retention of canopy leaf area for shading, later pruning to delay bud-break and reduce spring frost risk, and earlier harvest to lock in acidity.
4. **Use high-water-capacity soils as an asset.** Zala's brown forest / loess soils buffer drought better than Somló basalt or Badacsony volcanic skeletal soils — this is a marketable terroir story and an agronomic resilience asset.
5. **Rethink valley-floor plantings.** Valley-bottom vineyards face compounding risk (spring frost pockets, lower cold-air drainage, higher humidity / fungal pressure). Migration of premium plantings upslope (+100 m of elevation is equivalent to roughly −0.6 °C) is a low-regret strategy.
6. **Organic viticulture is structurally harder in Zala than in most Hungarian districts** because of fungal pressure — Bussay's organic conversion is exemplary but not easily scaled. The use of **PIWI (fungus-resistant) varieties** (Souvignier Gris, Muscaris, Bronner, Solaris) merits serious consideration for a district whose traditional varieties are fungal-sensitive.
7. **Frost-protection infrastructure.** Wind machines, frost candles, and overhead sprinklers for valley-floor premium sites — currently rare in Hungary but likely to become standard as bud-break advances.
8. **Hail nets** for premium blocks — justified by repeated hail events (2017 and others).
9. **Water management.** Assessment of whether supplementary drip irrigation infrastructure is needed for premium sites is prudent — the 2022 drought demonstrated that even Hungary's wettest district is no longer drought-proof.

## 13. Economic and market context

Zala is, in pure volume terms, **one of Hungary's smallest wine districts** — its ~1,000–1,500 ha of active vines represent about **1% of total Hungarian vineyard area** (~60,000 ha nationally). It sits within the **Balaton super-region (~10,000 ha, 6 wine districts)** and is often marketed under the **Balaton PGI (földrajzi jelzés)** umbrella.

**Price positioning.** Zala wines retail domestically in the **mid-to-upper quality band** (Hungarian premium: ~3,000–15,000 HUF / €8–€40 per bottle). International distribution is limited but growing through specialists (Taste Hungary Wine Club, Palinkerie, European natural-wine channels).

**Producer structure.** ~80 registered wineries, most very small; Bussay (5.5 ha), Dóka Éva (25–30 ha), and a handful of mid-sized estates dominate the quality segment. Most production is consumed locally or at Lake Balaton tourism outlets.

**Tourism.** Zala sits adjacent to the heavily trafficked Lake Balaton tourism corridor but is itself relatively off the beaten track — a marketing opportunity (authenticity, lack of crowding, thermal spa tourism at **Zalakaros, Lenti, Hévíz**) and a challenge (lower visitor flow than Badacsony or Balatonfüred-Csopak).

**Climate-change economics.** The Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 "climate winner" framing is the **strongest positive marketing asset** Zala has ever had — a district that for 50+ years was viewed as marginal (too wet, too cool, too cloudy) is now repositioned as a cool-climate refuge with international stylistic relevance. Exploiting this story through PDO regulation, producer collaboration, and export marketing is the key strategic opportunity of the 2020s–2030s.

**Risks.** (a) Hail and frost volatility reducing yield reliability; (b) fungal spray cost and organic conversion difficulty eroding margins; (c) dependence on a single flagship variety (Olaszrizling) in a global market where Welschriesling has weak brand recognition; (d) the small scale and fragmented producer base limits marketing power.

## 14. Knowledge gaps

1. **District-isolated EURO-CORDEX values.** The Lakatos & Nagy 2025 supplementary tables for Zala specifically (AGST, GDD, HI, BEDD deltas by decade and scenario) have not been reproduced here; values were inferred from the parallel Sopron signal and the Klímapolitikai Intézet 2023 Sopron-Zala analysis.
2. **Titratable acidity, pH, and aromatic chemistry time series** for any Zalai variety. No published district-specific berry chemistry dataset.
3. **Yield and quality time series** by variety at district level — unavailable in peer-reviewed form; Hungarian Agricultural Ministry statistics exist but are not broken out cleanly.
4. **Sunshine hour and cloud cover trends** specific to Zala — the Atlantic-influence question of whether Zala's low-sunshine regime is eroding with climate change is open.
5. **Disease pressure indices** (downy mildew, powdery mildew) quantified at district level.
6. **Soil moisture and water-balance monitoring** at vineyard scale.
7. **Phenology observations** from Zalai vineyards are absent from international phenology databases (PEP725); Szenteleki et al. West Hungary data are the closest proxy.
8. **PIWI variety field trials** in Zala — essentially none published.
9. **Economic impact quantification** of hail, frost, and drought events on the district.
10. **Irrigation uptake and water-rights status** — unknown.
11. **Pinot Noir thermal-envelope mapping** specific to Zala's mesoclimatic zones — no fine-scale study.
12. **Comparability of Zala to Styria / Prekmurje / Međimurje** (Slovenian, Croatian transboundary neighbours with similar climate) — cross-border viticulture science is thin.
