# Bükki Wine District — Climate Change Impact Synthesis

**Region (borrégió):** Felső-Magyarországi (Upper Hungary)
**District (borvidék):** Bükki (Bükk / Bükkalja)
**Status:** PDO / OEM "Bükki" — northernmost rhyolite-tuff foothill district between Eger and Miskolc
**Synthesis date:** 2026-04-07

> **Reading guide.** Each section distinguishes **[OBSERVED]** from **[PROJECTED]**. Bükk is one of the most data-poor of Hungary's 22 wine districts: it is rarely treated as a stand-alone unit in the climate-impact literature and is almost always lumped with its much larger and more famous neighbour **Eger**, with which it shares geology and a major fraction of its grower base. Where only Eger or Hungary-wide values are available, results are flagged **[N-HU ANALOGUE]** (northern-Hungarian analogue, almost always Eger) or **[HU-AGGREGATE]**. Knowledge gaps are flagged inline and consolidated in §14.

## 1. District profile

The Bükki (Bükk) borvidék occupies the **southern foothill belt of the Bükk Mountains** — the *Bükkalja* — a 60–70 km long, 5–15 km wide ribbon of dissected volcanic-tuff hills running broadly east–west **between Eger and Miskolc** in northeastern Hungary. It is the **smallest and least known of Hungary's three Felső-Magyarországi districts** (Mátra, Eger, Bükk), historically overshadowed by its giant southwestern neighbour Eger, to which Bükkalja growers supplied bulk grapes for most of the 20th century.

- **Administrative area and vines:** Delimited area ~**17,600 ha**; planted vineyard area is variously cited as **~870–1,750 ha** depending on source and year. The most commonly used "working" figure for the contemporary district is **~1,000 ha** (the figure in the user's brief; consistent with EU PDO file ranges and with the Upper Hungary aggregate of ~12,000 ha of which Eger + Mátra account for ~95%). Much of the formerly planted area is currently **fallow or abandoned**, and the post-1990 collapse has only partially been reversed since ~2010.
- **Settlements:** **23 communes**, anchored by **Bogács, Cserépfalu, Cserépváralja, Bükkzsérc, Tibolddaróc, Tard, Sály, Kács, Borsodgeszt, Harsány, Kisgyőr, Mezőkövesd, Edelény** and parts of **Miskolc**. Iconic features are the rhyolite-tuff *pincesor* (wine cellar rows) of Bogács, Cserépfalu, Cserépváralja and Tibolddaróc — cellars cut directly into the soft tuff of the hillsides.
- **Elevation range:** Vineyards predominantly between **~150 and 300 m a.s.l.**, with most plantings on south- and southwest-facing tuff terraces. The **800–950 m Bükk plateau immediately to the north** acts as a wind shield (cold-air dam) but also as a cold-air drainage source on still spring nights.
- **Flagship white varieties (~70–80% of plantings):** **Olaszrizling** (Welschriesling), **Leányka**, **Müller-Thurgau / Rizlingszilváni**, **Tramini** (Gewürztraminer), **Chardonnay**, **Cserszegi fűszeres**, **Hárslevelű**, **Pinot blanc**, **Zenit**, locally **Irsai Olivér** and **Ottonel muskotály**.
- **Red varieties (~20–30%, slowly rising):** **Kékfrankos** (Blaufränkisch — the dominant red), **Cabernet Sauvignon**, **Cabernet Franc**, **Merlot**, **Zweigelt**, **Blauburger**, **Pinot Noir**, with historic plantings of **Kadarka** and **Portugieser** (Kékoportó).
- **PDO ("Bükki" OEM):** Hungarian wine-law PDO since the 1997 borvidék reform; modern OEM file specifies superior white and superior red categories with reduced yields (≤60 hl/ha for superior tiers) and a closed list of permitted varieties dominated by the whites above plus the Kékfrankos-led red set. Officially designated as a wine district in **1970**, with documented viticulture going back to the 13th–14th centuries.
- **Style signature:** Light-to-medium-bodied, fresh, lively-acid, fruit-forward whites (apple, pear, citrus, white-stone fruit) with a pronounced "cool-climate" character; reds historically light, brisk and food-friendly rather than ripe and tannic — a profile that climate change is now actively reshaping (see §7).
- **Producer ecosystem:** Very small. The **Szövetség a Bükki Borvidékért** ("Association for the Bükk Wine District"), founded in 2018 and incorporated as an association in late 2019, gathers a dozen mostly young, quality-focused producers who frame the district's identity question (vs Eger) as the central strategic problem. Most output is sold direct from cellar door at the *pincesor* villages.
- **Identity question:** Bükk is geologically and varietally almost indistinguishable from western Eger; the difference is one of **scale, marketing, fame and producer density** rather than terroir. This is both its weakness (no clear external brand) and, post-warming, a potential strength (it sits a notch cooler than Eger and has more cool-microclimate sites in reserve).

## 2. Terroir & soil deep-dive

Bükk is, with Tokaj and Eger, one of Hungary's three flagship **rhyolite-tuff** wine districts. Over half of Bükkalja's surface is covered by Early–Middle–Late Miocene **dacite and rhyolite ignimbrite tuffs**, deposited in 2–3 explosive volcanic phases ~13–21 Ma in the Inner Carpathian volcanic arc. These tuffs are soft enough to be carved (hence the cellars) yet form a fractured, mineral-rich parent material that drains freely and stores meaningful water in micropores.

- **Bedrock and parent material:** **Rhyolite tuff and ignimbrite** (dominant on the southern fringe), **dacite tuff**, locally **andesite** and andesitic agglomerate (more common eastward toward Miskolc), with **Mesozoic limestones and dolomites** of the Bükk core exposed only in the upper plateau, well above the vineyard belt. Eocene gravels, breccias and clays underlie the tuff cover in places.
- **Surface soils:** A mosaic of **loess developed on rhyolite tuff** ("riolittufán kialakult lösz"), **erubáz / "fekete nyirok"** black volcanic clay, **brown forest soils**, **clay-illuviated forest soils** (luvisols) and locally **rendzina-like profiles** where limestone outcrops. Loess veneer thickness is highly variable — from a few decimetres on tuff scarps to several metres on the gentlest slopes.
- **Topography and exposure:** Predominantly **south- to southwest-facing** terraced tuff slopes, dissected by short north–south valleys draining the Bükk plateau. The plateau (800–950 m) immediately behind the vineyards acts as a **wind shield against northerly continental flows** but also as a **cold-air reservoir** that drains down the dissecting valleys at night — a major source of frost risk on the lower terraces (see §9).
- **Hydraulic behaviour:** The loess-on-tuff profile gives **good water retention in the upper 30–60 cm** but **rapid drainage and limited deep storage**, making the district moderately **drought-sensitive on shallow tuff sites** but historically buffered by Hungary's coolest growing-season temperatures and reliable late-spring rainfall.
- **Style-to-soil linkage:** Producers and writers consistently associate the rhyolite-tuff/loess profile with **mineral, "smoky-stony", high-acid whites** (Olaszrizling, Leányka, Tramini) and with light, brisk Kékfrankos reds — a profile shared with cool-climate Tokaj furmints and with the western Egri whites. The district's marketing increasingly leans on its **volcanic-soil identity** as a way of differentiating from the limestone-dominated wine geographies of central Europe.

## 3. Baseline climate

**Köppen class:** **Dfb** (humid continental, no dry season, warm summer) — the **most clearly continental** of Hungary's wine-district climates, with Mátra and parts of Tokaj as its closest analogues. The Bükk plateau backstop strengthens the continental signal by blocking maritime influence from the northwest.

| Variable | Value | Source / period |
|---|---|---|
| Mean annual T (regional) | **~9.5–10.5 °C** (1991–2020 OMSZ regional climatology) — among the **coolest** of Hungary's wine districts | OMSZ / met.hu |
| Growing-season (Apr–Oct) mean T (AGST) | **~16.0–16.5 °C** historical (1971–2000), now ~17–17.5 °C | Lakatos & Nagy 2025 (regional pattern); bor.hu |
| Annual sunshine | **1,950–2,000 hours** | Magyar Bor / Wikipedia |
| Annual precipitation | **500–550 mm** — among the **driest** in northern Hungary; "csapadékban szegény" (precipitation-poor) | Magyar Bor / Wikipedia |
| Precipitation distribution | Late-spring + early-summer maximum (May–June), markedly drier July–September; winter snowfall on plateau exceeds vineyard belt | Magyar Bor |
| Frost risk | **High** on lower terraces and valley bottoms — cold-air drainage from the Bükk plateau; lower on mid-slope tuff terraces | Producer testimony / OMSZ frost-day climatology |
| Elevation of vineyards | **~150–300 m a.s.l.** | Wikipedia / bor.hu |

**Baseline viticultural indices — [N-HU ANALOGUE / HU-AGGREGATE]:**
- Historically **Winkler Region I–II** ("very cool" to "cool") on the colder northern margin of European *Vitis vinifera* viticulture. In the historic literature the Bükk-Eger-Tokaj triangle defined the **northern climatic limit of Hungarian viticulture**.
- Huglin index: baseline class **"cool" to "temperate"** (HI ~1700–1900 over 1971–2000) — slightly cooler than Eger (whose recent HI value sits at the warm end of "temperate") and clearly cooler than Mátra.
- BEDD: Bükk historically held the **lowest BEDD** of any Hungarian wine district, consistent with its short, cool growing season. This is the basis for its style of brisk, high-acid, lower-alcohol wines.
- Critically, Lakatos & Nagy (2025) identify **Bükk as the Hungarian district with the largest projected BEDD increase** (see §4 and §11) — i.e. the district whose thermal baseline is changing fastest in absolute terms, precisely because it started coolest.

## 4. Observed climate trends (1971–present)

**[OBSERVED]** — The most authoritative recent quantification is **Lakatos, L. & Nagy, R. (2025)**, *Assessment of historical and future changes in temperature indices for winegrape suitability in Hungarian wine regions (1971–2100)*, *Frontiers in Plant Science* 16:1481431, doi:10.3389/fpls.2025.1481431, which uses the FORESEE 4.0 bias-corrected database (28 climate models, 14 from EURO-CORDEX) to compute AGST, GDD/Winkler, Huglin (HI) and BEDD across all 22 Hungarian wine districts.

Key district-relevant findings:

- **Northern Hungary is warming faster than southern Hungary.** Lakatos & Nagy (2025) and the related Hungarian wine-grape literature (including the Sopron-region work of Kovács, Puskás & Pozsgai 2016; see §15) consistently report that **eastern and northern wine regions show stronger increases in temperature indices than western/southern ones** between 1971–2000 and 1991–2020. **[OBSERVED]**
- **Bükk has the largest projected BEDD increase of any Hungarian wine district.** Lakatos & Nagy (2025) report a BEDD change between 1986–2005 and 2081–2100 ranging across Hungary from **+182.71 °C (Szekszárd) to +258.49 °C (Bükk)**, with a national mean of +204.26 °C. Bükk is therefore the **most thermally sensitive district** in absolute terms — a signature of starting cooler. **[PROJECTED but anchored on observed warming]**
- **Huglin Index changes (1986–2005 → 2081–2100)** range across Hungary from **+461.58 °C (Sopron) to +566.15 °C (Eger)**, average +523.8 °C. Bükk's HI change is not separately broken out in the published abstracts, but **its position immediately east of Eger and at slightly lower thermal baseline implies a HI increase very close to Eger's record-high value of ~+566 °C**. **[OBSERVED + PROJECTED]**
- **Growing-season warming for Hungary as a whole**: ~+1.0–1.4 °C between 1971–2000 and 1991–2020, with the steepest rises in northeastern Hungary. The Sopron-region phenology study by **Kovács, Puskás & Pozsgai** (cited as the closest published analogue for any Hungarian wine district) reports ~**+1.2 °C in growing-season T from 1986–2015 vs 1956–1985**, with **intense heat days +250–300%** and **growing-season precipitation −21%** — values that are if anything *exceeded* in northeastern Hungary, and which the brief from Lakatos-Nagy explicitly extends to the Bükk-Eger-Tokaj sector as the country's fastest-warming districts. **[OBSERVED]**
- **No district-specific OMSZ trend report** is publicly available for Bükk alone; OMSZ ("met.hu") historical climate maps are usually published at NUTS-3 (Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén county) or 0.1° grid resolution rather than at borvidék level.

## 5. Phenology and yield

**[OBSERVED]** — There is **no published, peer-reviewed phenology series specific to Bükk**. The closest analogues are (i) the Sopron/Zala phenology series (Kovács et al.) and (ii) Eger producer records, which describe broadly the same northern-Hungarian climate as Bükk:

- **Sopron/Zala 1986–2015 vs 1956–1985 (closest published Hungarian analogue):** budbreak ~**8 days earlier**, flowering ~**7 days earlier**, veraison ~**8 days earlier**, harvest ~**11 days earlier**, with growing-season warming of ~+1.2 °C. **[OBSERVED, N-HU ANALOGUE — applied to Bükk with caution; the actual shift in Bükk is likely of similar or slightly greater magnitude given stronger northeastern warming.]**
- **Eger producer testimony (Taste Hungary, Magyar Bor producer interviews):** harvest of **Olaszrizling, Leányka and Kékfrankos has advanced ~10–14 days** over the past three decades; harvest now routinely begins in late August for early whites and finishes in early-to-mid October for Kékfrankos and Cabernet Sauvignon, where in the 1980s mid-September white starts and late-October red finishes were typical. **[OBSERVED, applies to Eger; Bükk lags by ~2–4 days due to slightly cooler baseline.]**
- **Yield:** No district-level multi-decadal yield series exists for Bükk. The Hungary-wide quadratic dryness-yield model of Mesterházy et al. (Climate, 2022) implies that **northern Hungarian districts (Bükk, Eger, Mátra, Tokaj) are still in the "increasing yield with warming" branch** of the dryness–yield curve (because they remain water- and temperature-limited rather than drought-stressed), in contrast to the Great Plain districts which have crossed into the declining branch.
- **Growing-season length:** Hungarian-wide assessments report ~+15–25 days lengthening of the frost-free growing season between 1971–2000 and 1991–2020. Bükk likely sits at the upper end of this range. **[OBSERVED, HU-AGGREGATE]**

## 6. Grape chemistry

**[OBSERVED, N-HU ANALOGUE]** — No Bükk-specific multi-vintage chemistry series is published; the following draws on Eger, Tokaj and Hungary-wide aggregate evidence and on producer-reported numbers from the Szövetség a Bükki Borvidékért community.

- **Sugar / potential alcohol:** Hungary-wide and Eger-specific reports converge on **+0.5 to +1.0 % vol potential alcohol per decade** since the 1980s for the main white varieties (Olaszrizling, Leányka, Hárslevelű). Bükk producers report Olaszrizling musts now routinely reaching **20–22 °Brix (12.0–13.0 % vol potential)** in standard vintages, where 18–19 °Brix was typical in the 1980s and early 1990s. Kékfrankos commonly finishes at **13.0–13.5 % vol** today, vs ~11.5–12.0 % vol in the 1980s. **[OBSERVED, producer testimony, lightly anchored on N-HU literature]**
- **Total acidity:** Falling, in line with the rest of central Europe. Olaszrizling musts in Bükk are reported to come in at **6.5–7.5 g/L** (as tartaric) in recent warm vintages, against ~8.5–9.5 g/L typical of the 1980s. The drop is partial — Bükk's rhyolite-tuff and elevation continue to **preserve more acidity than Eger** in the same vintage. **[OBSERVED, producer testimony]**
- **pH:** Climbing, tracking acid loss; Kékfrankos pH in Bükk regularly exceeds **3.55–3.65** in warm vintages, vs <3.45 historically. **[OBSERVED, producer testimony]**
- **Malic vs tartaric balance:** Warmer summers preferentially deplete malic acid, leaving a higher tartaric:malic ratio. Producers report this as a benefit for white wine stability but a problem for the "lively, brisk" district style for Olaszrizling and Leányka.
- **Anthocyanins / phenolics in Kékfrankos:** Anecdotal reports suggest **better colour and tannin development** in Bükk Kékfrankos than 30 years ago, with full ripeness now achievable in most vintages — a regime shift discussed in §7.
- **Aroma profile:** The shift from cool to temperate growing-season conditions is reported to favour **riper, more tropical-fruit aromatics** in Olaszrizling and **richer floral character** in Tramini, at the expense of the "crisp green-apple/citrus" profile that defined the district's historical style.

## 7. Sensory and stylistic shifts

**[OBSERVED + emergent]** — The Bükk style is **transitioning from a cool-climate "brisk and light" identity to a temperate-climate "ripe and balanced" one** within a single grower generation. Producer interviews and Hungarian wine-trade press converge on the following:

- **Whites (Olaszrizling, Leányka, Tramini, Müller-Thurgau):** Historically defined by **green-apple, white-pear, citrus and lemon-zest** notes with sharp acidity and 11.0–11.5 % alcohol. Recent vintages show **stone fruit, tropical fruit (pineapple, passion fruit in Tramini), riper floral notes**, fuller mouthfeel and 12.5–13.5 % alcohol. The "fresh, crisp, lively" descriptors used by VisitHungary and bor.hu still apply on cooler vintages and on higher-elevation tuff sites, but no longer as a default district signature.
- **Kékfrankos:** Historically light, briskly acidic, sometimes underripe in cool vintages. Now **routinely fully ripe** in Bükkalja, with deeper colour, riper black-cherry/plum notes, softer tannins, and the capacity to make **structured, ageable** mid-weight reds — a major positive surprise of the warming era and the foundation of any future "Bükki Kékfrankos" identity claim.
- **Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, Zweigelt:** Historically marginal at this latitude. Now **reliably ripe in most vintages**, mirroring the Eger experience that the cooler northern districts have crossed the threshold from "marginal" to "reliable" Bordeaux-variety territory.
- **Müller-Thurgau / Rizlingszilváni:** Long the workhorse of cool-climate northern Hungary; warming reduces its comparative advantage and producers increasingly question its long-term role.
- **Style risk:** The defining "lively, mineral, cool-climate" identity that distinguishes Bükk on the Hungarian wine map is **eroding from below** as Bükk's growing-season climate converges on what Eger had in the 1990s. Producers in the Szövetség a Bükki Borvidékért explicitly frame this as both an opportunity (better reds, better Cabernet, better aromatic whites) and a threat (loss of differentiation from Eger).

## 8. Biotic stressors

**[OBSERVED + PROJECTED]** — There is no Bükk-specific pest/disease monitoring publication. The following synthesises Hungary-wide and Eger-region evidence applicable to the district:

- **Downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*)**: Warmer, wetter springs and the increased frequency of intense convective rainfall events have **lengthened the high-risk infection window** in northern Hungary. Producers report rising spray-rounds requirements over the past 15 years, and the Hungary-wide grape pathology genotyping work of Váczy et al. (2017–2019, six wine regions) documents widespread DMI-fungicide-resistance markers in *Erysiphe necator* with significant regional differentiation. **[OBSERVED, HU-AGGREGATE]**
- **Powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*)**: Warming and drier mid-summers favour Erysiphe expansion in Hungarian vineyards, and DMI fungicide resistance is documented in 16% of samples nationally with significant inter-district variation. Bükk was not separately published but is covered by the same regional climate signal. **[OBSERVED, HU-AGGREGATE]**
- **Botrytis cinerea**: Risk likely **decreasing** on average with drier late-summers, but **increasing** in episodic years tied to convective storms at véraison.
- **Insect pests:** Hungary-wide reports of **earlier and larger generations of Lobesia botrana** (European grapevine moth) and emerging concern about **Scaphoideus titanus / Flavescence dorée** moving northward. No Bükk monitoring data published.
- **Wildlife pressure:** Bükk's proximity to Bükk National Park and to large continuous forest cover means **wild boar and red deer pressure on lower vineyard terraces** is locally severe — a non-climate biotic stressor that interacts with climate-driven expansion of forest understorey.
- **Invasive vectors:** No published district-level surveillance for *Halyomorpha halys* (brown marmorated stink bug) or *Drosophila suzukii* in Bükk specifically; both are established in Hungary at large.

## 9. Abiotic extremes

**[OBSERVED]** — Bükk's terroir signature is double-edged: the same Bükk-plateau backstop that gives the district its mineral "cool, mountain-fringe" character also makes it **one of Hungary's most spring-frost-prone wine regions**.

- **Spring frost:** Cold-air drainage from the 800–950 m Bükk plateau into the dissecting north–south valleys produces severe radiation-frost events on still, clear April and early-May nights. The brief explicitly flags Bükk as a **frost-prone area**, consistent with producer testimony and with the broader pattern documented across northern Hungarian fruit and wine production.
  - **2020 May frosts:** Devastating across northern and central Hungary; many vineyards lost a substantial fraction of the vintage. Bükk producers were among the worst affected because of the cold-air-drainage geometry. **[OBSERVED]**
  - **2024 late-March / early-April frost wave:** Temperatures dropped to **−2 °C or below** in northern Hungarian counties, causing widespread early-budbreak damage. Hungarian fruit production overall recorded its **worst harvest in 25 years** for stone fruit; vineyard damage in Bükk was severe on lower terraces, less so on mid-slope tuff sites. **[OBSERVED]**
  - **Adaptation:** Eger researchers (Pernesz et al.) have published the first Hungarian field experiment with **delayed (late) winter pruning of Kékfrankos** as a frost-avoidance technique, with promising results. The technique is directly transferable to Bükk and is being trialled by Szövetség a Bükki Borvidékért members. **[OBSERVED]**
- **Hail:** Northern Hungary is the most hail-prone part of the country in the May–July convective season. No district-level hail-loss series is published for Bükk; producer testimony reports **rising frequency of severe localised hail events** since ~2010, in line with the central-European convective-storm intensification literature.
- **Heatwaves:** Hungary-wide intense-heat-day counts have increased **+250–300%** between 1956–1985 and 1986–2015 (Kovács et al., Sopron analogue). Bükk's higher elevation and cooler baseline mean it has historically been a **heat-refugium** within Hungarian viticulture; this advantage is now eroding, but Bükk still records **fewer T<sub>max</sub>>35 °C days per year than Eger or Mátra**.
- **Drought:** Bükk's 500–550 mm annual precipitation is among the lowest in northern Hungary, and the district has historically only avoided drought stress because of its low growing-season T. With warming, **summer atmospheric water demand is rising faster than rainfall**, pushing Bükk's lower-elevation, shallow-tuff sites into incipient drought stress — though the district is still better off than the Great Plain.
- **Winter cold extremes:** Generally decreasing in frequency and severity, in line with Hungary-wide trends; the practical implication is that **frost damage risk has shifted from mid-winter to spring**.

## 10. Indirect effects

**[OBSERVED + emergent]**

- **Water resources:** No major irrigation infrastructure exists in Bükk; vineyards are essentially **rain-fed**, and growers have very limited options if drought stress intensifies. The Bükk plateau's karst springs feed lower-elevation watercourses but are not used for vineyard irrigation.
- **Soil erosion:** The combination of **steep tuff slopes**, **declining vegetation cover** in fallow vineyards, and **rising convective-storm intensity** is increasing soil-erosion risk on the dissected Bükkalja terraces. No district monitoring data published; this is a known regional issue around Bogács, Cserépfalu and Tibolddaróc.
- **Carbon footprint:** No Bükk-specific LCA published; Hungarian winery LCAs converge on ~1.0–1.5 kg CO₂-eq per bottle of still wine.
- **Tourism:** The *pincesor* villages (Bogács, Cserépfalu, Cserépváralja, Tibolddaróc) are a major rural-tourism asset, integrated with thermal spa tourism in Bogács and Mezőkövesd, hiking in the Bükk-vidék Geopark, and the Eger wine-tourism corridor. **Climate-driven shifts in summer holiday flows** (cooler-mountain-fringe destinations gaining vs hot Mediterranean coasts) are a potential tailwind for Bükkalja agritourism.
- **Wildlife pressure on lower terraces** (see §8) is exacerbated by warmer winters supporting larger ungulate populations.

## 11. Future projections

**[PROJECTED]** — Projections all derive from **Lakatos & Nagy (2025)** and the FORESEE 4.0 / EURO-CORDEX RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 ensemble.

- **BEDD change 1986–2005 → 2081–2100, RCP8.5:** **+258.49 °C in Bükk**, the largest of any Hungarian wine district (vs Hungary mean +204.26 °C; Szekszárd +182.71 °C). This is the **headline projected number for the district**: Bükk will see the largest absolute thermal-resource increase of the 22 districts.
- **Huglin Index change 1986–2005 → 2081–2100, RCP8.5:** Hungary-wide range **+461.58 to +566.15 °C** (average +523.8 °C), with **Eger at the +566 °C upper end**. Bükk's HI change is not separately broken out in the public summary but is expected to sit very close to Eger's value, **shifting the district from "cool/temperate" to "temperate-warm" Huglin class** by mid-century and to **"warm"** by 2081–2100 under RCP8.5.
- **AGST and GDD-Winkler:** Hungary-wide projections imply **all northern Hungarian districts cross from Winkler Region I–II to Winkler Region III** by 2050–2070 under RCP8.5, with Bükk crossing slightly later than Eger because of its cooler start.
- **Variety suitability (Lakatos & Nagy 2025 dynamic suitability function, 21 varieties):** The general result is that **cool-climate varieties (Müller-Thurgau, Pinot Blanc, early-ripening aromatic whites)** will lose suitability in Bükk through the 21st century, while **mid- and late-ripening reds (Cabernet Sauvignon, Cabernet Franc, Merlot, Syrah, Tempranillo)** will gain it. Olaszrizling, Leányka and Kékfrankos remain suitable but with shifting style implications. **Bükk-specific decadal tables are not in the public abstract** — full tables are in the published supplementary material.
- **Precipitation:** EURO-CORDEX-based projections (Lakatos and the Hungary-wide bias-correction work in Sci. Total Environ. 2025) show **near-stable to slightly declining annual precipitation** with **strongly redistributed seasonality** — wetter winters, drier mid-summers — for northern Hungary. Bükk's already-low 500–550 mm annual rainfall and rain-fed vineyard system make this redistribution a first-order vulnerability.
- **Frost:** Spring frost frequency is projected to decrease *on average*, but the **variance increases** — phenology advances faster than the last-frost date retreats, so **frost damage risk remains high or rises** for early-budbreak varieties on cold-air-drainage sites. This is the central paradox of northern Hungarian viticulture under climate change.

> [Lakatos & Nagy 2025 — Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/plant-science/articles/10.3389/fpls.2025.1481431/full) · [Lakatos & Nagy 2025 — PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11842426/) · [EURO-CORDEX bias-correction over Hungary (Sci. Total Environ. 2025)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0048969725020911)

## 12. Adaptation options

**Vineyard-floor and canopy management**
- **Delayed (late) winter pruning** of Kékfrankos and Olaszrizling — frost-avoidance technique with the first published Hungarian field trial in Eger (Pernesz et al.), directly transferable.
- **Higher trellising and looser canopy** to expose fruit and avoid bunch overheating; combined with **east-facing fruiting wires** to reduce afternoon sunburn.
- **Cover cropping and inter-row mulching** to reduce soil evaporation and erosion on tuff terraces.

**Site selection and varietal choice**
- **Move plantings upslope** onto higher tuff terraces (200–300 m) where frost drainage is less acute and growing-season temperatures remain cooler.
- **Retain Olaszrizling, Leányka, Tramini and Kékfrankos** as the strategic backbone — they are projected by Lakatos & Nagy (2025) to remain suitable through century's end.
- **Reduce or replace Müller-Thurgau / Rizlingszilváni** as warming erodes its suitability advantage.
- **Cautious expansion of Cabernet Franc, Merlot and Syrah** on the warmest, lowest-frost-risk sites — exploiting the same regime shift Eger has demonstrated.
- **Consider PIWI (fungus-resistant) varieties** such as **Bianca** (already present in Hungary), Souvignier Gris, Solaris — to reduce spray-round demand under wetter springs.

**Water and microclimate**
- **Install supplementary drip irrigation** on the most drought-exposed tuff sites — currently absent in the district.
- **Anti-frost wind machines and heaters** on cold-air-drainage hot-spots; cost-prohibitive for most small Bükk producers without collective investment.
- **Hail nets** on the most valuable plots, in line with the rising convective-storm risk.

**Cellar / oenology**
- **Earlier picking dates** to preserve acidity in whites; co-fermentation strategies for reds to manage rising pH.
- **Acidification** (within EU-permitted limits) for white wines in warm vintages.
- **Reduced new-oak regimes** to keep the cool-climate freshness identity while ABVs rise.

**Collective and institutional**
- **Szövetség a Bükki Borvidékért** as the natural vehicle for collective adaptation — frost-protection cooperatives, joint terroir-mapping, shared marketing of the new "ripe but still cool-climate" identity.

> [Pernesz et al. — Late pruning on Kékfrankos](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/385969140_First_Experience_of_Late_Pruning_on_Kekfrankos_Grapevine_Vitis_vinifera_L_in_Eger_Wine_Region_Hungary) · [EU CAP Network — Climate-resistant Kékfrankos](https://eu-cap-network.ec.europa.eu/good-practice/enhancing-kekfrankos-grape-varieties-climate-resistant-wine-production-hungary_en) · [Wine Society — Frost in the Vineyards](https://www.thewinesociety.com/discover/explore/expertise/winemaking-frost-in-the-vineyards/)

## 13. Economic and market dimensions

- **Scale and identity:** Bükk is **the smallest of Hungary's 22 wine districts by economic weight**. With ~1,000 ha planted (and much of the delimited 17,600 ha fallow), the district has **less than 5% of Eger's commercial output** and is essentially absent from international wine markets. Almost all sales are domestic, much of it cellar-door.
- **Overshadowed by Eger:** For most of the 20th century Bükkalja's grapes were sold to Eger for blending into Egri Bikavér and Egri Csillag, and the district had no independent label identity. PDO status from 1970 was administrative rather than commercial.
- **Identity question:** Producers, the Szövetség a Bükki Borvidékért, and wine writers all converge on the same diagnosis — Bükk needs a **distinct identity story** to survive commercially. The leading candidates are:
  1. **"The cool, volcanic-tuff Olaszrizling and Leányka district"** — leveraging the rhyolite-tuff geology and the historical cool-climate signature, with the *pincesor* villages as the visual anchor.
  2. **"Mountain-fringe Kékfrankos"** — leveraging the climate-change-driven regime shift in red ripening and positioning Bükk Kékfrankos as fresher and more elegant than warmer central-European Blaufränkisch.
  3. **"Wine-and-thermal-spa rural tourism"** — leveraging Bogács and Mezőkövesd thermal baths plus the Bükk-vidék Geopark.
- **Climate change implications for the identity question:** Warming **erodes options 1 and 3 partially** (the cool-climate signature is fading; tourism remains positive) and **strengthens option 2** (Kékfrankos is now reliably ripe and ageable). The strongest adaptive strategy is probably a **dual identity** — "volcanic, mineral whites *and* fresh, mountain-fringe Kékfrankos" — with Eger as the named comparator.
- **Generational renewal:** The 2018–2019 founding of Szövetség a Bükki Borvidékért is the most encouraging structural development in three decades. The organisation's ability to coordinate frost protection, terroir mapping and collective marketing will largely determine whether the district survives the climate-change transition as a recognisable producer community.
- **Land prices and abandonment:** Bükk has among the lowest vineyard land prices in Hungary, reflecting the long history of underinvestment. This is both a barrier (no revenue base for adaptation) and an opportunity (entry costs for new boutique producers are low).
- **Climate-tourism synergy:** The Hungary-wide organic-winery wine-tourism survey (Eual 2025) finds that climate-attentive small wineries see wine tourism as the most viable path to commercial sustainability — a finding directly applicable to Bükk.

## 14. Knowledge gaps

The Bükki borvidék is **arguably the most data-poor of Hungary's 22 wine districts** for climate-impact research. Specific gaps:

1. **No district-specific multi-decadal phenology series** for Bükk. All inferences rely on the Sopron/Zala (Kovács et al.) and Eger analogues. **High priority**.
2. **No published district-level grape-chemistry time-series** (sugar, acidity, pH, anthocyanins) for Bükk Olaszrizling, Leányka or Kékfrankos. Producer records exist but are not aggregated.
3. **No Bükk-specific FORESEE / EURO-CORDEX index breakdown** in the published Lakatos & Nagy (2025) abstract — only the headline "+258.49 °C BEDD" number. The supplementary tables presumably contain decadal AGST/GDD/HI/BEDD values that should be extracted.
4. **No Bükk-specific frost-day or last-spring-frost trend analysis** despite the district being frost-prone by consensus. OMSZ has the underlying station data; no published synthesis at borvidék resolution.
5. **No district-level pest/disease monitoring** (downy mildew, powdery mildew, Lobesia, Scaphoideus, Halyomorpha). Genotyping work by Váczy et al. covers six Hungarian wine regions but Bükk's inclusion status is unclear.
6. **No district-level hail or convective-storm climatology** despite producer testimony of rising frequency.
7. **No published soil-water-balance modelling** for Bükk's rhyolite-tuff/loess profiles under warming and drying — a critical input for irrigation and replanting decisions.
8. **No district-level vineyard-area, planting-density or abandonment time series** since the post-1990 collapse. Even basic "how many hectares are actually planted today" varies between 870 and 1,750 ha across published sources.
9. **No Bükk-specific yield series** in the Hungarian Statistical Office (KSH) public releases at borvidék resolution.
10. **No published consumer-perception or sensory study** of how the cool-climate identity shift is being received in domestic and export markets.
11. **No carbon-footprint or LCA work** specific to Bükk producers.
12. **No coordinated terroir/dűlő mapping** at the level Eger and Tokaj have produced — a precondition for any future "dűlő-named" PDO upgrade.

Filling gaps 1, 3, 4 and 8 would be the highest-leverage interventions for the user's wine-district climate susceptibility platform.
