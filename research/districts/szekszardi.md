# Climate Change and Wine Quality in the Szekszárdi Wine District (Szekszárdi borvidék), Hungary

*An exhaustive, citation-backed viticulture–climatology synthesis*
Pannon Wine Region, Tolna County, Southern Transdanubia
Compiled April 2026

---

## 1. District profile

The **Szekszárdi borvidék** is one of the six PDO wine districts of the **Pannon wine region** of southern Hungary, administratively seated in Szekszárd (Tolna County) and encompassing approximately **15 settlements** on the loess hills immediately south of the Mezőföld plain, bounded to the east by the Danube floodplain and to the south by the Sárköz. It is Hungary's southern flagship red-wine district, sharing climatic influences with the Villányi borvidék to the south and Pécs/Mecsek to the south-west.

**Vineyard area.** Published figures diverge depending on whether the total wine-district cadastre, the strictly-cultivated area, or the PDO-eligible area is cited. Frequently cited values are:

- Total production area of the wine district: ~**6,001 ha** (of which ~3,789 ha first-class).
- Planted vineyards: **~2,100–2,187 ha** (Taste Hungary cites 2,187 ha; other sources 2,116 ha).
- Szekszárd PDO area under vine: **~1,620 ha**.

**Flagship varieties** (dominated by red).

- **Kékfrankos (Blaufränkisch)** — the backbone of modern Szekszárd, replacing Kadarka during the 20th century because of its superior disease resistance, yield stability and ability to hold acidity into hot ripening seasons. Lead grape of Szekszárdi Bikavér.
- **Kadarka** — historic signature of Szekszárd; introduced by Serbian refugees in the 17th–18th centuries, became the dominant grape of 19th-century "Bull's Blood"-style blends. Thin-skinned, late-ripening, highly sensitive to powdery mildew, botrytis and rain at harvest. National area collapsed from **>60,000 ha** (pre-phylloxera / early 20th century) to **<400 ha in the early 1990s**; now undergoing revival, particularly at Heimann & Fiai, through clonal selection of smaller-berry, looser-cluster variants with thicker skin and slightly earlier, more homogenous ripening.
- **Szekszárdi Bikavér** ("Bull's Blood of Szekszárd") — a multi-variety Kékfrankos-led red PDO blend (composition detailed in §13).
- **Bordeaux reds** — Merlot, Cabernet Franc, Cabernet Sauvignon — widely planted since the 1990s; late-ripening, benefit in most years from Szekszárd's long warm Indian summer.
- **Minor reds**: Pinot Noir, Syrah, Zweigelt, Kékoportó (Portugieser), Blauburger, Turán, Bíborkadarka.
- **White varieties** (~15–20% of area): Chardonnay, Hárslevelű, Olaszrizling (Welschriesling), Tramini, Sauvignon Blanc — all secondary to the red-wine identity.

**PDO products.** Szekszárdi PDO (Oltalom alatt álló eredetmegjelölés) including varietal wines and the regulated **Szekszárdi Bikavér** and **Szekszárdi Bikavér Prémium** blends (full specification in §13).

**Notable producers** (non-exhaustive): Heimann & Fiai, Takler, Dúzsi Tamás, Vida Péter, Vesztergombi, Bodri, Mészáros, Tűzkő, Sebestyén, Lajvér, Eszterbauer, Alisca. Bodri alone farms ~100 ha.

## 2. Terroir & soil

Szekszárd sits on the eastern spur of the **Transdanubian Hills**, a chain of rolling loess uplands topping out around 280 m (Sötét-völgy ridge system). The most cited physiographic features are:

- **Deep quaternary loess (Pleistocene aeolian silt)**: the loess blanket on the Szekszárd Hills is "generally a couple of metres thick, but which can reach up to an incredible 30 m in some places" (Taste Hungary). This is among the thickest loess bodies in the Carpathian Basin.
- **Carbonate content**: the loess contains 5–7% active lime baseline, with local horizons up to **10–30% active CaCO₃** — appreciably higher than sandy, lime-poor soils and contributing to fuller, more complex wines (loose structure, good water-holding capacity, high base saturation).
- **Terra rossa enclaves**: Pannonian-basin iron-rich red clay subsoils appear beneath the loess at certain elevations (notably in the Porkoláb-völgy, Iván-völgy, Remete and Baranya-völgy sectors) and are considered the most prestigious premium red-wine terroirs.
- **Brown forest soils (ramann-type) and erosional loess-derived soils** dominate the rest of the slopes.
- **Slope & aspect**: the best vineyards are on **south- and south-east-facing slopes** (5–20% gradient) of the deeply incised erosional valleys; aspect-driven microclimate variation is strong — the many narrow north–south valleys (e.g. Porkoláb-völgy) create cool-corridor thermal inversions that in some sites delay ripening by several days.
- **Hydrology**: the loess is internally well-drained but unstable when wetted, so **terracing, turf strips and tarmacked hillside service roads (which double as stormwater conduits)** are long-standing erosion-control infrastructure.

The hills fall away to the Danube and Sió floodplain, exposing the most forward-sloped vineyards to warm easterly insolation and a strong diurnal signal from the river corridor.

## 3. Baseline climate

**Macroclimate classification.** Szekszárd lies in a **transitional temperate climate zone** with marked **sub-Mediterranean and Atlantic influences** — one of the warmest climates in Hungary. The Danube Valley and the relatively low latitude (~46.35° N) plus thermal protection by the surrounding hills produce one of Hungary's **longest growing seasons** and **infrequent spring/autumn frosts**.

**1961–1990 normals (OMSZ Szekszárd / Pécs reference stations, [HU-AGGREGATE] for southern Transdanubia where station-specific values are not published in English literature):**

- Annual mean temperature: **~10.5–10.8 °C**.
- Growing season (April–October) mean T: **~16.5–17.0 °C** — already in the "warm temperate" class by the Gladstones/Huglin scheme.
- Annual precipitation: **~600–640 mm**, unimodal with early-summer (May–June) maximum and a secondary autumn peak; growing-season precipitation ~380–420 mm.
- Annual sunshine: **~2,000–2,100 hours**.
- Winkler GDD (base 10 °C, Apr–Oct): **~1,500–1,600 °C·d** → **Winkler Region III (warm)**.
- Huglin index (base 10 °C, day-length corrected, Apr–Sep): **~2,000–2,150** → "Temperate warm" to "Warm" (HI+1/+2) — suitable for Merlot, Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah.
- AGST (Apr–Oct mean T): ~16.5 °C — upper end of "warm temperate" suitability for Kékfrankos and Kadarka.
- Mean number of summer days (Tmax ≥ 25 °C): ~80–90 d·yr⁻¹.
- Mean number of hot days (Tmax ≥ 30 °C): ~15–22 d·yr⁻¹.

**1991–2020 normals.** Hungary-wide, annual mean temperature has risen **~+1.1 °C** relative to 1961–1990 (OMSZ national series), with Southern Transdanubia tracking national warming. Szekszárd-area estimates for 1991–2020:

- Annual mean T: **~11.6–12.0 °C**.
- Growing-season mean T: **~17.5–18.2 °C**.
- Winkler GDD: **~1,650–1,800 °C·d**.
- Huglin index: **~2,200–2,400** (now clearly in HI+2 "warm", occasionally touching HI+3 "very warm").
- Hot days (≥30 °C): **~25–35 d·yr⁻¹** — approximately doubled vs. 1961–1990.
- Growing-season precipitation: broadly stable in mean but far more variable and increasingly concentrated in short extreme events.

Szekszárd is consistently classified in the comparative Hungarian literature as **one of the two warmest wine regions (alongside Villány)**. [HU-AGGREGATE] Station-specific published normals for the village of Szekszárd itself are sparse in international databases; Pécs-Pogány (~40 km SW) is the nearest WMO synoptic reference.

## 4. Observed climate trends (OMSZ-anchored)

[OBSERVED] **Temperature.** OMSZ homogenised national series (1901–2020; 33 long stations, 55 from 1951, 114 from 1975) show an unambiguous positive trend in annual and seasonal mean T across all Hungary since the 1980s, with Southern Transdanubia warming comparably to, or slightly more slowly than, the northern/north-eastern wine regions.

- National annual-mean T trend ~**+0.12–0.14 °C per decade** for 1901–2020, accelerating to ~**+0.4 °C per decade** since the mid-1980s.
- Summer (JJA) warming is the strongest seasonal signal; spring is also warming noticeably, contributing to earlier budbreak.
- Lakatos & Nagy (2025) and Lakatos et al. find that Hungarian wine regions have all crossed into warmer Winkler/Huglin classes during 1991–2020 relative to 1971–2000.

[OBSERVED] **Heat extremes.**

- OMSZ: hot days (Tmax ≥ 30 °C) and extremely hot days (≥35 °C) have both increased significantly since the 1980s. Heatwave frequency and duration have doubled over 1961–2020 nationally.
- The 2003, 2012, 2015, 2017, 2019, 2022 and 2024 summers delivered multiple heatwave episodes with daily maxima above 35 °C in Tolna County. **2024 was described by Hungarian wine press as a "very challenging vintage" across southern Hungary**: drought followed by extreme heat compressed ripening so strongly that "all the varieties got ripened at the same time" and pickers had to harvest everything together, with **crop ~10–20% below normal**.

[OBSERVED] **Precipitation.**

- National annual total precipitation has shown a modest negative trend (roughly −5 to −10% since the early 20th century), but with strong decadal variability.
- In **western and southern Transdanubia**, some sub-areas have lost up to **~15%** of annual precipitation relative to the mid-20th century.
- **Spring and autumn** precipitation has decreased; summer rainfall has become less frequent but more intense (shorter convective events).
- **Extreme precipitation events (EPE)**: OMSZ and SW-Hungary high-density monitoring networks (Pécs, Mecsek, Tolna hills) report significant upward trends in days with >20 mm and >30 mm since 1971–2020.
- **Dry spell length** (consecutive days with <1 mm) has increased.

[OBSERVED] **Drought.** Spatial–temporal drought analyses for 1961–2010 show the Transdanubia region (Central, Western, and Southern) with a **statistically significant deteriorating trend** (Mann–Kendall) in multiple standard drought indices (SPI, PDSI variants), indicating more frequent and longer agricultural-drought episodes — confirmed by the climate-vulnerability paper for Szekszárd (Pieczka et al., *Climate* 2021) which explicitly concludes that intensifying drought is the dominant exposure signal.

[OBSERVED] **Frost.** Late spring frost episodes remain climatologically infrequent in Szekszárd relative to inland Hungary due to the sub-Mediterranean influence and river corridor, but the advance of budbreak by ~1–2 weeks since the late 20th century increases the exposure window. The pan-European April 2021 frost event caused widespread damage elsewhere in Europe; Tolna escaped the worst but the advancing phenology is flagged as a rising risk.

## 5. Phenology & yield

[OBSERVED] Grapevine phenology in Hungary has advanced in line with the broader European signal:

- Budbreak: **~1–2 weeks earlier** since the 1980s (variety dependent).
- Flowering and veraison: each advanced by **~1–2 weeks**.
- Harvest: red varieties in Szekszárd are now routinely picked **2–3 weeks earlier** than in the 1970s–1980s — Kékfrankos harvests often commence in early–mid September rather than late September/early October, and Kadarka has shifted comparably.
- The **interval between veraison and harvest** has compressed in hot vintages, threatening aromatic precursor accumulation and berry balance.

[OBSERVED] **Yields.** Szekszárd-specific long-time-series yield data are not published in the English literature; district-level aggregates from the Hungarian wine office (HNT) show moderate decadal-scale variability with occasional severe downward shocks in drought years (2003, 2012, 2022, 2024). In 2024 the district crop was reported ~**10–20% below normal** due to drought and heat compression.

[PROJECTED] Under RCP8.5, southern Hungarian phenology is projected to shift an additional **1–3 weeks earlier** for harvest by 2071–2100, consistent with European-wide projections (1–2 weeks earlier under low-emissions, 3–4 weeks under high-emissions by end of century; ~10 days earlier budbreak under most scenarios).

**[GAP]** Publicly available, Szekszárd-specific phenological time series (budbreak / flowering / veraison / harvest) for individual varieties are limited — most Hungarian phenology work has focused on Eger and Tokaj. This is a clear local knowledge gap.

## 6. Grape chemistry and ripening for reds

[OBSERVED] The central viticulture–climatology question in Szekszárd is whether its red varieties can still deliver **balanced ripening** — sugar/tannin/anthocyanin in equilibrium — under an increasingly hot, solar-loaded growing season.

- **Sugar accumulation** is now regularly reaching levels that translate to **13.5–15+% potential alcohol** in Kékfrankos and Bordeaux reds in hot vintages, compared with ~12.5–13% historically. Hungarian producers in the hot-Hungarian belt (Villány, Szekszárd) repeatedly note that 2017, 2019, 2021, 2022, and 2024 delivered intense, high-alcohol wines.
- **Acidity retention** in Kékfrankos is one of its signature advantages over Kadarka and Merlot under warming. It is a natural late-ripening, acid-retaining variety — partly why it has displaced Kadarka. Nonetheless, nocturnal temperature rise (Tmin trends are stronger than Tmax in many OMSZ summer series) erodes malic acid retention and pushes pH upward.
- **Anthocyanin and polyphenol biosynthesis** is known to drop markedly when daytime canopy temperatures exceed ~**35 °C** and when nights remain above ~20 °C — both conditions now occur multiple times per summer in the Tolna loess belt. Growers have responded with higher canopy, partial leaf-retention on the west-facing cluster zone, and later-in-the-day harvest cessation.
- **Tannin ripeness vs. sugar ripeness decoupling** (the classic "warm climate red" problem): rising heat accelerates sugar accumulation faster than phenolic (tannin/anthocyanin) maturity, so winemakers face harvest-timing trade-offs — pick early for acidity and get under-ripe tannins, or wait for phenolic ripeness and get excess alcohol. This has explicitly driven canopy, rootstock and clonal choices in Szekszárd over the last ~15 years.

**Kadarka-specific chemistry challenges** [OBSERVED]:

- **Thin skin** → poor physical protection against UV and heat loading, elevated sunburn incidence, and extreme vulnerability to mildew (powdery and downy) and botrytis — especially problematic under the new regime of rain between heat peaks.
- **Low tannin / low anthocyanin baseline** ("Hungary's Pinot Noir") → the variety has little phenolic buffer against heat degradation; berry colour loss is observed in hot vintages.
- **Stomatal closure above 34–35 °C** (Heimann empirical observation): above this leaf-temperature threshold, Kadarka closes stomata defensively, halting photosynthesis and effectively pausing ripening — producing stalled, out-of-balance must.
- **Late, uneven ripening with loose clusters** — historically an asset in a cool regime, now increasingly a liability because heterogeneous berries mature under different thermal histories in the same cluster.

Heimann & Fiai's clonal programme (with the Pécs Research Institute, starting 2005 with **30+ Kadarka clones**, narrowed to ~7 favoured clones) explicitly selects for **smaller berries, looser bunches, and berry skins that thicken even in less sunny periods** to raise thermal and UV tolerance.

## 7. Sensory and style trajectories

[OBSERVED] The mainstream red style of Szekszárd in 2020–2025 is richer, more tannic, more oak-modulated and higher in alcohol than the pre-2000 norm. Industry commentary is explicit:

- **Kékfrankos**: the district still makes "spicy, sour-cherry, refreshing acidity" styles, but hot vintages push toward darker fruit, riper tannin and alcohol above 14%.
- **Cabernet Franc and Cabernet Sauvignon**: in hot years (2017, 2019, 2021, 2022, 2024) the wines are "pretty intense", with Villány the extreme and Szekszárd close behind.
- **Kadarka**: the surviving modern style is praised precisely for its "featherlight elegance", translucent ruby colour, cherry aromatics and gentle tannin — attributes that are **directly threatened** by warming. The clonal and canopy work at Heimann and a handful of other estates is explicitly framed as a rescue operation for this style under a warming climate.
- **Szekszárdi Bikavér**: the blend composition is effectively a climate-adaptive tool (§13). The minimum 45% Kékfrankos anchors acidity, the minimum 5% Kadarka preserves aromatic lift, and the Bordeaux component (each ≤40% individually) provides structural depth and can be adjusted year-to-year. In practice, hot vintages have increased the Bordeaux contribution; cooler vintages allow more Kadarka.

[OBSERVED] **Alcohol creep** is the single most commonly cited stylistic shift by Szekszárd producers. Many wineries have introduced **reverse osmosis, partial dealcoholisation, later pruning, higher canopies, leaf retention on west-facing rows, and earlier picking** specifically to manage it.

**[GAP]** No published peer-reviewed sensory panel study systematically tracks Szekszárd red-wine style drift over multi-decadal vintages, unlike analogous studies for Tokaj Furmint or Bordeaux Cabernet.

## 8. Biotic stressors

[OBSERVED] **Fungal diseases** remain the dominant biotic risk in Szekszárd. Hungarian nation-wide citizen-science studies of powdery mildew (*Erysiphe necator*), downy mildew (*Plasmopara viticola*) and grey mould (*Botrytis cinerea*) have documented that climate variability is a first-order driver of infection severity. For Szekszárd specifically:

- Kadarka's thin skin makes it the single most mildew- and botrytis-sensitive cultivar in the district (see §6).
- Heavy convective rainfall followed by high humidity between heat peaks — the emerging Szekszárd summer rainfall pattern — is close to worst-case for downy mildew and botrytis.
- The white variety **Bianca** (not major in Szekszárd but widely grown nationally) was originally highly resistant to both mildews; by the late 2000s a *P. viticola* isolate capable of overcoming its resistance had been reported in Hungary, illustrating that **pathogen evolution is already eroding earlier resistance baselines**.

[OBSERVED] **Invasive and emerging pests** — a regional watch-list rather than a confirmed Szekszárd outbreak list:

- ***Drosophila suzukii*** (spotted-wing drosophila): now established throughout Hungary since ~2012, oviposits in ripening thin-skinned red grapes and stone fruit. Kadarka is at particular risk.
- ***Scaphoideus titanus*** / **Flavescence dorée phytoplasma**: under active Hungarian phytosanitary surveillance; warming is expected to expand the vector's northward range.
- ***Lycorma delicatula*** (spotted lanternfly): not yet confirmed in Hungary (as of 2025) but an emerging European concern given its wide host range including *Vitis*.
- **European grapevine moth** (*Lobesia botrana*): generations per season are increasing with warmer summers, consistent with literature from southern Europe.

**[GAP]** Szekszárd-specific long-term trapping and disease-pressure time series are not published in the international literature.

## 9. Abiotic extremes

[OBSERVED]

- **Heatwaves**: multi-day Tmax ≥ 35 °C events are now near-annual; these cause stomatal closure, sunburn on cluster-zone berries (especially south-west aspects), photosynthetic shutdown and hydric stress on non-irrigated vineyards.
- **Drought**: flagged as *the* dominant exposure hazard for Szekszárd by Pieczka et al. (*Climate* 2021). Soil-water deficits increase steeply in the loess even though its field capacity is moderate-to-high, because deep loess can dry cumulatively over multi-year dry sequences.
- **Sunburn**: most frequent on Kadarka, Merlot, and to a lesser extent Cabernet Franc. Heimann's canopy work explicitly targets cluster-zone shading.
- **Frost**: climatologically infrequent in Szekszárd relative to the rest of Hungary; but earlier budbreak raises the exposure window, and the April 2021 European frost outbreak demonstrated that large swings are still possible in a warming mean climate.
- **Hail**: localised but damaging when it occurs — convective-storm hail climatology for SW Hungary is increasing in frequency under the shorter, more intense summer rainfall regime.
- **Wind erosion** on exposed loess slopes: a recognised but secondary concern.

[PROJECTED] All heat and drought indicators rise under both RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 through 2100. Extreme precipitation frequency also rises, but mean summer precipitation falls.

## 10. Indirect effects

- **Loess erosion**. Loess is "stable when dry, but subject to severe erosion when wet". A climate regime with longer dry spells punctuated by more intense convective storms is the textbook worst case for loess slopes. Szekszárd's terraces, vegetated inter-rows, and engineered drainage roads are all pre-existing defences that must be intensified under climate change. Without reinforcement, gully erosion in the narrow Szekszárd valley systems (Porkoláb-völgy, Remete, Iván-völgy) is expected to increase.
- **Irrigation pressure**. Historically non-irrigated, Szekszárd vineyards have been adding drip irrigation as a "rescue" infrastructure, particularly on shallow-soil ridges. Water-rights and groundwater availability are rising concerns, and Hungarian national water policy constrains vineyard irrigation more tightly than Southern European peers.
- **Atmospheric CO₂ rise** (~420 ppm now vs. ~340 ppm in the 1980 baseline) contributes to enhanced photosynthesis and water-use efficiency — an asset partially offsetting drought, but not resolving heat-stress pigment and aromatic losses.
- **UV**: summer UV load in central Europe has risen somewhat with declining aerosol pollution; direct viticultural consequences for pigment/tannin development are under-studied but likely net negative for thin-skinned cultivars such as Kadarka.
- **Labour and harvest logistics**: the compression of ripening windows (all varieties ready within days) identified in 2024 forces simultaneous picking, straining labour supply and cellar capacity and implicitly favouring larger, mechanically-equipped estates.
- **Cellar logistics**: higher must temperatures increase must cooling and fermentation-management demand; cellar-energy use rises.

## 11. Future projections

**Key study: Lakatos, L. & Nagy, R. (2025)**, "Assessment of historical and future changes in temperature indices for winegrape suitability in Hungarian wine regions (1971–2100)", *Frontiers in Plant Science*, doi:10.3389/fpls.2025.1481431.

- **Data**: FORESEE-HUN v1.0 historical (1971–2022; OMSZ HUCLIM interpolated to 0.1° × 0.1°) and FORESEE 4.2 projection database drawing 14 EURO-CORDEX regional climate models, bias-corrected, for **RCP4.5 and RCP8.5**.
- **Indices**: AGST (Apr–Oct mean T), GDD (Winkler, base 10 °C), Huglin Index (HI), Biologically Effective Degree Days (BEDD), and a "dynamic suitability function" for 21 cultivars evaluated in decadal increments 2031–2100.
- **Coverage**: all 22 Hungarian PDO wine regions including Szekszárd.

**Szekszárd-relevant findings** (aggregated from the paper and parallel Pieczka-et-al. southern-Hungary work — Szekszárd-specific values in the published literature remain sparse):

- Across Hungarian wine regions, **AGST changes** (late-century vs. late 20th-century baseline, RCP8.5) range **+2.44 °C (Sopron) to +2.96 °C (Tokaj)**, national mean **+2.71 °C**. Szekszárd sits in the southern-warmer cluster — the paper emphasises that **northern regions warm fastest and southern regions (Villány, Szekszárd) warm slightly less but start hotter**.
- **BEDD change** (near future 2016–2035 vs. 1986–2005) is **+182.71 °C for Szekszárd** — in fact the **lowest absolute BEDD increment** among the 22 districts, whose national mean is +204.26 °C. The low absolute increment reflects that Szekszárd is already near the upper bound of the BEDD operative range, i.e. additional heat contributes less to the bounded BEDD metric.
- By 2081–2100 under RCP8.5, all four indices place Szekszárd and Villány in a **"hot" to "very hot" macroclimate class** (HI clearly >3000 — equivalent to Mediterranean-Aegean values; Winkler Region IV–V). Cultivars historically aligned with Szekszárd's climate (Kékfrankos, Kadarka) move out of their thermal optimum; late-ripening Mediterranean cultivars (Syrah, Grenache, Aglianico, Tempranillo, Touriga Nacional) move into optimum. Hungarian commentary has framed this as Hungary becoming "a Mediterranean wine country".

**Climate Vulnerability and Adaptation Challenges in Szekszárd Wine Region** — Pieczka, Bartholy et al., *Climate* (MDPI) 9(2):25, 2021, https://doi.org/10.3390/cli9020025 — the first dedicated Szekszárd climate-vulnerability assessment:

- Two regional climate models used: **RegCM** and **ALADIN-Climate**, under high-emissions forcing.
- **Exposure**: Szekszárd's exposure value rises by **+1.61 (RegCM) and +2.03 (ALADIN-Climate)**, vs. national means **+1.39 and +1.57** — i.e. Szekszárd is **above the Hungarian average in exposure increment**, driven particularly by drought intensification.
- **Sensitivity**: high, because the dominant cultivars (especially Kadarka, Merlot) are heat- and drought-sensitive, and because ~2,100 ha on loess slopes have limited soil-water buffering.
- **Adaptive capacity**: moderate — some technical capacity (terracing, clonal selection, Heimann-style precision viticulture) but limited irrigation infrastructure and high fragmentation of smaller holdings.
- **Overall**: both external (exposure) and intrinsic (sensitivity) vulnerability components show **deteriorating trends**; explicit call for adaptation action.

**[PROJECTED] variety suitability shifts for Szekszárd by 2071–2100** (synthesised from the Lakatos & Nagy functional-suitability framework and regional context; Szekszárd-specific cultivar-by-cultivar outputs are aggregated rather than individually published):

- **Out of optimum**: Kadarka (earliest to leave safe operating space on heat and disease grounds), Pinot Noir, Merlot in hot vintages.
- **Near but declining**: Kékfrankos — retains viability thanks to late ripening, but acid retention and stylistic identity under threat.
- **Remaining / improving**: Cabernet Sauvignon, Cabernet Franc, Syrah, Tempranillo, Touriga Nacional, Aglianico — all become more climatically plausible for Szekszárd by 2050 and 2100.

## 12. Adaptation

**Already underway in Szekszárd (2020–2025):**

- **Clonal selection**. Heimann & Fiai's >30-clone Kadarka trial (with the Pécs Research Institute, since 2005) has narrowed to ~7 clones selected for smaller berries, looser clusters, and skins that thicken even under less sunny conditions — an explicit genetic adaptation strategy against heat and mildew. Kékfrankos clonal diversity work under the EU CAP Network project (Szekszárd, Villány, Hajós-Baja) is explicitly climate-resilience focused, identifying heat-tolerant, differently-ripening clones.
- **Rootstock choice**. Increasing use of drought-tolerant rootstocks (110R, 140Ru, 1103P) on drought-prone ridge sites; retention of Berlandieri×Riparia types on deeper loess.
- **Canopy management**. Higher trellising, VSP with retained leaf cover on the cluster zone (especially south-west aspects), later leaf pulling, partial defoliation timing shifted later to avoid sunburn windows.
- **Training systems**. Some experimentation with umbrella or sprawl canopies for Kadarka to provide cluster shading.
- **Pruning timing**. Late pruning to delay budbreak and reduce late-frost exposure; experimental on a minority of plots.
- **Irrigation**. Drip irrigation as emergency/selective infrastructure, not routine — consistent with Hungarian regulation.
- **Varietal diversification**. Planting of Mediterranean reds (Syrah, Tempranillo, Touriga Nacional in trial plots) alongside retention of flagship varieties. PDO rules limit how far this can go without compromising Szekszárdi Bikavér identity.
- **Soil management**. Cover crops, mulching, terrace maintenance, vegetated inter-rows to buffer loess erosion under intensifying convective rainfall.
- **Harvest-logistics**: earlier starts, night picking, cold-chain refrigerated transport of clusters to cellar.
- **Winemaking adaptations**: reverse osmosis partial dealcoholisation, earlier stabilisation, lower-extraction protocols for Kadarka, co-fermentation and blending to stabilise Bikavér style.

**Recommended additional adaptation under PROJECTED conditions:**

- Expand genetic-diversity banks and clone trials for Kadarka, Kékfrankos, Kékoportó to preserve regional identity under warming.
- Formal PDO flexibility to accept heat-tolerant minor varieties in Bikavér blends (already permitted up to 10% — potentially worth expansion).
- Landscape-scale drought-early-warning and irrigation-infrastructure planning.
- District-wide soil-carbon and cover-crop programmes for loess stabilisation.
- Expanded phenology / disease monitoring networks specific to Szekszárd.

## 13. Economic & market dimension

**Szekszárdi Bikavér PDO regulation** (current, as of 2025):

- Minimum **four varieties** in the blend.
- Minimum **45% Kékfrankos**.
- Minimum **5% Kadarka** (mandatory; Kadarka is the *identity* guarantor of Szekszárdi Bikavér, distinguishing it from Egri Bikavér).
- Cabernet Sauvignon, Cabernet Franc and Merlot individually each ≤ **40%**.
- Additional minor varieties ≤ **10% combined**: Blauburger, Kékoportó (Portugieser), Menoire, Pinot Noir, Syrah, Zweigelt, Bíborkadarka, Alibernet, Turán, Tannat, Sagrantino, Malbec, Mészikadar.
- **Minimum one year oak aging** (both tiers); wine cannot be released before 31 December of the year following harvest.
- **Alcohol**: minimum ~12.08% vol; natural sugar levels ranging 12.08–13.59% vol by component variety.
- **Yield**: ≤140 q/ha standard Bikavér; ≤80 q/ha **Prémium Bikavér**.
- Mandatory intermittent-operation pressing; sweetening prohibited; no dominance of "barrique" flavour; only bottled release (no bulk sales).
- Grapes must be vinified within the Szekszárd region.
- The figure of a bull may appear on the label.

**Market context.**

- Hungarian wine exports: **1.12 → 1.29 million hl from 2023 to 2024** (+15.5% volume), value **€113.5 → €133.8 million** (+17.9%). Bikavér is a key export identifier, though Egri Bikavér volume exceeds Szekszárdi in international listings.
- **Kadarka revival** is a key marketing narrative: leveraging the "indigenous grape" global trend, Heimann, Takler, Bodri, Dúzsi Tamás and Vida Péter among others position Kadarka as the *distinctive* Szekszárd proposition — higher-value, lower-volume, lifestyle-positioning — precisely the commercial answer to a climate-constrained production regime.
- **PDO brand** is a structural asset — but the Bikavér identity is under pressure from the same climate trends forcing variety reconsideration.

**[GAP]** Publicly-available Bikavér (Szekszárd vs Eger) export split and price-point time series are limited in the English-language literature.

## 14. Knowledge gaps

1. **Station-specific long homogenised climate series for Szekszárd itself** (rather than Pécs or southern-Transdanubia aggregates) are not readily available internationally.
2. **Szekszárd-specific phenology records** (budbreak → harvest dates) by variety, long-time-series — not published.
3. **Szekszárd-specific GDD, Huglin, BEDD projections cultivar-by-cultivar** — Lakatos & Nagy 2025 provides the framework, but the public paper does not tabulate all regions individually; an aggregate-to-specific gap remains.
4. **Empirical sensory-panel evidence** for stylistic drift in Szekszárd reds over the last ~30 years — anecdotal reporting only.
5. **Local disease-pressure monitoring data** (powdery, downy, botrytis, *D. suzukii*) on Kadarka and Kékfrankos specifically — citizen-science data is national, not Szekszárd-resolved.
6. **Quantified soil-water balance and drought-stress modelling** for Szekszárd loess-slope vineyards across clonal and rootstock combinations — not published.
7. **Irrigation water-budget and infrastructure assessment** at district level — not public.
8. **PDO-flexibility analyses** (e.g. how Bikavér composition can be legally evolved to preserve brand under climate pressure) — policy gap.
